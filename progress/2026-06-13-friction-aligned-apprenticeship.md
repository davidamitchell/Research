### Key synthesis insight

Employees systematically choose AI policy clarification tools over peer consultation because the four micro-transaction cost families of human consultation (discovery, access, social-status, and verification) are higher than the near-zero cost of an AI query. This rational cost-minimisation simultaneously removes the social friction through which contextual judgment and policy expertise are transmitted. The resolution is **stage-specific friction design**: make the authoritative path cheap to start (reduce discovery and access costs), but insert a mandatory independent-reasoning or challenge-before-accept step at the transfer stage — after the AI answer appears but before it is accepted — preserving the cognitive engagement where skill formation occurs without raising total cost above the bypass threshold.

Three tensions were identified; two were resolved by stage specificity and bounded augmentation logic; one (whether high-trust institutions can maintain voluntary peer escalation alongside widely available AI) remains open and is a candidate for future research.
