# Governance Policy Application: Deterministic Requirements vs Stochastic Large Language Model (LLM) Elements

## Research Question

To what extent must governance policy application be deterministic, consistent, reproducible, and auditable, versus allowing stochastic or probabilistic elements when Artificial Intelligence (AI) or Large Language Models (LLMs) are involved?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Regulatory and standards-based requirements for consistency, reproducibility, traceability, and auditability in policy enforcement
- Technical analysis of where non-determinism in Large Language Model (LLM) outputs conflicts with governance obligations
- Published frameworks, guidance, or case studies on acceptable tolerance for probabilistic outputs in regulated contexts
- Risk classification approaches for deciding which decisions require determinism

**Out of scope:**
- Pure machine-learning research on stochasticity beyond what is needed to explain governance and compliance implications
- Non-AI determinism requirements in traditional rule-based systems except as baseline comparators

**Constraints:** Prioritize sources that bridge regulatory requirements with AI or LLM system properties, and prefer official or standards-body material where available.

## Context

- Governance policy application is hardest to defend when materially similar cases can produce different outcomes without a reconstructable reason, because contestability, auditability, and executive accountability all depend on being able to explain what happened and who can change it. [inference; source: https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/rules-business-and-organisations/dealing-citizens/are-there-restrictions-use-automated-decision-making_en; https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/; https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/]
- Current hosted Large Language Model services remain only partially reproducible, because Microsoft documents residual variability even when a seed and backend fingerprint are held constant and engineering analysis shows that serving-path behavior still introduces nondeterminism. [fact; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output; https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/]
- Prior completed work in this repository already concluded that high-stakes governance is most defensible when deterministic policy enforcement, evidence logging, and meaningful human intervention sit outside raw model output, so this item focuses on how far that control pattern is required by external sources. [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-hybrid-architecture-probabilistic-llm-deterministic-governance.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-compliance-risks-stochastic-llm-governance-decisions.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-data-governance-standards-ai-agentic-applicability.html]

## Approach

1. Identify the determinism requirements in major governance frameworks, including General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) safeguards for automated decisions, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) GOVERN function, and International Organization for Standardization and International Electrotechnical Commission (ISO/IEC) 38505 and ISO/IEC 38507.
2. Review accessible official or technical sources on probabilistic Large Language Model behavior and reproducibility limits in deployed systems.
3. Examine how audit and compliance frameworks, especially the European Union (EU) AI Act, the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) guidance, and the NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework, handle outputs that are not fully reproducible.
4. Synthesize a decision framework for classifying which governance decisions require strict determinism and which can tolerate controlled stochasticity.

### §2 Investigation

#### A. Governance frameworks define accountability, monitoring, and contestability requirements

- [fact; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/] The NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework Core says the GOVERN function is cross-cutting and requires legal and regulatory requirements to be understood and documented, transparent policies and controls, ongoing monitoring and periodic review, clear roles and responsibilities, and executive leadership responsibility for Artificial Intelligence system risk decisions.
- [fact; source: https://www.iso.org/standard/56639.html] ISO/IEC 38505-1:2017 provides guiding principles for governing bodies on the effective, efficient, and acceptable use of data, including assuring stakeholders that they can have confidence in the organization's governance of data.
- [fact; source: https://www.iso.org/standard/56641.html] ISO/IEC 38507:2022 provides guidance for governing bodies to enable and govern the use of Artificial Intelligence and is applicable to current and future uses of Artificial Intelligence and their implications for the organization.
- [fact; source: https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/rules-business-and-organisations/dealing-citizens/are-there-restrictions-use-automated-decision-making_en] European Commission guidance says individuals should not be subject to solely automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effects unless narrow exceptions and suitable safeguards apply, including information about the logic involved, human intervention, consequences, and the right to contest the decision.
- [fact; source: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/] The Information Commissioner's Office states that when Article 22 of the United Kingdom (UK) General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) applies, organizations must give people information about the processing, provide simple ways to request human intervention or challenge a decision, and carry out regular checks to make sure systems are working as intended.
- [fact; source: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/] The same Information Commissioner's Office guidance says that mere human involvement in the lifecycle does not amount to meaningful human review, because in most cases review should come after the automated decision and must relate to the actual outcome.
- [fact; source: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12] Article 12 of the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act requires high-risk systems to automatically record events over the lifetime of the system and to enable traceability appropriate to the intended purpose.
- [fact; source: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14] Article 14 requires high-risk systems to support effective human oversight, including understanding capacities and limitations, detecting anomalies and unexpected performance, correctly interpreting outputs, disregarding or reversing outputs, and interrupting the system safely.
- [fact; source: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14] Article 14 also warns against the tendency to automatically rely or over-rely on system output, a failure pattern the Act names automation bias.
- [fact; source: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-15] Article 15 requires high-risk systems to achieve an appropriate level of accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity and to perform consistently in those respects throughout their lifecycle, while being resilient to errors, faults, and inconsistencies.
- [fact; source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11023-018-9482-5] Floridi et al. define explicability as the combination of intelligibility and accountability and argue that socially significant Artificial Intelligence decisions require a factual, direct, and clear explanation of the decision-making process and accountable allocation of responsibility.
- [inference; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/rules-business-and-organisations/dealing-citizens/are-there-restrictions-use-automated-decision-making_en; https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-15; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11023-018-9482-5] Taken together, these sources frame governance-policy application as an accountable control process that must be documented, monitorable, contestable, and attributable, not as an opaque output stream that cannot be reconstructed.

#### B. Deployed Large Language Model outputs remain only partially reproducible

- [fact; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output] Microsoft states that if the same chat-completion question is asked multiple times, different responses are likely by default and are therefore considered nondeterministic.
- [fact; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output] Microsoft also states that determinism is not guaranteed even when the `seed` parameter and `system_fingerprint` are the same across calls, and that longer outputs generally become less deterministic.
- [fact; source: https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/] Thinking Machines Lab says that Large Language Model service interfaces remain nondeterministic in practice even at temperature zero and argues that residual variance persists in both hosted services and common open-source inference stacks.
- [fact; source: https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/] The same technical analysis argues that the main practical source of nondeterminism is not merely sampling but serving-path behavior, especially batch-sensitive execution that changes floating-point accumulation behavior.
- [inference; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output; https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/] Provider features can reduce output variance, but they do not convert current deployed Large Language Model infrastructure into a governance-grade source of perfectly reproducible final decisions.

#### C. The conflict sits at the final governance decision surface, not at every generative step

- [inference; source: https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/rules-business-and-organisations/dealing-citizens/are-there-restrictions-use-automated-decision-making_en; https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-15] When a model output directly determines an approval, denial, sanction, escalation, or other consequential state change, residual nondeterminism conflicts with the need for traceability, meaningful challenge, override, and consistent performance.
- [inference; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://www.iso.org/standard/56639.html; https://www.iso.org/standard/56641.html; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11023-018-9482-5] Governance frameworks do not require every upstream cognitive step to be mathematically identical, but they do require that the accountable decision process, assigned roles, and evidence record be stable enough to justify and reproduce the organizational outcome.
- [inference; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-hybrid-architecture-probabilistic-llm-deterministic-governance.html] Stochasticity is therefore more tolerable in preparatory functions such as summarization, option generation, counterexample discovery, and draft rationale production than in the final control step, provided another deterministic or meaningfully supervised gate remains authoritative.

#### D. A risk-tier decision framework separates strict determinism from controlled stochasticity

- [inference; source: https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/rules-business-and-organisations/dealing-citizens/are-there-restrictions-use-automated-decision-making_en; https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-15] Strict determinism is required when a step can create legal or similarly significant effects, change access or enforcement state, trigger a regulatory or contractual commitment, or make an outcome difficult to reverse after execution.
- [inference; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-compliance-risks-stochastic-llm-governance-decisions.html] Controlled stochasticity is acceptable when the Large Language Model is limited to advisory or drafting roles, its output is captured as evidence, and a deterministic rule set or empowered reviewer still decides the final outcome.
- [inference; source: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-hybrid-architecture-probabilistic-llm-deterministic-governance.html] The minimum compensating controls for tolerated stochasticity evidenced here are explicit logging, meaningful human or rule-based final authority, override or stop capability, and fail-closed routing when the model output is not fit to drive action directly.

### §3 Reasoning

- [inference; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://www.iso.org/standard/56639.html; https://www.iso.org/standard/56641.html] The strongest governance sources speak in terms of accountable process, stakeholder assurance, monitoring, and documented roles, so the relevant unit of analysis is the control boundary that produces the organizational decision rather than the model's internal token path alone.
- [inference; source: https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/rules-business-and-organisations/dealing-citizens/are-there-restrictions-use-automated-decision-making_en; https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-15] Those obligations become most stringent where a system changes someone's rights, obligations, access, or exposure, because that is where contestability, override, and traceability are tested.
- [inference; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output; https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/] Since current deployed Large Language Models remain partially nondeterministic even under reproducibility controls, strict determinism cannot be achieved by parameter tuning alone.
- [inference; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-hybrid-architecture-probabilistic-llm-deterministic-governance.html] The defensible architecture is therefore to allow stochasticity where it improves interpretation or search, but to relocate determinism into the final control gate, the evidence record, and the meaningful oversight path.

### §4 Consistency Check

- [inference; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/rules-business-and-organisations/dealing-citizens/are-there-restrictions-use-automated-decision-making_en; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-15] No reviewed official source authorizes stochastic Large Language Model output to act as the sole final authority for high-risk or rights-significant governance decisions without additional safeguards.
- [inference; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output; https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/] There is no contradiction between acknowledging useful reproducibility controls and still requiring deterministic external control layers, because the official vendor documentation and technical explanation both leave residual variance in place.
- [inference; source: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14] There is also no contradiction between allowing human review and requiring determinism, because the cited oversight sources require human authority to be meaningful, not symbolic, and to be able to override, reverse, or stop the outcome.

### §5 Depth and Breadth Expansion

- [inference; source: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-15] **Regulatory lens:** the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act sharpens the answer from general accountability into concrete operational requirements, namely lifetime event logging, human override, safe-stop capability, and lifecycle consistency.
- [inference; source: https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/ai-foundry/openai/how-to/reproducible-output; https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/defeating-nondeterminism-in-llm-inference/] **Technical lens:** stochasticity is not only a prompt-level sampling choice but also a serving-path property, so governance controls that rely on provider-side "best effort" determinism remain fragile.
- [inference; source: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/guidance-on-ai-and-data-protection/how-do-we-ensure-fairness-in-ai/what-is-the-impact-of-article-22-of-the-uk-gdpr-on-fairness/; https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14] **Behavioral lens:** meaningful oversight fails when reviewers are positioned only to rubber-stamp or over-rely on automated output, so workflow design matters as much as model quality.
- [inference; source: https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/; https://www.iso.org/standard/56639.html; https://www.iso.org/standard/56641.html] **Organizational lens:** deterministic governance is partly a responsibility-allocation problem, because policies, review frequency, and executive accountability must remain stable even when models, prompts, or vendors change.
- [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-hybrid-architecture-probabilistic-llm-deterministic-governance.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-09-compliance-risks-stochastic-llm-governance-decisions.html] **Implementation lens:** prior repository work makes the abstract answer operational by showing that the most defensible pattern is to keep probabilistic reasoning upstream and deterministic control surfaces downstream.

### §6 Synthesis

**Executive summary:**

Governance policy application must be deterministic at the final decision surface whenever an output can create legal, rights-significant, compliance, security, or hard-to-reverse operational effects, because the strongest official sources require traceability, meaningful oversight, consistent performance, and contestable outcomes.
