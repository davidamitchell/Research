# Empirical evidence on rollout of organisation-wide low-code and no-code programs

## Research Question

What does peer-reviewed and independently verified empirical evidence reveal about the outcomes, success factors, governance models, and failure modes of organisation-wide low-code or no-code (LCNC) programs that let non-Information Technology (IT) business staff build applications, often called citizen development?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Peer-reviewed studies and independently conducted empirical research, including surveys, case studies, interview studies, and longitudinal analyses, on organisation-wide LCNC programs
- Quantitative and qualitative outcome measures, including time-to-delivery, local productivity, software quality, user satisfaction, relationship with central IT, and risk from business-built tools operating outside formal IT visibility and control
- Success factors identified across multiple independent studies
- Failure modes and documented risks in LCNC rollout programs
- Governance structures and their relationship to rollout outcomes
- Challenges in moving from pilot efforts to organisation-wide rollout
- Industry surveys with disclosed methodology when they materially sharpen the academic evidence

**Out of scope:**
- Vendor marketing materials or case studies without independent verification
- General low-code or no-code platform performance benchmarks not tied to rollout programs
- Theoretical frameworks without empirical grounding
- Application development methodology comparisons not focused on business-user builders

**Constraints:**
- Prefer peer-reviewed or independently verified studies over vendor-produced content
- Every source listed must include a verifiable URL
- No specific time-horizon restriction, but flag recency risk on fast-moving findings

## Context

[fact; source: https://digital.gov/2021/08/16/5-tips-for-implementing-citizen-development-in-your-rpa-program/; https://research.universityofgalway.ie/en/publications/adoption-of-low-code-and-no-code-development-a-systematic-literat-6/] Digital.gov defines citizen developers as non-technical employees enabled to create local automations, and the 2025 Journal of Systems and Software review describes LCNC as extending software creation beyond professional engineers to broader organisational participants.
[inference; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890] The practical decision is whether an organisation can move from scattered local experimentation to a rollout model that preserves speed while keeping software quality, security, compliance, and accountability inside a workable business-IT operating model.

## Approach

1. Identify peer-reviewed and independently conducted studies that specifically examine LCNC rollout programs and describe outcomes.
   1a. What quantitative outcomes, such as productivity gains, application quality, or time-to-delivery, have been measured across studies?
   1b. What qualitative outcomes, such as user satisfaction, IT relationship, or governance strain, have been reported?
2. Synthesize the success factors identified empirically across rollout studies, focusing on preconditions and operating practices associated with better outcomes.
3. Document failure modes and risks that have been empirically observed, including what causes programs to stall, create tools outside formal IT visibility, or generate unsustainable technical debt.
4. Examine how governance models differ between studied implementations and what the evidence says about which approaches produce better outcomes.
5. Assess the rollout evidence, focusing on the transition from pilot activity to organisation-wide scale and the barriers that appear at that transition.

#### C. Success factors and governance structures

- [fact; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://www.fernuni-hagen.de/bima/forschung/publikationen/MISQE2024_CitizenDevelopLCNC.shtml] Binzer et al. state that the success of citizen development requires addressing security, compliance, and organisational change through deliberate strategic design choices rather than only deploying the platform.
- [fact; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://portal.fis.tum.de/en/publications/governing-citizen-development-to-address-low-code-platform-challe/] Viljoen et al. emphasize the crucial roles of technical experts and platform-specific functionalities in governing citizen development and say this governance differs from conventional software-development governance.
- [fact; source: https://digital.gov/2021/08/16/5-tips-for-implementing-citizen-development-in-your-rpa-program/] Digital.gov recommends a centralized repository, extensive code review, role-based access controls, separate development, test, and production environments, leadership sponsorship, careful license allocation, and collaboration with chief information officer (CIO) and IT teams.
- [fact; source: https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890] The 18-firm collaboration study identifies self-empowerment, ideation generation, coordinated scaling, project execution, and professional catalyst as distinct mechanisms in enterprise citizen-developer programs.
- [fact; source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10257-022-00553-8] Fischer et al. structure Robotic Process Automation (RPA) implementation into three major phases, initialization, implementation, and scaling, after analyzing 35 project reports and refining the framework through expert interviews and workshops.
- [inference; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890; https://digital.gov/2021/08/16/5-tips-for-implementing-citizen-development-in-your-rpa-program/; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10257-022-00553-8] Durable rollout depends on combining business-user enablement with central intake, training, review, escalation, and lifecycle controls rather than giving makers unrestricted autonomy.

#### D. Failure modes and barriers

- [fact; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://portal.fis.tum.de/en/publications/governing-citizen-development-to-address-low-code-platform-challe/] Viljoen et al. identify substandard software quality, tools outside formal IT visibility, and technical debt as core risks when software development is entrusted to novices.
- [fact; source: https://research.universityofgalway.ie/en/publications/adoption-of-low-code-and-no-code-development-a-systematic-literat-6/; https://research.rtu.lv/en/publications/challenges-of-low-codeno-code-software-development-a-literature-r/] The 2025 systematic review and the 2022 LCNC literature review both identify governance, security, complexity, and platform lock-in as recurring challenges.
- [fact; source: https://colab.ws/articles/10.1109%2Faccess.2023.3258539] Kass et al. show that adoption drivers and inhibitors are context dependent even when experts agree on the most and least important factors, which means rollout barriers vary by organisational setting and cannot be solved with a single template.
- [fact; source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10257-022-00553-8] Fischer et al. report that expert knowledge is still needed to create reliable and scalable automations and cite prior work estimating that up to 50% of initial RPA implementations fail.
- [inference; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://colab.ws/articles/10.1109%2Faccess.2023.3258539; https://research.universityofgalway.ie/en/publications/adoption-of-low-code-and-no-code-development-a-systematic-literat-6/; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10257-022-00553-8] The main rollout failures are mixed socio-technical failures that combine weak governance, unclear ownership, training gaps, and complexity boundaries that exceed maker capability.

#### E. Scale-up and transition from pilot to rollout

- [fact; source: https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890] The 18-firm collaboration study treats coordinated scaling and professional catalyst roles as separate mechanisms from self-empowerment, which indicates that enterprise rollout needs capabilities that are not required in small local experiments.
- [fact; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3] The 24-company strategy study presents citizen development as a strategic design problem, which implies that rollout success depends on organisation-wide choices rather than on isolated tool adoption.
- [fact; source: https://digital.gov/2021/08/16/5-tips-for-implementing-citizen-development-in-your-rpa-program/; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10257-022-00553-8] Digital.gov and Fischer et al. both distinguish initial enablement from later structured rollout by emphasizing leadership support, standard protocols, separate environments, and explicit scaling phases.
- [inference; source: https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890; https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://digital.gov/2021/08/16/5-tips-for-implementing-citizen-development-in-your-rpa-program/; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10257-022-00553-8] Pilot success does not translate into organisation-wide success unless the program adds intake, triage, support, training, and escalation mechanisms that absorb rising coordination cost.

### §6 Synthesis

**Executive summary:**

Empirical evidence shows that organisation-wide LCNC programs can improve local delivery speed and broaden participation, but durable rollout depends on treating citizen development as a governed operating model rather than as a simple tooling rollout. [inference; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890; https://research.universityofgalway.ie/en/publications/adoption-of-low-code-and-no-code-development-a-systematic-literat-6/]
Across the strongest accessible studies, the recurring success pattern is bounded maker autonomy supported by central standards, technical experts, collaboration mechanisms, training, review paths, and escalation for higher-complexity work. [fact; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890; https://digital.gov/2021/08/16/5-tips-for-implementing-citizen-development-in-your-rpa-program/]
The recurring failure pattern is socio-technical: studies repeatedly describe substandard software quality, tools outside formal IT visibility, technical debt, security strain, and context-specific adoption barriers when governance and support lag behind growth. [fact; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://research.universityofgalway.ie/en/publications/adoption-of-low-code-and-no-code-development-a-systematic-literat-6/; https://research.rtu.lv/en/publications/challenges-of-low-codeno-code-software-development-a-literature-r/; https://colab.ws/articles/10.1109%2Faccess.2023.3258539]
Evidence for exact enterprise-scale performance gains remains thin, because the best accessible multi-organisation studies focus much more on governance design and collaboration mechanics than on standardized before-and-after outcome metrics. [inference; source: https://research.universityofgalway.ie/en/publications/adoption-of-low-code-and-no-code-development-a-systematic-literat-6/; https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890]

**Key findings:**

1. **The strongest accessible empirical studies show that enterprise citizen-development programs succeed when organisations treat them as governed operating models, pairing maker enablement with central standards, technical support, and explicit design choices around security, compliance, and organisational change.** ([inference]; high confidence; source: https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/3; https://aisel.aisnet.org/misqe/vol23/iss3/6; https://hdl.handle.net/10125/108890)
