# What Are the Micro-Transaction Costs of Internal Knowledge Sourcing?

## Research Question

What micro-transaction costs are borne by knowledge seekers and providers during internal peer-to-peer transfers, and how do these costs shift the choice between self-solving ("make") and help-seeking ("buy") strategies?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Search, coordination, social, and verification costs in internal knowledge exchange.
- Cost distribution between seeker and provider roles.
- Decision thresholds that trigger self-solving versus help-seeking behaviour.

**Out of scope:**
- Full enterprise financial accounting of knowledge-management systems.
- External procurement and vendor contracting costs.

**Constraints:** Use transaction-cost economics and organisational behaviour literature with operationally measurable cost components.

## Context

Internal peer-to-peer knowledge transfer is better explained as a live cost-allocation problem than as a culture-only problem, because workers compare the real effort of finding, reaching, trusting, and integrating another person's knowledge against the effort of solving alone. [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032]

A micro-cost model matters because support channels can exist formally while self-solving, duplicate work, or outsider sourcing still win behaviourally when internal requests are slower, riskier, or harder to verify at the moment of need. [inference; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1030.0192; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-trust-institutions-vs-incentive-schemes-knowledge-sharing.html]

## Approach

1. Define a taxonomy of micro-transaction costs in internal knowledge sourcing.
2. Analyse how costs differ by role, hierarchy, and department boundary.
3. Build a practical decision model linking cost level to make-versus-buy behaviour.

### §2 Investigation

#### Prior completed-item sweep

- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] The completed activation-energy item finds that knowledge requests stall when expertise is hard to identify, hard to reach, or socially risky to ask for.
- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html] The completed low-effort-pathways item argues that workers stay on the route with the lowest combined search, access, interpretation, and social-risk burden.
- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-trust-institutions-vs-incentive-schemes-knowledge-sharing.html] The completed trust-versus-incentives item concludes that repeated exchange stays durable when institutions keep the next request cheap, safe, and easy to interpret.

#### A. What costs arise before transfer starts?

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428] Borgatti and Cross model information seeking as a function of knowing what another person knows, valuing that knowledge, gaining timely access to that person's thinking, and perceiving that asking will not be too costly.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032] Hansen finds that lower-intensity relationships across subunits help teams search for useful knowledge in other units.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1030.0192] Gray and Meister define knowledge sourcing as intentionally accessing others' expertise, experience, insights, and opinions, and report that this behaviour helps explain learning outcomes in a global organisation.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1030.0192] Discovery cost is not only locating an expert, because the seeker must also estimate expected value quickly enough to justify the interruption and waiting time of asking.
- [fact; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] Menon and Pfeffer report that managers often value external knowledge more highly than internal knowledge because learning from internal competitors carries different status implications and internal knowledge is more exposed to scrutiny.
- [inference; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] Social cost enters before transfer begins, because the seeker must judge whether asking an insider will signal weakness, create reciprocal debt, or expose the request to local status competition.

#### B. What costs arise after an expert has been found?

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032] Hansen finds that the same weak ties, lower-intensity cross-unit relationships, that help search can impede the transfer of complex knowledge, which tends to require a stronger tie between the two parties.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] Szulanski finds that internal transfer barriers are driven mainly by the recipient's limited ability to understand and use incoming knowledge, unclear understanding of why a practice works, and arduous source-recipient relationships rather than by motivation alone.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893] Argote and Ingram argue that interactions among people, tasks, and tools are the hardest forms of organisational knowledge to transfer across contexts.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171110] Grant frames the firm as an institution for integrating specialised knowledge and emphasises coordination mechanisms rather than knowledge creation alone.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171110] Verification cost sits heavily on the seeker after contact, because the seeker must decode whether another person's answer fits the local task, tools, and context well enough to use safely.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893] Provider cost sits in interruption, scheduling, explanation, and translation effort, because the provider must make tacit or context-bound knowledge legible enough for another unit or colleague to apply it.

#### C. How do role, hierarchy, and boundary change the cost mix?

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032] Hansen shows that cross-subunit knowledge exchange becomes slower when the knowledge being transferred is highly complex.
- [fact; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] Menon and Pfeffer show that internal status competition changes the perceived value and social meaning of asking insiders for help.
- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] The adjacent activation-energy item reports that visible hierarchy, evaluation anxiety, and uncertainty about who can help suppress help-seeking before first contact.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] Department boundaries and status distance reweight the cost mix by raising access delay, interpretation effort, and image risk at the same time, so the same question can be cheap within a local team and expensive across a formal boundary.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1030.0192; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] Intellectually demanding work raises the potential value of asking, but it also raises the penalty of asking the wrong person or receiving advice that is hard to interpret correctly.

#### D. What behavioural threshold follows?

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://doi.org/10.1086/227496] Coase and Williamson treat the transaction as the relevant unit of choice and explain governance choices as attempts to economise on comparative coordination costs.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1086/227496] Williamson states that the transaction-cost approach applies both to efficient firm boundaries and to the organisation of internal transactions.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://doi.org/10.1086/227496; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] The make-versus-buy threshold inside the firm is crossed when the seeker's expected search, access, social, and verification costs, plus expected provider delay, exceed the expected time and error cost of self-solving.
- [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-trust-institutions-vs-incentive-schemes-knowledge-sharing.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] The same threshold can be shifted downward by visible expertise maps, predictable access routines, and socially safe asking norms, because those routines lower several micro-cost components at once rather than only improving content quality abstractly.

### §3 Reasoning

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] The strongest direct evidence concerns seeker-side discovery, access, and interpretation costs, plus the way complexity changes transfer requirements after an expert has been found.
- [fact; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] The strongest evidence for social cost is that internal asking can change status exposure and scrutiny, which makes insider knowledge behaviourally expensive even when it is formally available.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171110] Provider-side costs are less directly measured in the consulted studies, so interruption and explanation burden should be treated as synthesis-level inference grounded in transfer difficulty, relational arduousness, and knowledge-integration demands rather than as separately quantified facts.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://doi.org/10.1086/227496; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032] The decision model is therefore comparative rather than absolute: asking wins when the expected net resolution gain exceeds the combined micro-cost of discovery, access, transfer, and validation.

### §4 Consistency Check

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428] Borgatti and Cross report weaker support for a generic undifferentiated cost variable than for knowing, value, and access.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] That does not undermine the cost model, because the wider literature shows that cost is multi-part, with discovery, delay, image risk, and interpretation burden each able to block asking independently.
- [fact; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] Menon and Pfeffer could look inconsistent with an internal-knowledge advantage, because they show cases where outsider knowledge is valued more highly than insider knowledge.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] The tension resolves when firm-level coordination advantage is separated from person-level request cost, because an internal option can be organisationally cheaper in aggregate while still being behaviourally expensive for an individual request.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893] Overall confidence remains medium because the taxonomy is strongly supported, but provider-side cost allocation and exact threshold weights remain inferential rather than directly quantified across a common dataset.

### §5 Depth and Breadth Expansion

**Economic lens**

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://doi.org/10.1086/227496] Internal knowledge requests are internal transactions in the Williamson sense, so comparing self-solving and help-seeking is a comparative transaction-cost choice rather than a purely cultural preference.

**Behavioural lens**

- [fact; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] Asking for help is partly an impression-management act, because the seeker must manage status exposure before any technical transfer begins.

**Organisational lens**

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171110] Cross-boundary requests are costly partly because specialised knowledge is distributed unevenly and because integrating it often requires stronger coordination mechanisms than search alone.

**Operational lens**

- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-trust-institutions-vs-incentive-schemes-knowledge-sharing.html] The highest-fit intervention pattern is to lower visible expertise cost, access uncertainty, and social risk together, because reducing only one component leaves another component able to keep self-solving on the cheaper side of the threshold.

### §6 Synthesis

#### Executive Summary

- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://doi.org/10.1086/227496; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] Internal peer-to-peer knowledge sourcing shifts from help-seeking to self-solving when the expected cost of finding, accessing, trusting, and integrating another person's knowledge exceeds the expected cost of solving the problem alone.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] The recurring micro-cost families are discovery cost, access and waiting cost, social and status cost, and verification and interpretation cost, with complexity and department boundaries increasing several costs at once.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893] Seekers bear the upfront and fit-assessment costs, while providers bear interruption and explanation costs that become material when knowledge is tacit, ambiguous, or cross-boundary.
- [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-trust-institutions-vs-incentive-schemes-knowledge-sharing.html] A reliable way to shift behaviour toward asking is to make expertise visible, access predictable, and asking socially safe at the same time.

## Findings

### Executive Summary

Internal peer-to-peer knowledge sourcing shifts from help-seeking to self-solving when the expected cost of finding, accessing, trusting, and integrating another person's knowledge exceeds the expected cost of solving the problem alone. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://doi.org/10.1086/227496; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105]

The recurring micro-cost families are discovery cost, access and waiting cost, social and status cost, and verification and interpretation cost, with complexity and department boundaries increasing several costs at once. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference]

Seekers bear the upfront discovery, access, and fit-assessment burden, while providers bear interruption and explanation costs that rise when knowledge is tacit, ambiguous, or cross-boundary. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171110]

The strongest practical implication is that organisations are more likely to shift behaviour toward asking when they lower several micro-cost components together, especially expertise visibility, access predictability, and interpersonal safety. [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-trust-institutions-vs-incentive-schemes-knowledge-sharing.html]

### Key Findings

1. **Internal knowledge sourcing is constrained by four recurring micro-cost families, discovery, access and waiting, social and status exposure, and verification and interpretation, rather than by a single generic request cost.** ([inference]; high confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference)
2. **Search and transfer are distinct stages, because lower-intensity cross-unit relationships help people locate expertise across subunits but often slow the transfer of complex knowledge after the expert has been found.** ([fact]; medium confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032)
3. **Seekers usually bear the first visible costs of asking, including locating expertise, estimating whether it is worth asking, waiting for access, and absorbing the social meaning of the request.** ([fact]; high confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1030.0192; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference)
4. **Providers bear real but less directly measured costs in the form of interruption, scheduling, explanation, and translation effort, and those costs rise when knowledge is tacit, ambiguous, or tightly coupled to local tools and routines.** ([inference]; medium confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://doi.org/10.1006/obhd.2000.2893; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171110)
5. **Internal help can become behaviourally more expensive than outsider help when status rivalry, scrutiny, or evaluation anxiety make insider requests socially costly before any technical transfer occurs.** ([fact]; medium confidence; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html)
6. **The internal make-versus-buy decision is best modelled as a threshold comparison in which workers self-solve when expected request costs and delays outweigh the expected resolution benefit of asking.** ([inference]; medium confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x; https://doi.org/10.1086/227496; https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-03-02-transaction-costs.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-03-10-nature-of-the-firm-coase-organisations.html)
7. **Organisational routines reduce internal knowledge-sourcing cost reliably when they lower several cost components together, especially expertise visibility, access predictability, and interpersonal safety.** ([inference]; medium confidence; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-trust-institutions-vs-incentive-schemes-knowledge-sharing.html)
8. **Knowledge sourcing is associated with stronger learning outcomes, and the reported effect is stronger for workers with stronger learning orientation and for roles whose tasks are more intellectually demanding.** ([fact]; medium confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1030.0192)
