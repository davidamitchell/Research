# Public sentiment on AI in banking and high-trust institutions

## Research Question

What does current (2024–2025) survey data reveal about customer sentiment toward Artificial Intelligence (AI) in banking and high-trust Financial Services (FS) institutions — in Australia, across Asia-Pacific (APAC), and globally — and would a two-plane architecture (a "production plane" that augments human employees, and an "operational plane" that minimises direct AI involvement in binding customer-financial-data decisions) represent a viable, trust-differentiated approach that can be clearly communicated to customers?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Customer sentiment surveys from 2024–2025 covering AI in banking and FS
- Australian-specific data (Publicis Sapient, KPMG/University of Melbourne)
- APAC regional data (F5/Twimbit, FICO, EPAM Continuum, Edelman)
- Global benchmarks (Accenture, Qualtrics XM Institute, Edelman Trust Barometer)
- Regulatory context: Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) Cross-industry Prudential Standard (CPS) 230, Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) AI guidelines, Association of Banks in Singapore (ABS) Generative AI (Gen AI) Guardrails Handbook
- Architecture viability: production plane (human augmentation) vs. operational plane (AI guardrails on binding financial decisions)
- Communication strategies for trust differentiation

**Out of scope:**
- Detailed technical implementation of specific AI/Machine Learning (ML) models
- Non-financial-services industries (healthcare, education) except where trust comparison is relevant
- Vendor-specific product recommendations
- Retail banking product design

**Constraints:**
- Publicly available survey data and reports only
- English-language sources
- Published 2024–2026

## Context

The 2024 Publicis Sapient Customer Banking Report found that 96% of Australian banking customers have reservations about their banks using AI. Within that: 58% prefer speaking to a human bank employee, 54% worry about job losses, and 49% are concerned about data security and privacy. These numbers represent a near-universal unease in one of the world's most digitally mature banking markets.

At the same time, AI investment in banking is accelerating — McKinsey projects banks that scale AI will achieve 60%+ productivity gains in some processes. The tension between customer anxiety and institutional investment creates a strategic question: can an organisation articulate an architecture that addresses both the efficiency imperative and the trust deficit?

The proposed two-plane model — a production plane where AI makes human employees more effective (not replacing them), and an operational plane that minimises direct AI involvement in systems where customer financial data drives binding decisions — is a specific, testable architectural claim. This research evaluates whether survey evidence supports that architecture as a trust differentiator.

## Approach

1. **Q1 — Australian baseline:** Gather the Publicis Sapient 2024 data and the KPMG/University of Melbourne 2025 Trust in AI study for Australia-specific sentiment.
2. **Q2 — APAC regional sentiment:** Collect data from EPAM Continuum, F5/Twimbit, FICO APAC, and Edelman Trust Barometer on APAC-wide customer attitudes.
3. **Q3 — Global benchmarks:** Use the Accenture 2025 Global Banking Consumer Study (49,300 respondents, 39 countries) and Qualtrics XM Institute 2025 data for global comparison.
4. **Q4 — Regulatory alignment:** Assess whether the two-plane model aligns with APRA CPS 230, MAS AI guidelines, and ABS Gen AI Guardrails.
5. **Q5 — Architecture viability:** Evaluate whether existing evidence supports "human augmentation + AI guardrails on binding decisions" as a trust-building strategy, and what communication requirements exist.

### §3 Reasoning

**Facts established:**

1. Australian AI trust is among the lowest globally: 36% willing to trust AI (KPMG 2025), 96% have reservations in banking context (Publicis Sapient 2024).
2. APAC is split: developing APAC markets (China, India) are AI-optimistic; developed markets (Australia, Japan) are AI-skeptical.
3. Globally, only 26% trust organisations to use AI responsibly (Qualtrics 2025); banking remains the most trusted FS sub-sector at 64% overall (Edelman 2025).
4. Customer concerns cluster around three themes: loss of human connection (58% AU, 50%+ global), job displacement (54% AU, 97% India/Singapore), and data privacy/security (49% AU, 68–80% global).
5. Acceptance pattern: AI for routine tasks (support triage, fraud alerts, balance checks) is accepted by ~48% of APAC consumers; AI for binding financial decisions (advice, loans) has ~39% confidence — a 9-point gap.
6. Regulatory frameworks (APRA CPS 230, MAS, ABS) mandate human oversight for high-risk AI decisions, aligning with the operational plane concept.
7. Direct experience is the strongest trust driver — Edelman shows 40–50 point trust increase when users interact with AI tools that help them understand financial concepts.

**Inferences drawn:**

1. The 96% Australian reservations figure, combined with the 36% trust figure, represents the widest trust-use gap in the KPMG dataset — Australians use AI nearly as much as the global average (50%) but trust it at the lowest rate.
2. A two-plane architecture is not only viable but is effectively what regulators are mandating: human oversight on binding decisions (operational plane) and permitted AI augmentation for staff productivity (production plane).
3. The differentiation value depends on communication clarity — the architecture itself is regulatory table-stakes; the competitive advantage comes from articulating it to customers in terms they understand and experience.

**Assumptions made:**

1. Customer sentiment surveys from 2024–2025 remain directionally valid through 2026.
2. "Binding financial decisions" can be operationally defined and separated from "augmentation tasks" in a real banking architecture.
3. Customers will attribute trust value to an explicit articulation of the two-plane separation, rather than treating it as unremarkable boilerplate.

### §4 Consistency Check

**Potential contradiction 1:** EPAM reports 96% satisfaction among AI users in banking, while Publicis Sapient reports 96% have reservations.
**Resolution:** These are not contradictory — they describe different populations. EPAM's 96% satisfaction is among the 21% who have actually used AI tools; Publicis Sapient's 96% reservations covers all customers, including the 79% who have not used AI. The data suggests an "experience gap": once customers use AI, satisfaction is high, but most have not yet done so.

**Potential contradiction 2:** APAC is described as both "more positive about AI" (FutureCIO: 71% believe AI has positive impact) and having deep trust deficits (F5: trust gap persists).
**Resolution:** APAC averages mask extreme country-level variation. China and India drive up the positive numbers; Australia and Japan pull them down. The aggregate "APAC is positive" claim is accurate at the regional mean but misleading for any single country.

**Potential contradiction 3:** Edelman says trust in FS is rising (64% globally, up 2 points), while Qualtrics says only 26% trust organisations to use AI responsibly.
**Resolution:** These measure different constructs. Edelman measures trust in financial institutions as entities; Qualtrics measures trust in organisational AI use specifically. Customers can trust their bank while distrusting their bank's AI.

### §5 Depth and Breadth Expansion

**Technical lens:** The production/operational plane model maps to established enterprise architecture patterns. The "production plane" parallels Decision Support Systems (DSS) — AI prepares data, surfaces insights, and drafts recommendations that human employees review. The "operational plane" parallels core banking systems with straight-through processing (STP) — these are regulated, auditable, and the two-plane model says AI's role here should be minimised or purely supervisory (fraud detection monitoring, not decisioning).

**Regulatory lens:** APRA CPS 230 (effective July 2025) and MAS AI guidelines create a regulatory environment where the operational plane's constraints are not optional — they are mandatory for high-risk AI deployments. Australia does not yet have AI-specific legislation, but CPS 230's operational risk framing effectively captures AI risk. The European Union (EU) AI Act's risk-based classification system also supports tiered approaches. The two-plane model would position an institution ahead of regulatory evolution.

**Economic lens:** McKinsey projects banks that scale AI will see 60%+ productivity gains. Accenture shows banks with top 20% advocacy scores grow revenue 1.7x faster. If the two-plane architecture can convert Australia's 96% reservations into measured advocacy through trust-building, the economic case is strong: a 1.7x revenue growth multiplier on the 83% of Australians who say responsible AI practices would increase their trust.

**Behavioural lens:** The "experience gap" (21% have used AI banking tools; 96% of those are satisfied) suggests a trust problem rooted in unfamiliarity, not in technology failure. Behavioural economics research on "ambiguity aversion" — the tendency to prefer known risks over unknown risks — predicts exactly this pattern. The communication strategy should focus on making the AI's role concrete and observable, not abstract and theoretical. Edelman's finding that direct experience raises trust by 40–50 points strongly supports "show, don't tell" communication.

**Demographic lens:** Edelman's 41-point UK age gap (18–34: 59% trust; 55+: 18% trust) and EPAM's data showing 37% of 18–34 year-olds considering switching banks suggest that trust-based AI positioning is more urgent for retaining older customers while meeting younger customers' digital expectations. The two-plane model can be framed differently by channel: digital-first for younger customers with AI-augmented speed, and human-first with AI-powered preparation for older customers.
