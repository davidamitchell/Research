# Regulatory and standards preconditions for deployment of Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems that can take multi-step actions: does incomplete access control and data governance constitute a control failure?

## Research Question

Under applicable regulatory and standards frameworks, including Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) CPS 230, the European Union (EU) Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS) v4, International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) 42001, National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-207, NIST SP 800-53, Basel Committee operational resilience principles, United Kingdom (UK) Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) Artificial Intelligence (AI) guidance, and ISO 31000, what organisational preconditions are required before deploying AI systems that can take multi-step actions on behalf of users, and does deploying those systems into an environment where access control does not yet limit identities to the minimum permissions needed for each task, the data estate is not fully classified, citizen development is ungoverned, and systems capability debt remains unresolved constitute a current or foreseeable control failure?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- What each named framework requires, explicitly or implicitly, as organisational preconditions for safe deployment of AI systems that can take multi-step actions on behalf of users (agentic AI)
- Whether incomplete access control that does not yet limit identities to the minimum permissions needed for each task, and incomplete data governance, constitute a control failure (not merely a governance gap) under each framework
- The specific language and provisions most useful for a board risk committee presentation and a regulator relationship context
- Gaps in the current regulatory landscape where the argument must rely on principles and inference rather than explicit provision
- Assessment of how regulators in comparable jurisdictions are likely to close those gaps
- Frameworks to assess: APRA CPS 230, DORA, PCI-DSS v4, ISO 42001, NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA)), NIST SP 800-53, Basel Committee on Banking Supervision operational resilience principles, UK FCA and PRA AI guidance, ISO 31000
- Context: a New Zealand Crown-owned bank subject to Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ) prudential supervision, NZ Privacy Act 2020, and PCI-DSS; operating Microsoft 365 with Copilot and Copilot Studio and building agentic capability on AWS Bedrock; with existing access control debt, a partially classified data estate, a citizen development surface predating agentic AI, and systems capability gaps historically bridged by manual processes and ungoverned tooling

**Out of scope:**
- AI model safety or alignment frameworks not directly relevant to enterprise deployment governance
- Theoretical novelty of the synthesis argument (covered by the companion Q1 item)
- Empirical evidence on systems capability debt costs and citizen development causation (covered by the companion Q2 item)
- RBNZ-specific guidance (assumed as primary regulatory context; this item focuses on comparators and broader frameworks)
- Implementation details of specific technical controls

**Constraints:**
- Prioritise official framework text and official guidance documents over secondary commentary
- Assess each framework individually before synthesising cross-framework findings
- The "control failure vs. governance gap" distinction is legally and regulatorily significant, so be precise about which frameworks support which characterisation
- Sources must be current versions of each framework (not superseded versions)

## Context

- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-04-24-ai-agent-regulation-global-financial-services.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-04-24-business-led-low-code-agent-governance.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-04-22-knowledge-curation-governance-for-regulated-ai.html] Prior completed research in this repository already established that low-code and agentic Artificial Intelligence (AI) deployment in regulated financial institutions does not move prudential, privacy, conduct, or governance accountability away from the institution, and that durable value depends on governed knowledge, bounded use-case routing, and enforceable platform guardrails.
- [inference; source: https://handbook.apra.gov.au/standard/cps-230; https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022R2554; https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/207/final; https://www.iso.org/standard/65694.html] The unresolved question is whether introducing write-capable agents into an estate with access controls that still grant more permissions than needed for each task, unclassified data, ungoverned citizen development, and unresolved systems capability debt should now be characterised as a control failure under existing frameworks, because machine-speed execution removes human-speed friction without first repairing the underlying control surface.

## Approach

1. **Framework-by-framework precondition analysis**: For each named framework, extract the explicit provisions or principles that bear on: (a) preconditions for deploying automated systems acting on behalf of users; (b) access control and least-privilege requirements; (c) data classification and governance requirements; (d) third-party and technology risk management; (e) operational resilience and severe-but-plausible scenario requirements.
2. **Control failure vs. governance gap assessment**: For each framework, assess whether the described environment (incomplete access control, unclassified data, ungoverned citizen development, unresolved systems capability debt) constitutes a current control failure, a foreseeable control failure, or a governance gap; note the specific provisions or principles that support each characterisation.
3. **Board and regulator language extraction**: Identify the specific language, provisions, and cross-framework patterns most useful for a board risk committee presentation and a regulator relationship; prioritise provisions that are explicit, enforceable, or precedent-backed.
4. **Regulatory gap mapping**: Identify where the argument must rely on principles and inference rather than explicit provision; assess how each named regulator or standards body is likely to close those gaps based on consultation papers, speeches, and stated regulatory direction.
5. **APRA CPS 230 deep dive**: As the primary Trans-Tasman comparator and likely RBNZ influence, analyse CPS 230's treatment of technology risk, operational risk management systems, and the principle that critical operations must remain resilient to severe-but-plausible scenarios; assess applicability to the agentic AI deployment scenario.
6. **DORA deep dive**: As the leading jurisdiction standard for digital operational resilience, analyse DORA's Information and Communication Technology (ICT) risk management framework, third-party risk provisions, and the requirement for pre-deployment risk assessment; assess whether agentic AI operating across an unclassified data estate would satisfy DORA's ICT risk requirements.
7. **Zero trust and credential delegation**: Analyse NIST SP 800-207 specifically regarding agentic credential delegation operating across an environment with incomplete least-privilege implementation; assess whether this constitutes an architectural violation of zero trust principles and how that maps to regulatory control requirements.
8. **ISO 31000 risk management failure**: Assess whether the failure to evaluate and remediate known foundational risk (access control debt, data classification gaps, ungoverned citizen development) before deploying capability that amplifies that risk constitutes a risk management failure under ISO 31000's principles and guidelines.

#### APRA CPS 230

- [fact; source: https://handbook.apra.gov.au/standard/cps-230] CPS 230 requires APRA-regulated entities to identify, assess, and manage operational risks with effective internal controls, monitoring, and remediation, to maintain critical operations within tolerance levels through severe disruptions, and to manage risks arising from service providers.
- [fact; source: https://handbook.apra.gov.au/standard/cps-230] CPS 230 also requires sound information and information technology capability, monitoring of the age and health of information assets, documentation of the processes and resources needed to deliver critical operations, including people, technology, information, facilities, service providers, interdependencies, associated risks, obligations, key data, and controls, and business continuity plans tested against severe but plausible scenarios.
- [inference; source: https://handbook.apra.gov.au/standard/cps-230] Under CPS 230, incomplete least-privilege access, an unclassified data estate, and unresolved systems capability debt are not neutral background conditions once write-capable agentic AI is proposed, because the entity would be accelerating actions across information assets and service-provider dependencies without first demonstrating the sound technology capability, mapped data and control environment, and severe-but-plausible scenario readiness the standard requires.
- [inference; source: https://handbook.apra.gov.au/standard/cps-230] If write-capable agents are already operating in or adjacent to critical operations, the position is best described as a current control failure under CPS 230 rather than a mere governance gap, because weak internal controls and weak technology capability would already be affecting the resilience of a live operational process.

#### DORA

- [fact; source: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022R2554] DORA requires a sound, comprehensive, and well-documented Information and Communication Technology (ICT) risk management framework, an internal governance and control framework for ICT risk, clear roles and responsibilities, annual review, internal audit, and continuous improvement.
- [fact; source: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022R2554] DORA also requires financial entities to identify, classify, and adequately document ICT-supported business functions, information assets, and ICT assets, map critical assets and interdependencies, continuously monitor and control the security and functioning of ICT systems, conduct business impact analysis for severe disruptions, and maintain a register and audit rights for ICT third-party arrangements.
- [inference; source: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022R2554] Under DORA, incomplete least-privilege access and an unclassified data estate are current control failures, not merely governance gaps, because the regulation explicitly requires identified and classified assets, documented dependencies, and protection against unauthorised access as part of the pre-existing digital-operational-resilience baseline.
- [inference; source: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022R2554] Deploying agentic AI across that environment would therefore aggravate an existing DORA deficiency and create an additional foreseeable control failure on ICT concentration, continuity, and auditability grounds.

#### PCI DSS

- [fact; source: https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/standards/pci-dss/; https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/document_library/?category=pcidss&document=pci_dss] PCI DSS is the baseline technical and operational standard designed to protect payment account data, applies to entities that store, process, or transmit cardholder data or could impact the security of the cardholder data environment, and the official PCI document library identifies PCI DSS v4.0.1 as the current standard.
- [assumption; source: https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/standards/pci-dss/; https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/document_library/?category=pcidss&document=pci_dss; https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/pdfs/pci_fs_data_storage.pdf] Requirement-level mapping to stored-data protection and business-need-to-know access control remains materially unchanged in PCI DSS v4.0.1, but the direct standard PDF could not be retrieved in this runtime. **Justification:** the official PCI public materials continue to frame PCI DSS around payment-data protection and controlled access, and the document library identifies the current standard version, but the inaccessible full text limits clause-level precision.
- [inference; source: https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/standards/pci-dss/; https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/document_library/?category=pcidss&document=pci_dss] In any environment that touches payment account data, incomplete least-privilege access and partial data classification should be treated as a current control failure rather than a governance gap before agentic AI is introduced, because PCI DSS is a protection baseline for exactly the data-handling and access-path risks that agentic automation would multiply.

#### ISO/IEC 42001

- [fact; source: https://www.iso.org/standard/81230.html] ISO/IEC 42001 specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an Artificial Intelligence Management System (AIMS) and presents AI governance as a structured way to manage risks and opportunities associated with AI.
- [fact; source: https://www.iso.org/standard/81230.html] ISO/IEC 42001's public summary states that the standard is intended for organisations developing, providing, or using AI systems and is designed to put in place policies and procedures for the sound governance of AI-related risk and opportunity.
- [inference; source: https://www.iso.org/standard/81230.html] Under ISO/IEC 42001, incomplete access control, unclassified data, and ungoverned citizen development mean that the management-system preconditions for disciplined AI deployment are not yet in place, so deployment would be a current management-system deficiency and at least a foreseeable control failure.

#### NIST SP 800-207

- [fact; source: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/207/final] NIST SP 800-207 states that Zero Trust Architecture assumes no implicit trust based on network location or asset ownership, requires authentication and authorization before a session to an enterprise resource is established, and focuses on protecting resources rather than network segments.
- [fact; source: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/207/final] NIST SP 800-207 defines zero trust as a response to remote users, bring-your-own-device patterns, and cloud-based assets that sit outside a traditional enterprise boundary.
- [inference; source: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/207/final] Agentic credential delegation across an environment that still lacks granular least privilege is a current architectural control failure under zero trust principles, because it multiplies the number and speed of resource access decisions while relying on precisely the implicit trust and broad standing permissions that Zero Trust Architecture is intended to eliminate.

#### NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5

- [fact; source: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/53/r5/upd1/final] NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 is the NIST security and privacy control catalog for information systems and organizations, and it is the reference control catalog used to structure access, audit, privacy, and system-security expectations across federal and comparator control environments.
- [inference; source: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/53/r5/upd1/final] As a comparator framework, SP 800-53 strengthens the conclusion that incomplete access discipline, weak auditability, and poor asset understanding are control-surface problems rather than abstract governance issues, and introducing agentic automation before those layers are designed and tested is a foreseeable control failure.

#### Basel Committee principles for operational resilience

- [fact; source: https://www.bis.org/bcbs/publ/d516.htm] The Basel Committee's principles for operational resilience are intended to strengthen banks' ability to withstand operational-risk events that could cause significant operational failures or wide-scale disruptions, including cyber incidents and technology failures.
- [inference; source: https://www.bis.org/bcbs/publ/d516.htm] The Basel framing supports a control-failure characterisation when agentic AI is layered onto incomplete access and data governance, because the operational-resilience question is whether the bank can withstand technology-driven disruption in critical operations, not whether the technology is novel enough to postpone control remediation.

#### FCA / Bank of England DP5/22 and FCA FS23/6

- [fact; source: https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2022/october/artificial-intelligence; https://www.fca.org.uk/publications/feedback-statements/fs23-6-artifical-intelligence-machine-learning] DP5/22 and FS23/6 frame the United Kingdom approach as one of safe and responsible AI adoption under the current regulatory framework, ask whether clarification of existing regulations may be helpful, and state that the supervisory authorities began by considering how existing sectoral legal requirements and guidance apply to AI.
- [fact; source: https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2022/october/artificial-intelligence] DP5/22 says the supervisory authorities' approach is largely limited to clarifying how the existing regulatory framework applies to AI and addressing any identified gaps, with specific attention to governance and decision-making challenges.
- [inference; source: https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2022/october/artificial-intelligence; https://www.fca.org.uk/publications/feedback-statements/fs23-6-artifical-intelligence-machine-learning] The United Kingdom material therefore supports a regulator-facing argument that incomplete access control, unclassified data, and ungoverned citizen development are not excused by the absence of AI-specific rules, because the policy stance is that existing governance and resilience obligations already apply and gaps should be interpreted through those existing frameworks.

#### PRA SS1/23

- [fact; source: https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2023/may/model-risk-management-principles-for-banks-ss] PRA SS1/23 treats model risk as a risk discipline in its own right, applies its expectations to all model and risk types within scope, including vendor models, and sets five principles covering model identification and classification, governance, development and use, independent validation, and model-risk mitigants.
- [fact; source: https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2023/may/model-risk-management-principles-for-banks-ss] SS1/23 expects firms to conduct an initial and then annual self-assessment, prepare remediation plans where shortcomings are found, document findings, and share them with boards in a timely manner.
- [inference; source: https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2023/may/model-risk-management-principles-for-banks-ss] Where agentic AI is used in material decision support, model-led workflow execution, or risk processes, deploying it without inventory, risk classification, governance, independent validation, and mitigants is a current control failure under SS1/23's model-risk logic; outside formal SS1/23 scope, the statement remains a strong comparator rather than a direct rule.

#### ISO 31000

- [fact; source: https://www.iso.org/standard/65694.html] ISO 31000 provides principles and guidelines for risk management, embeds risk management into governance, strategy, planning, reporting, processes, values, and culture, and stresses monitoring, reviewing, and continually improving risk management.
- [inference; source: https://www.iso.org/standard/65694.html] Under ISO 31000, failure to evaluate and remediate known foundational access-control, data-governance, and operating-model weaknesses before deploying capability that amplifies those weaknesses is a risk-management failure, not a mere governance preference issue.

#### NIST AI RMF 1.0 cross-check

- [fact; source: https://www.nist.gov/publications/artificial-intelligence-risk-management-framework-ai-rmf-10; https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework] NIST AI RMF 1.0 is a voluntary, practical framework structured around Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage and is intended to help organizations designing, developing, deploying, or using AI systems manage AI-related risk throughout the lifecycle.
- [inference; source: https://www.nist.gov/publications/artificial-intelligence-risk-management-framework-ai-rmf-10; https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework] Although not a binding banking rule, AI RMF 1.0 independently converges on the same precondition logic as the prudential and resilience frameworks: governance and context mapping must exist before deployment, so introducing agentic AI into an unmapped, weakly controlled estate is contrary to the direction of recognized AI risk-management practice.

#### Cross-framework pattern
