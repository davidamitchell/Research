# Compliance Risks of Relying on Stochastic LLM Outputs for Governance, Privacy, and Regulatory Decisions

## Research Question

What evidence or guidance exists on the compliance risks of relying primarily on stochastic LLM (Large Language Model) outputs for governance, privacy, or regulatory decisions?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Published enforcement actions, regulatory guidance, or advisory opinions where AI/LLM variability contributed to compliance failure
- Academic and industry risk assessments of LLM-based decision-making in regulated domains (financial services, healthcare, data protection)
- Specific risk categories: inconsistent outcomes across similarly situated cases, inability to audit decision paths, hallucinated regulatory citations, classification errors
- Guidance from regulators (ICO (Information Commissioner's Office), FCA (Financial Conduct Authority), OCC (Office of the Comptroller of the Currency)) on AI reliability requirements

**Out of scope:**
- General AI risk not specifically tied to compliance or regulatory obligations
- Risks from adversarial attacks on LLMs (prompt injection, jailbreaking) — these are a separate research question

**Constraints:** Evidence should be from credible sources (regulators, peer-reviewed research, documented incidents); anecdotal reports noted but not treated as primary evidence

## Context

The use of LLMs in governance workflows — classifying data sensitivity, deciding on access requests, generating compliance reports, or flagging policy violations — introduces risk if the probabilistic nature of LLM outputs is not adequately controlled. Regulators in financial services and data protection have begun issuing guidance on AI reliability, and several enforcement actions have referenced AI decision-making failures. Understanding the documented risk landscape enables architects and risk officers to make evidence-based decisions about where LLMs can and cannot be the primary decision mechanism.

## Approach

1. Search for regulatory enforcement actions and advisory opinions that reference AI/ML decision-making failures in compliance contexts
2. Review academic literature on LLM reliability in high-stakes decision-making (legal, medical, financial domains)
3. Examine regulator-published AI guidance for financial services (FCA, OCC, FINRA (Financial Industry Regulatory Authority), EBA (European Banking Authority)) and data protection (ICO, EDPB (European Data Protection Board))
4. Synthesise a risk taxonomy: type of stochastic failure → regulatory obligation violated → evidence of occurrence → available mitigations
