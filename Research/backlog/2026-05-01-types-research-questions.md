# What types of research questions exist and when is each type appropriate?

## Research Question

What are the main types of research questions (descriptive, explanatory, evaluative, comparative, and relational), how does each type drive a different research design and methodology choice, and what decision criteria should a researcher use to select the appropriate type?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The standard typology of research questions as found in research methodology literature: descriptive ("What is?"), explanatory or causal ("Why?", "What causes?"), evaluative ("How effective?"), comparative ("What is the difference between?"), and relational ("What is the relationship between?")
- The research design implications of each type: which types require experimental or quasi-experimental designs, which are suited to survey or interview-based studies, which suit systematic review
- Decision criteria for selecting a question type: what is already known, the research purpose (exploration vs. explanation vs. evaluation), and available data/methods
- How question type interacts with research paradigm: positivist vs. interpretivist traditions tend to favour different question types

**Out of scope:**
- The qualities of a well-formed question within a type — covered in `2026-05-01-qualities-effective-research-questions`
- Techniques for generating and sharpening questions — covered in `2026-05-01-developing-sharpening-research-questions`
- Aim/objective/question hierarchy — covered in `2026-05-01-number-scope-research-questions`

**Constraints:**
- Use canonical typologies from research methodology literature; note where disciplines use different naming conventions for the same type
- Distinguish between the question type and the research design it implies — they are related but not identical

## Context

Selecting the wrong question type is a structural error: a descriptive question ("What is the current state of X?") cannot be answered by an Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT), and a causal question ("Does X cause Y?") cannot be answered by a cross-sectional survey. The typology is not just a labelling exercise — it is the mechanism by which the question constrains methodology. Researchers who understand the typology can avoid designing studies that cannot answer their stated question, which is one of the most costly errors in academic and applied research.

## Approach

1. **Map the typology** — Survey canonical methodology texts for their question typology. Produce a consolidated, mutually-exclusive typology with a clear definition and example for each type.

2. **Link each type to a research design** — For each question type, identify the research design(s) that can validly answer it and those that cannot. Produce a decision matrix: question type → appropriate design → inappropriate design.

3. **Identify paradigm interactions** — Does the positivist/interpretivist divide map onto specific question types, or can any type appear in either paradigm? Review examples of each type in both traditions.

4. **Examine mixed and hybrid question sets** — Some research projects use a primary descriptive question followed by explanatory sub-questions. What are the design implications of mixing question types within a single study?

5. **Produce a decision guide** — A short decision tree: given a research purpose, what is the most appropriate question type and the minimum research design required to answer it?
