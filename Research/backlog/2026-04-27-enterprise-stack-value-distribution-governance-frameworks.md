# Enterprise data stack value-distribution frameworks: what frameworks — including the seven-layer stack and Software Repricing Matrix discussed in the April 2026 ServiceNow investment analysis — are most useful for understanding where durable value accumulates in enterprise technology stacks, especially at the governance layer?

## Research Question

What frameworks — specifically the seven-layer enterprise data stack and the Software Repricing Matrix described in the April 2026 Liam Highland Capital (LHC) ServiceNow investment analysis, together with comparable frameworks from enterprise architecture, investment analysis, and technology strategy literature — most clearly explain how durable economic value distributes across enterprise technology stacks as lower-layer resources (storage, compute, intelligence) commoditise, and what do these frameworks collectively say about governance as the layer that accumulates and compounds value that cannot be replicated by adding more compute?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The seven-layer enterprise data stack as described in the LHC video: (1) data origination, (2) storage, (3) compute, (4) semantic layer, (5) reasoning, (6) action and orchestration, (7) governance — including which vendors are cited at each layer (Snowflake/Databricks at layer 2, AWS/Azure/Google Cloud Platform (GCP) at layer 3, Palantir at layer 4, Anthropic/OpenAI/Google at layer 5, UiPath at layer 6, ServiceNow at layers 6–7)
- The Software Repricing Matrix described in the video: a four-quadrant model using (x) coordination complexity and (y) AI revenue directness to classify major software companies (AI-native infrastructure, domain defenders, disruption zone, platform transformers) and explain differential price-to-earnings (PE) multiple compression year to date
- The Lionus Capital Artificial Intelligence (AI) Threshold Effect essay framing: "durable value accrues to the constrained version — the one that wraps raw capability with permissions, audit trails, and predictable cost"; "for enterprises, control is not friction, it is the product"
- The terminal value repricing thesis: why terminal value (60–70% of enterprise value for 20% growth companies) is repriced on AI disruption uncertainty even when near-term cash flows are intact
- Comparable frameworks from other sources: Clayton Christensen's commoditisation-and-complementarity theory, Geoffrey Moore's core/context framework, technology stack value migration literature (e.g., value chain analysis applied to software layers), and any published enterprise architecture frameworks that address value layer positioning
- Frameworks used specifically to evaluate governance-as-competitive-moat, including the economic logic of switching costs, institutional knowledge compounding, and the difference between feature differentiation and cost-to-replace moats

**Out of scope:**
- Detailed financial valuation of ServiceNow (covered in the source video)
- ServiceNow's specific product roadmap and agentic AI capabilities (covered by `2026-04-27-servicenow-orchestration-agentic-ai-roadmap`)
- Connection to specific AI governance architecture frameworks in prior research (covered by `2026-04-27-governance-moat-prior-research-implications`)
- Vendor-specific competitive analysis beyond what is needed to illustrate the frameworks

**Constraints:**
- Every framework cited must be traceable to a specific named source with a URL — "industry consensus" or unnamed frameworks are not acceptable
- The LHC video is a primary source for the specific frameworks described; its framing must be accurately represented before being evaluated or extended
- Recency matters: the commoditisation of inference (99% cost reduction in 2.5 years as cited in the video) is a fast-moving factual claim — verify or contextualise with external sources

## Context

The April 2026 LHC ServiceNow investment analysis (issue #416) presents two analytical frameworks — a seven-layer enterprise data stack and a four-quadrant Software Repricing Matrix — and uses them to argue that governance is the durable value layer in AI-augmented enterprise stacks. This is directly relevant to the ongoing research programme on AI governance architecture (Universal Entity Lifecycle Governance Framework (UELGF), Policy Administration Point/Policy Decision Point/Policy Enforcement Point (PAP/PDP/PEP)), which proceeds from the assumption that governance mechanisms are worth investing in. The question this item addresses is: are there established frameworks — beyond the LHC analysis — that independently support the governance-layer value thesis, and how do they compare and compose? The answer informs both the intellectual grounding of the governance research programme and the investment/procurement decisions that flow from it.

Prior research directly relevant to this item:
- [ServiceNow Platform Strategy (completed)](https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-03-08-servicenow-platform-strategy.html) — existing ServiceNow platform research from an IT operations perspective
- [Enterprise AI platform operating models (completed)](https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-04-22-enterprise-ai-platform-operating-models.html) — organisational structure for multi-platform AI estates

## Approach

1. **Catalogue the LHC frameworks precisely:** Extract and formally specify the seven-layer stack and Software Repricing Matrix as described in the source video. For each layer/quadrant, identify: the named vendors, the stated characteristic, and the implied value-distribution logic. This ensures the primary source is accurately represented before any comparison is attempted.
2. **Locate the intellectual lineage:** Trace the intellectual antecedents of the seven-layer stack model. Is it original to the LHC analysis, or does it draw on established frameworks (Open Group Architecture Framework (TOGAF) technology stack, Zachman Framework, cloud reference architectures, AI platform reference architectures from major cloud vendors)? Similarly, trace the Software Repricing Matrix to comparable two-by-two frameworks in investment analysis literature.
3. **Commoditisation-and-complementarity theory:** Investigate Clayton Christensen's commoditisation-and-complementarity theory and Geoffrey Moore's core/context framework as potential theoretical grounding for the governance-layer value thesis. Assess whether they predict governance-layer value retention as lower layers commoditise.
4. **Governance-as-moat frameworks:** Survey the literature on switching costs, institutional knowledge compounding, and governance-as-competitive-advantage in enterprise software. Identify frameworks that specifically address the economic logic of "cannot be replicated with compute" moats vs. feature differentiation moats.
5. **Enterprise architecture value-layer frameworks:** Survey enterprise architecture frameworks (TOGAF, ArchiMate, Gartner Pace Layering, Simon Wardley Mapping) for explicit treatments of value distribution across technology layers, particularly whether governance/orchestration layers are identified as value-retention layers.
6. **AI commoditisation and value migration:** Identify published analyses of where value migrates as AI inference commoditises. Are there independent predictions that governance and orchestration layers capture more value as reasoning becomes cheap?
7. **Synthesis:** Produce a structured comparison of the frameworks, assessing: (a) which frameworks independently predict governance-layer value retention, (b) which predict disruption of orchestration/workflow layers, (c) what the key disagreements are, and (d) what empirical indicators would distinguish the competing predictions.
