# How many research questions does a project need, and how do they relate to aims and objectives?

## Research Question

How many research questions should a research project have, what is the structural relationship between research questions, research aims, and research objectives, and how does getting this structure wrong propagate errors into methodology and reporting?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Guidance from research methodology literature on the appropriate number of research questions for different project scales (dissertation, journal article, funded study)
- The definitional distinction between a research question (what the study seeks to find out), a research aim (the broad purpose of the study), and a research objective (a concrete, achievable step toward the aim)
- How the hierarchy — aims → questions → objectives — structures methodology choices: sample selection, data collection instruments, and analysis strategy
- Common mismatches: too many questions for available resources; conflation of aims with questions; questions that cannot generate testable objectives

**Out of scope:**
- The qualities of a well-formed individual question — covered in `2026-05-01-qualities-effective-research-questions`
- Question typology (descriptive, explanatory) — covered in `2026-05-01-types-research-questions`

**Constraints:**
- Ground numeric recommendations in methodology literature, not opinion
- Distinguish undergraduate, master's, doctoral, and professional-research contexts where norms differ

## Context

A persistent confusion in research design is the conflation of research aims ("to explore the relationship between X and Y"), research questions ("What is the relationship between X and Y?"), and research objectives ("To conduct semi-structured interviews with 20 practitioners in sector Z"). These three constructs are hierarchically related and serve different functions. Getting the structure right — one over-arching aim, one to three tightly scoped questions, and concrete objectives mapped to each question — is necessary before a study can be designed coherently. Over-questioning is a common failure mode: a 5,000-word dissertation cannot address six substantial research questions.

## Approach

1. **Define the three constructs** — Produce precise definitions of aims, research questions, and objectives with canonical sources. Map the hierarchy.

2. **Survey numeric guidance** — What does the literature say about how many questions are appropriate for a given project type? Are there conventions that vary by discipline (social science vs. natural science vs. engineering)?

3. **Model the mapping** — Show how a single aim, two research questions, and four objectives form a coherent, internally consistent project design. Show the failure mode where six questions produce an under-powered study.

4. **Assess the downstream effects** — How does the number and structure of research questions constrain the methodology (research design, sampling strategy, data collection, and analysis approach)?

5. **Produce guidance** — A practical decision framework: for a given project scope and word/time budget, how many research questions is the upper limit, and how should they be sequenced (sub-questions off a primary question vs. parallel questions)?
