# Adam Smith, Organisational Design, Desire Paths, and AI Strategy

## Research Question

What can Adam Smith's insights into human nature and morality — drawn from *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (ToMS) and *The Wealth of Nations* (WoN) — teach us about designing organisations that align with how people naturally behave (desire paths), and how does this intersect with AI strategy?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Adam Smith's core arguments in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (ToMS): sympathy, the impartial spectator, moral sentiments as the social fabric
- Adam Smith's core arguments in *The Wealth of Nations* (WoN): self-interest, the invisible hand, division of labour, incentive alignment
- Michael Munger's commentary on Smith via the "Two Idiots and a Podcast" (TAITC) podcast series (https://taitc.buzzsprout.com/)
- Desire paths as an organisational design concept: the gap between the formally designed path and the path people actually take
- Organisational design patterns that exploit or accommodate desire paths (reducing friction, aligning incentives, emergent norms)
- Intersection with AI strategy: how AI tools and agents may surface or amplify desire-path dynamics in knowledge work, team coordination, and governance

**Out of scope:**
- Full biographical or historical treatment of Adam Smith beyond what is directly relevant to the research question
- Macro-economic policy debates (tariffs, trade, monetary policy) that do not bear on org design or AI strategy
- Deep implementation guides for specific AI products

**Constraints:** Publicly accessible sources; primary texts (ToMS, WoN) are in the public domain; podcast episodes are accessible via Buzzsprout.

## Context

Adam Smith is most often cited for WoN and the concept of the invisible hand, but his earlier and arguably more fundamental work — ToMS — establishes a theory of moral psychology: humans are motivated by sympathy, the desire for approval, and an internalised "impartial spectator" that moderates self-interest. Together the two works offer a richer model of human behaviour than is usually attributed to Smith.

Desire paths (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desire_path) are the informal routes people create when the designed path does not match actual behaviour — a concept from urban planning that transfers directly to organisational design. Organisations that fight desire paths waste energy enforcing rules; organisations that study and incorporate them build systems people actually follow.

The connection to AI strategy is timely: AI tools introduced into organisations will either follow the formally prescribed workflows or will be used in the desire-path ways that actually reflect how people work. Understanding the Smithian underpinnings of this dynamic — self-interest, sympathy, moral legitimacy, emergent norms — may be key to deploying AI in ways that actually take hold.

## Approach

1. Survey *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (ToMS): identify the key claims about human motivation, sympathy, the impartial spectator, and how social norms emerge — the "moral architecture" of Smith's model.
2. Survey *The Wealth of Nations* (WoN): identify the claims about self-interest, incentives, division of labour, and emergent order that extend and sometimes tension with ToMS.
3. Review Michael Munger's TAITC podcast episodes on Smith: identify any novel framings, applied examples, or connections to modern institutional economics that go beyond standard readings.
4. Map the desire-path concept onto Smith's framework: where do ToMS and WoN predict desire-path emergence? What conditions cause people to deviate from the designed path?
5. Identify org-design patterns implied by this synthesis: what does building with rather than against desire paths look like in practice, grounded in Smith's model?
6. Apply the synthesis to AI strategy: in what specific ways do Smith's insights shape how AI tools should be introduced, governed, or evolved in organisations?
