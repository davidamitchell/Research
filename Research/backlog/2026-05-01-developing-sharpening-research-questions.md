# How do you develop and sharpen a research question from a broad topic using templates and systematic refinement?

## Research Question

What are the most effective systematic techniques and fill-in-the-blank templates for narrowing a broad research topic into a specific, scoped, answerable research question — and how should a researcher iterate on a draft question until it meets the quality bar?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Techniques for narrowing a broad topic: concept mapping, PICO (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome) framework (from clinical research), SPIDER (Sample, Phenomenon of Interest, Design, Evaluation, Research type) framework, and comparable structures from social science methodology
- Fill-in-the-blank question templates: "If [group] experiences [phenomenon], how do they [action/outcome] under [conditions]?" and similar structural templates
- The iterative refinement cycle: how to draft, test against quality criteria, diagnose weaknesses, and revise until the question is stable
- How to use existing literature to sharpen a question (literature gap analysis as a sharpening technique)
- Practical examples: a worked example from a broad topic to a final research question, showing each iteration

**Out of scope:**
- Quality criteria definitions — covered in `2026-05-01-qualities-effective-research-questions`
- Failure mode diagnosis — covered in `2026-05-01-common-mistakes-research-questions`
- The typology of question types — covered in `2026-05-01-types-research-questions`

**Constraints:**
- PICO stands for Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome — primarily used in clinical/health research but applicable more broadly
- SPIDER stands for Sample, Phenomenon of Interest, Design, Evaluation, Research type — used in qualitative systematic reviews
- Ground template recommendations in literature; flag templates that are domain-specific vs. generally applicable

## Context

Many researchers begin with a broad topic rather than a question. "I want to research AI and healthcare" is a topic; "What factors predict clinician adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI)-assisted diagnostic tools in emergency departments in the United Kingdom?" is a question. The gap between them is bridged by systematic narrowing techniques, not by inspiration. This item focuses specifically on that bridge: the techniques, templates, and iteration process that reliably convert a topic into a question that meets the quality bar. It is the most actionable item in the series — a practical how-to guide for researchers at the very start of a project.

## Approach

1. **Survey narrowing techniques** — Identify and document the main techniques used to move from a broad topic to a research question: concept mapping (visual narrowing), PICO/SPIDER frameworks, literature gap analysis, and question laddering (asking "why?" and "how?" iteratively until a specific question emerges).

2. **Collect and evaluate templates** — Gather fill-in-the-blank question templates from the literature, methodology courses, and practitioner guides. Evaluate each for: generality (how many question types it suits), specificity (does the template force appropriate scoping?), and usability (can a novice apply it without guidance?).

3. **Document the iteration cycle** — What is the recommended cycle for draft → evaluate → revise? How many iterations are typical before a question stabilises? What signals indicate the question is ready?

4. **Worked example** — Trace a single example from broad topic through to final question, showing each narrowing technique applied and how the question changes with each iteration.

5. **Produce a practitioner guide** — A step-by-step guide: start with a broad topic, apply narrowing technique, draft a question using a template, evaluate against quality criteria, revise, repeat.
