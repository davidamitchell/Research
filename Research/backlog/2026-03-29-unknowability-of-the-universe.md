# The Unknowability of the Universe

## Research Question

What does JB Manchuk's treatment of General Relativity (GR) and Zen Buddhism reveal about the epistemological limits of human knowledge — specifically, is the universe fundamentally unknowable, and if so, in what senses?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- JB Manchuk's arguments and writings on the unknowability of the universe
- The intersection of General Relativity and Zen Buddhism as epistemological frameworks
- Specific claims about the limits of observation, measurement, and conceptual knowledge
- Comparative analysis: where GR and Zen Buddhist thought converge or diverge on unknowability

**Out of scope:**
- Comprehensive history of Zen Buddhism or General Relativity as disciplines
- Other authors' treatments of physics–philosophy intersections unless directly relevant to Manchuk's thesis
- Practical applications of either GR or Zen practice

**Constraints:** Source availability; Manchuk may not have widely indexed publications — web search required.

## Context

The issue of whether the universe is fundamentally unknowable sits at the intersection of physics and contemplative philosophy. General Relativity (GR) introduces horizons (event horizons, cosmological horizons) that place hard physical limits on what any observer can access. Zen Buddhism has long engaged with the limits of conceptual thought and the inadequacy of language to capture reality. JB Manchuk appears to draw these two traditions together to argue for a structured account of cosmic unknowability. Understanding this argument matters for anyone thinking about the foundations of knowledge and the relationship between scientific and contemplative epistemology.

## Approach

1. Locate JB Manchuk's primary source material (books, papers, lectures, interviews) on this topic.
2. Identify the specific claims Manchuk makes about GR and its epistemic limits.
3. Identify the specific Zen Buddhist concepts Manchuk deploys and how they map onto GR concepts.
4. Analyse how the two frameworks are synthesised into an account of unknowability.
5. Check for peer responses, critiques, or corroborating work.
