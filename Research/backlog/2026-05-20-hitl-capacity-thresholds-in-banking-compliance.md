# At what threshold does Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) oversight collapse into automation rubber-stamping in bank compliance operations?

## Research Question

What measurable workload, alert-volume, and staffing thresholds indicate that Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) compliance review is no longer a meaningful challenge function, and which operational controls sustain reviewer vigilance when Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems perform most first-pass filtering?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Quantitative and behavioural indicators of oversight degradation in high-volume review queues.
- Capacity, rest-cycle, and failure-injection mechanisms that preserve reviewer challenge capability.
- Auditability of challenge and veto capacity under model risk governance expectations.
- Mapping to Federal Reserve model risk management expectations and similar supervisory guidance.

**Out of scope:**
- Fully autonomous decisioning with no human review requirement.
- Workforce policy outside operational oversight design.

**Constraints:** Seek evidence that supports threshold-setting and testable governance controls, not only qualitative commentary.

## Context

If review teams cannot realistically challenge AI outputs under production load, Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) becomes performative rather than protective, creating both regulatory and operational risk.

## Approach

1. Identify empirical research on alert fatigue, automation bias, and reviewer throughput limits.
2. Define metrics for meaningful challenge capacity (review depth, overturn rates, time-on-task, escalation quality).
3. Assess intervention designs such as rest cycles, random challenge sampling, and simulated failure injections.
4. Derive an audit-ready framework for testing and evidencing effective human challenge capacity.
