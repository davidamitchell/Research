# How should banks break the syntactic confidence trap in Artificial Intelligence (AI)-generated compliance narratives?

## Research Question

How does authoritative prose from generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems change reviewer behaviour in anti-money laundering (AML) and Know Your Customer (KYC) processes, and what interface and evidence controls are required so humans do not mistake fluent output for verified truth?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Behavioural effects of polished AI narrative output on compliance analysts.
- User interface indicators that expose uncertainty and provenance (confidence markers, citation traces, evidence completeness indicators).
- Regulatory implications under European Union (EU) AI Act Article 14 and relevant financial-crime compliance expectations.
- Liability allocation risks when analysts adopt unsupported AI summaries.

**Out of scope:**
- Broader anti-money laundering (AML) policy design unrelated to AI assistance.
- General-purpose writing assistants outside regulated compliance workflows.

**Constraints:** Focus on controls that can be audited and embedded into production compliance tooling.

## Context

Regulated teams need concrete design patterns that prevent syntactic confidence from bypassing evidentiary checks, because fluent but unsupported summaries can distort escalation and approval decisions.

## Approach

1. Review empirical evidence on tone, confidence, and truth-perception effects in AI-assisted review.
2. Map required transparency and oversight obligations to concrete user-interface and workflow controls.
3. Analyse legal and operational accountability implications when analysts rely on unsupported AI output.
4. Produce an auditable control checklist for banking compliance implementations.
