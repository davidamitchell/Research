# To what degree does over-reliance on agentic AI tools accelerate measurable skill decay in practitioners, and what interventions best preserve human capability without sacrificing productivity gains?

## Research Question

To what degree and through what mechanisms does over-reliance on agentic Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools—particularly when encouraged by productivity mandates—accelerate measurable skill decay in verification, judgment, and domain expertise among practitioners? How does the "AI for speed" paradigm affect junior versus senior practitioners differently, and what interventions—deliberate practice protocols, hybrid apprenticeship models, mandatory human challenge thresholds, and skill audits—best preserve human oversight competence in agentic environments without sacrificing short-term efficiency gains?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Empirical evidence and longitudinal studies on AI-induced skill decay or deskilling in knowledge work, software engineering, and regulated professional domains
- Proxy metrics and measurement approaches for skill decay where longitudinal data is unavailable (e.g., fallback performance during AI outages, error detection rates without AI assistance)
- Differential impact of AI tool adoption on junior versus senior practitioners, including apprenticeship and knowledge transfer implications
- Intervention designs that demonstrably preserve human oversight competence: deliberate practice, mandatory challenge thresholds, competency audits, hybrid training
- Analogous evidence from automation-induced deskilling in adjacent domains (aviation, radiology, financial services)

**Out of scope:**
- AI model capability improvement or training methodology
- Consumer-tier AI use outside enterprise or professional contexts
- Workforce planning or headcount optimisation
- Macro-level labour market displacement effects

**Constraints:**
- Flag inferences clearly; longitudinal causal evidence is likely sparse and must be distinguished from cross-sectional correlation
- Expand all acronyms on first use
- Include analogous automation-deskilling evidence where direct AI evidence is limited

## Context

Existing repository research has identified skill decay as one of four interacting enterprise failure modes in AI governance, and established that human oversight quality degrades under volume pressure. [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-02-incentive-misalignment-shadow-ai-skill-decay-controls.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-02-hitl-review-volume-bottleneck-rubber-stamp.html]

The remaining gap is specific empirical evidence on the rate and mechanisms of AI-induced skill decay, measurable proxies that organisations can track, the differential effect across seniority levels, and what intervention designs have been validated rather than merely proposed. [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-01-human-oversight-ai-software-development.html]

## Approach

1. **Sub-question 1 — Degree and mechanisms of skill decay:** What is the empirical evidence for AI-induced skill decay in knowledge work and software engineering? Are there longitudinal studies or controlled experiments? What proxy metrics (fallback performance, error detection, competency audit scores) are used where longitudinal data is absent?

2. **Sub-question 2 — Differential impact by seniority:** How does the "AI for speed" paradigm affect junior practitioners differently from senior ones? What does evidence from cognitive apprenticeship, expert-novice skill acquisition, and analogous automation transitions (aviation automation, radiology Computer-Aided Detection (CAD)) say about differential decay?

3. **Sub-question 3 — Effective interventions:** What deliberate practice protocols, mandatory challenge thresholds, hybrid apprenticeship models, or skill audit designs have been empirically tested for preserving human competence in AI-augmented environments? What is the evidence for each?

## Sources

Starting points:

- [ ] [Bainbridge (1983) Ironies of automation](https://doi.org/10.1016/0005-1098(83)90046-8) — seminal paper on how automation erodes operator skill and situation awareness; foundational theory for AI-deskilling claims
- [ ] [Parasuraman & Riley (1997) Humans and automation: Use, misuse, disuse, abuse](https://doi.org/10.1177/001872089703900402) — foundational framework for automation-induced skill degradation and complacency
- [ ] [Carr (2014) The Glass Cage: Automation and Us](https://www.amazon.com/Glass-Cage-Automation-Nicholas-Carr/dp/0393240762) — book-length treatment of automation-induced deskilling with domain examples
- [ ] [Cabitza et al. (2017) Unintended Consequences of Machine Learning in Medicine](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2017.7797) — evidence on skill decay and automation reliance in clinical decision support
- [ ] [Microsoft Research (2023) The Impact of AI on Developer Productivity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590) — short-term productivity evidence that explicitly omits long-term skill measurement
