# Why Do Trust-Based Institutions Outperform Incentive Schemes for Knowledge Sharing?

## Research Question

Why do explicit transactional incentives for sharing often decay or backfire, while trust-based relational institutions such as psychological safety sustain lower long-run knowledge-sharing costs?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Comparative effects of extrinsic incentives versus relational norms on knowledge sharing.
- Opportunism, gaming, and motivation-crowding risks in incentive-led schemes.
- Mechanisms through which trust reduces repeat interaction costs.

**Out of scope:**
- Compensation benchmarking and salary-market analysis.
- General employee-engagement interventions unrelated to knowledge transfer.

**Constraints:** Focus on evidence from organisational psychology, institutional economics, and field studies in workplace settings.

## Context

Many organisations still rely on point-based or bonus-based sharing programmes that produce short-lived compliance. A durable model requires understanding when and why trust-based institutions outperform transactional exchange logic.

## Approach

1. Compare short-run and long-run outcomes of incentive-led versus trust-led sharing systems.
2. Identify failure modes of explicit incentives, including opportunism and signalling distortions.
3. Define institutional conditions that make trust effects durable and scalable.

## Sources

- [ ] [Gneezy, U. & Rustichini, A. (2000) Pay Enough or Don't Pay at All](https://doi.org/10.1162/003355300554917) — evidence on motivation crowding and incentive backfire effects.
- [ ] [Edmondson, A. C. (1999) Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams](https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999) — foundational evidence on trust and learning behaviour in teams.
