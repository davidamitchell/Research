# What qualities make a research question effective and answerable?

## Research Question

What are the defining qualities (specificity, researchability, scope, and manageability) that distinguish an effective, answerable research question from a vague or unresearchable one, and how do these qualities apply across qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-methods research contexts?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The core qualities most frequently cited in research methodology literature: specific, answerable, scoped, motivated, and decomposable
- How these qualities differ or are weighted differently across qualitative (interpretive), quantitative (empirical), and mixed-methods research designs
- Practical tests a researcher can apply to evaluate whether their question meets the quality bar
- The relationship between a research question and the research design choices it constrains

**Out of scope:**
- Detailed typology of question types (descriptive, explanatory, evaluative) — covered in `2026-05-01-types-research-questions`
- Question-generation techniques and templates — covered in `2026-05-01-developing-sharpening-research-questions`
- Common formulation mistakes — covered in `2026-05-01-common-mistakes-research-questions`

**Constraints:**
- Ground claims in published research methodology literature (Creswell, Crotty, Bryman, or similar canonical sources)
- Distinguish general qualities from domain-specific conventions (e.g., scientific hypothesis vs. interpretive question)

## Context

The quality of a research question is the single most important determinant of whether a research project can succeed. A weak question — too broad, too vague, or unanswerable within constraints — propagates errors downstream into methodology, data collection, and conclusions. This item focuses on the core quality criteria so researchers can self-evaluate before investing in a full study design. It is the foundational question from which the other items in this series derive.

## Approach

1. **Identify the core quality criteria** — Survey canonical research methodology texts (Creswell, Bryman, Punch) for the most consistently cited qualities. Produce a consolidated, ranked list.

2. **Differentiate by research paradigm** — Assess how quality criteria shift between quantitative (testable, falsifiable), qualitative (open-ended, exploratory), and mixed-methods designs. Identify criteria that are universal vs. paradigm-specific.

3. **Operationalise each criterion** — For each quality, identify a practical test the researcher can apply: a yes/no checklist question or a diagnostic prompt.

4. **Examine edge cases** — Are there legitimate research questions that violate one or more criteria? (e.g., deliberately broad scoping questions in exploratory research; action research questions oriented toward practice change rather than knowledge production)

5. **Synthesise a quality checklist** — Produce a practical self-evaluation rubric that a researcher can apply to any candidate question.
