# How do errors compound in AI-agent-heavy codebases, and what review strategies can manage this risk?

## Research Question

How do errors ("boooos") compound in codebases developed with high volumes of AI agent-generated code — including how local patches cause global regressions — and what review and governance strategies can reliably detect and limit this compounding effect?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The compounding error hypothesis: what it means for errors to compound with "serial learning and no bottlenecks" (no human review gate)
- Evidence for error compounding in AI-generated code: studies, post-mortems, empirical measurements of defect density in AI-assisted vs. human-only development
- Local patch / global regression problem: how AI agents apply context-local fixes that break globally (especially in large codebases that exceed context windows)
- Review strategies: human code review, AI-assisted review (and its documented limitations), test coverage as a proxy, static analysis
- The "agent wrote the tests" problem: when the test suite itself is AI-generated, what reliability can it provide?
- Abstractions, duplication, and backwards compatibility: how AI agents inherit and reproduce structural complexity from training data

**Out of scope:**
- AI model training data quality (root cause is acknowledged; focus is on consequences and mitigations)
- Full treatment of specific refactoring methodologies
- Security vulnerabilities as a separate topic (covered in other items)

**Constraints:**
- Distinguish anecdote and practitioner opinion from controlled studies
- Flag where effect sizes are estimated rather than measured

## Context

At a 2026 developer conference, Mario (Pi coding agent creator) argued that AI coding agents compound errors ("boooos") because they apply local fixes without global understanding, especially when the codebase exceeds context window size. He observed that agents inherit complexity patterns from internet training data (90% of which he characterised as "garbage to mediocre") and introduce abstractions, duplication, and backwards compatibility layers that make the codebase progressively harder to reason about. He coined "enterprise-grade complexity within two weeks with two humans and ten agents." Critically, review agents do not reliably catch these errors because they share the same learned complexity patterns. The question is: what does evidence actually say about these dynamics, and what mitigation strategies work?

## Approach

1. **Evidence for error compounding** — What empirical research exists on defect density in AI-assisted vs. human-only codebases? What do post-mortems of AI-heavy development projects reveal?
2. **Local-global regression dynamics** — Specifically, is there evidence that AI patches at the local (function/file) level more frequently introduce global regressions than human patches? What mechanisms explain this (context window limits, lack of architectural understanding)?
3. **Reliability of AI-generated tests** — What does research show about the coverage and reliability of test suites generated by AI agents? Can they substitute for human-written tests as a correctness signal?
4. **Review strategy effectiveness** — Survey the evidence for: human code review effectiveness, AI-assisted code review effectiveness, static analysis, and property-based testing as defect detection mechanisms in AI-generated code.
5. **Governance recommendations** — Synthesise the above into concrete governance recommendations: at what throughput levels, and for what code criticality levels, does each review strategy provide adequate assurance?
