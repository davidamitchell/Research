# How do coupled enterprise risks—capability debt, incentive-driven shadow AI, skill decay, and oversight failure—manifest differently in agentic versus generative AI deployments, and what integrated risk frameworks best predict cascading failures?

## Research Question

How do the coupled enterprise risks—capability debt, incentive-driven shadow Artificial Intelligence (AI) adoption, skill decay, and oversight failure—manifest differently in agentic versus generative AI deployments, and what integrated risk frameworks best predict and prevent cascading failures? What are the long-term organisational impacts of prioritising measurable speed over unmeasured quality in AI tool adoption, and how can enterprises empirically test "AI for risk reduction first" strategies that address debt and incentives before scaling autonomous agents?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Comparative analysis of how the four coupled risk modes (capability debt, shadow AI, skill decay, oversight failure) manifest distinctively in agentic versus generative AI contexts—specifically focusing on the higher autonomy, longer action chains, and reduced friction of agentic systems
- Integrated risk frameworks and systems models that treat these four risks as a feedback loop rather than independent variables
- Long-term organisational impacts of speed-over-quality prioritisation: innovation capacity, incident response competence, regulatory compliance trajectory
- Empirical or theoretical frameworks for testing "AI for risk reduction first" strategies, including what Return on Investment (ROI) or risk-reduction evidence such strategies produce
- Case studies or natural experiments where AI adoption preceded capability debt reduction, and where it followed

**Out of scope:**
- Individual risk factor analysis already covered in companion items (this item focuses on integration and interaction effects)
- Consumer-tier AI product adoption outside enterprise contexts
- Macro-level economic or labour market analysis
- AI safety alignment in the machine learning (ML) training sense

**Constraints:**
- This item is downstream of the five companion items and should be researched after they complete (reflected in the `blocks:` field)
- Distinguish agentic AI (autonomous multi-step action chains) from generative AI (single-turn content generation) clearly throughout
- Ground all claims in observable enterprise patterns; flag inferences
- Expand all acronyms on first use

## Context

Existing repository research has established the four risk modes individually and the foundational governance design for their interaction. [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-02-incentive-misalignment-shadow-ai-skill-decay-controls.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-04-26-systems-capability-debt-agentic-ai-risk-synthesis.html]

The remaining gap is an integrated analysis of how these risks compose into cascading failure patterns that are specific to agentic systems—where autonomy, speed, and reduced human friction change the failure mode dynamics—and what evidence-based strategies enterprises can use to sequence AI adoption to reduce rather than amplify their risk exposure. [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-02-hitl-review-volume-bottleneck-rubber-stamp.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-07-five-eyes-ai-risks-and-advice.html]

## Approach

1. **Sub-question 1 — Agentic versus generative risk differentiation:** How do capability debt, shadow AI, skill decay, and oversight failure manifest differently when AI operates as a multi-step autonomous agent rather than a single-turn generative assistant? What unique failure modes arise from longer action chains, tool use, and reduced human friction?

2. **Sub-question 2 — Integrated risk frameworks and cascading failure prediction:** What systems models or integrated risk frameworks have been proposed for treating the four risks as a coupled feedback loop? Which frameworks have empirical support for predicting cascading failures rather than individual risk factors?

3. **Sub-question 3 — Long-term organisational impact of speed-over-quality prioritisation:** What evidence exists on the long-term effects of prioritising speed over quality in AI tool adoption—specifically on innovation capacity, incident response competence, and regulatory compliance posture?

4. **Sub-question 4 — "AI for risk reduction first" strategy testing:** How can enterprises empirically test a sequenced strategy that addresses capability debt, incentive alignment, and skill preservation before scaling autonomous agents? What ROI or risk-reduction evidence has emerged from early adopters of this approach?
