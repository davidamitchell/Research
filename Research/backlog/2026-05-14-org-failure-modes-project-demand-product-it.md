# Organisational failure modes: project-based demand with product-based IT teams

## Research Question

What failure modes have been empirically observed in organisations where demand is managed through a project-based model while IT teams are structured and operated as product teams?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Empirically documented failure modes arising from the mismatch between project-based demand governance (fixed scope, fixed budget, defined end date) and product-team operating models (persistent teams, continuous delivery, outcome-focused)
- Literature and case studies on dual funding tensions: project budgets cutting across product team capacity
- Observable symptoms: funding gaps, priority conflicts, team instability, erosion of product ownership
- Evidence from organisations in transition from project to product delivery
- Frameworks such as the Project-to-Product transition (Mik Kersten's Flow Framework) as analytical lenses

**Out of scope:**
- Pure theoretical arguments without supporting empirical or case-study evidence
- Failure modes unrelated to the project-vs-product structural mismatch
- Prescriptive transformation roadmaps without evidence of effectiveness

**Constraints:**
- Prefer empirical sources (surveys, case studies, retrospectives) over vendor white papers
- Every source must include a verifiable URL
- Acronyms expanded on first use throughout

## Context

A common transition state in IT organisations is operating product teams under project-based funding and governance. Business sponsors raise projects with defined outcomes and budgets; those projects draw on product teams that are designed for continuous, outcome-driven operation. The mismatch creates systemic friction: product teams must context-switch between projects, knowledge silos form, and teams are dissolved when project funding ends. Understanding these failure modes informs decisions about how to restructure demand governance during a project-to-product transformation.

## Approach

1. Define the key distinguishing characteristics of project-based and product-based demand management, drawing on Flow Framework and Team Topologies (TT) literature.
2. Search for empirical evidence — industry surveys, case studies, practitioner retrospectives — of failure modes that emerge specifically from operating product teams under project-based funding or governance.
3. Catalogue recurring failure modes (e.g., team instability, misaligned incentives, knowledge loss at project close, poor backlog continuity).
4. Examine whether the severity of failure modes varies by industry, organisation size, or degree of Agile/DevOps adoption.
5. Identify empirically supported approaches for managing the transition period and mitigating failure.

## Sources

- [ ] [Kersten (2018) Project to Product: How to Survive and Thrive in the Age of Digital Disruption with the Flow Framework](https://projecttoproduct.org/) — foundational argument for product-centric IT funding and the Flow Framework
- [ ] [Skelton and Pais (2019) Team Topologies: Organising Business and Technology Teams for Fast Flow](https://teamtopologies.com/book) — team interaction modes and fast-flow design
- [ ] [Forsgren et al. (2018) Accelerate: The Science of Lean Software and DevOps](https://itrevolution.com/accelerate-book/) — empirical evidence linking organisational structure and funding model to delivery performance
- [ ] [Project Management Institute (2023) Pulse of the Profession Survey](https://www.pmi.org/learning/thought-leadership/pulse) — annual empirical data on project success rates and failure drivers
