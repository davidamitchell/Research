# Customer contact as strategic signal: why people call and whether they want self-service

## Research Question

When customers contact a business, what are they actually seeking — and how should organisations decide between self-service automation and human interaction to maximise long-term customer value?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The reasons customers choose to contact a business (distress, complexity, relationship, opportunity)
- Whether customers want self-service or human contact, and under what conditions
- Customer delight, surprise, and discretionary generosity as retention drivers
- The Amazon "call me back" feature as an undercopied design pattern
- AI (artificial intelligence) in customer contact: co-pilot hybrid models vs. full replacement
- Octopus Energy and Amazon as case studies in customer service as competitive advantage

**Out of scope:**
- Deep technical design of contact centre platforms
- IVR (interactive voice response) system implementation detail

**Constraints:** Focus on the strategic and psychological dimensions; technical recommendations only where they directly illustrate a strategic point.

## Context

Rory Sutherland makes a provocative argument in his Business Leader interview: James Dyson reframed his call centre entirely — "if one of our customers chooses to telephone us, we should treat it as an honour and respond accordingly." Sutherland extends this to argue that the best call centre staff could justify six-figure salaries if their actual contribution to retention and conversion were properly measured.

The standard efficiency view treats customer contact as a cost to minimise: reduce call volume, push customers to self-service, automate what can be automated. Sutherland's counter-argument is that contact is signal — the customer chose to reach out, which means this is a high-stakes relationship moment. Handling it poorly destroys disproportionate value; handling it unexpectedly well creates disproportionate loyalty.

The Amazon "call me back" button is his sharpest empirical example: Amazon tests everything; it has had that feature for years; no one has copied it. The most likely reason is that organisations cannot easily prove the business case for something that works through customer perception and long-term retention rather than direct conversion in the session.

This research item connects directly to the AI adoption question: as AI is deployed to reduce headcount in contact centres, are organisations measuring what they're losing? Do customers actually want self-service, or do they want fast, competent, human resolution?

## Approach

1. Survey research on why customers choose to contact businesses (intent segmentation: distress, complexity, relationship, opportunity)
2. Review evidence on customer preference for self-service vs. human contact by context
3. Research the Amazon "call me back" design pattern and why adoption is low industry-wide
4. Examine Octopus Energy's customer service model (bonus on satisfaction, not call time)
5. Review *Unreasonable Hospitality* by Will Guidara for the customer delight and surprise-and-delight thesis
6. Analyse the Double Tree Hotels cookie example and analogous patterns as evidence for discretionary generosity ROI (return on investment)
7. Assess hybrid AI co-pilot models (e.g., VoiceStack) vs. full automation for customer contact
