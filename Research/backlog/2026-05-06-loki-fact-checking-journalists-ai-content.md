# What does Loki's human-in-the-loop claim verification approach offer that the `research-review-prompt.md` LLM-judge lacks, and how could it improve evidence traceability in research item review?

## Research Question

The `research-review-prompt.md` LLM (Large Language Model) judge assesses research items but cannot retrieve live evidence or provide per-claim rationale chains. Loki is an MIT (Massachusetts Institute of Technology)-licensed fact-checking tool built for journalists and content moderators, emphasising explainability and human oversight at each verification step. What specific capabilities does Loki provide — particularly its checkworthiness scoring and evidence traceability features — that the current LLM-judge approach lacks, and how applicable are they to improving research item review in this repository?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Comparison with the citation-discipline and speculation-control checks already in `research-review-prompt.md`: where Loki adds capability, where it is redundant
- Loki's explainability and human-in-the-loop features: do these address gaps in the current LLM-judge approach?
- Loki's design philosophy, intended user base (journalists, content moderators), and core claim-verification methodology
- Technical architecture: claim detection, evidence retrieval, verdict generation, and explainability features
- Published evaluation results and accuracy benchmarks
- Practical integration options: standalone CLI, API, GitHub Actions, or library embedding

**Out of scope:**
- General journalism fact-checking workflows (in scope only as context for tool design)
- Political disinformation detection use cases unless directly applicable to research quality
- Building or fine-tuning a new Loki-style model

**Constraints:**
- Primary sources required: official paper(s), GitHub repository, and any official documentation
- MIT licence must be confirmed before any integration recommendation
- Accuracy figures must be drawn from reproducible evaluations, not vendor claims

## Context

The research items in this repository are generated by Large Language Model (LLM) agents and then passed through an automated research review that applies citation-discipline, speculation-control, and remove-AI-slop checks. Loki is described as an MIT-licensed tool optimised for journalists and content moderators — a practitioner-facing audience with a high bar for claim accuracy and source traceability. Understanding how Loki approaches fact verification (particularly its emphasis on explainability and human oversight) is directly relevant to two open questions in this repository: (1) whether automated fact-checking tools can run inline in the research review pipeline, and (2) whether explainability features can help identify which specific claims in a completed research item require manual verification.

## Approach

1. **Gap analysis against current review process:** What does Loki provide that the `research-review-prompt.md` LLM judge cannot? Focus specifically on live evidence retrieval and per-claim rationale chains.

2. **Tool identity:** What exactly is Loki — is it a single open-source project, a platform, or a research prototype? What is its current maintenance status and who maintains it?

3. **Technical design:** How does Loki decompose and verify claims? What evidence sources does it query? What verdict labels does it produce, and are they calibrated?

4. **Accuracy and benchmarks:** What published accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 figures exist for Loki on standard fact-checking datasets? How does it perform on technical or scientific claims versus news claims?

5. **Integration and licensing:** Can Loki run in a GitHub Actions environment? Are there dependency or compute constraints? Does the MIT licence permit embedding in this repository's automated workflows?
