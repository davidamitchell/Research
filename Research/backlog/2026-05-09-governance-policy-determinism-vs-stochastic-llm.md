# Governance Policy Application: Deterministic Requirements vs Stochastic LLM Elements

## Research Question

To what extent must governance policy application be deterministic (consistent, reproducible, auditable) versus allowing stochastic/probabilistic elements when AI/LLMs (Large Language Models) are involved?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Regulatory and standards-based requirements for consistency, reproducibility, and auditability in policy enforcement
- Technical analysis of where non-determinism in LLM outputs conflicts with governance obligations
- Published frameworks, guidance, or case studies on acceptable tolerance for probabilistic outputs in regulated contexts
- Risk classification approaches for deciding which decisions require determinism

**Out of scope:**
- Pure ML research on stochasticity — focus is on governance and compliance implications
- Non-AI determinism requirements (e.g., traditional rule-based systems) except as baseline comparators

**Constraints:** Prioritise sources that bridge regulatory requirements with AI/ML system properties

## Context

Governance frameworks — both internal policies and external regulations — typically assume that the same inputs will produce consistent outputs over time. Auditability and accountability depend on this reproducibility. LLMs are inherently probabilistic: the same prompt can yield different responses across runs, model versions, or infrastructure configurations. This tension is a fundamental design challenge for any organisation seeking to use LLMs in policy-adjacent workflows. The question of *how much* determinism is required, and *for which decisions*, determines the architectural and operational constraints on AI-assisted governance systems.

## Approach

1. Identify the specific determinism requirements in major governance frameworks (GDPR Article 5(1)(f), NIST AI RMF Govern function, ISO/IEC 38505)
2. Review academic and industry literature on probabilistic AI outputs vs regulatory compliance
3. Examine how existing audit and compliance frameworks (SOC 2, ISO 27001) handle system outputs that are not fully reproducible
4. Synthesise a decision framework for classifying which governance decisions require strict determinism and which can tolerate controlled stochasticity
