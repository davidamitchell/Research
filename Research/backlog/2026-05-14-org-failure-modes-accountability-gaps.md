# Organisational failure modes: overlapping and absent accountability at strategic and IT layers

## Research Question

What failure modes have been empirically observed in organisations where accountability is either overlapping (two or more parties hold the same accountability) or absent (no party owns a given area), at the strategic leadership level, the IT delivery layer, or both simultaneously?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Empirically documented failure modes arising from duplicate or missing accountability in IT governance contexts
- Both strategic-layer manifestations (e.g., two senior leaders owning the same portfolio outcome) and IT-layer manifestations (e.g., no single team owning a shared service)
- Combined cases where gaps exist at both layers concurrently
- Observable symptoms: decision paralysis, conflicting priorities, finger-pointing, unowned technical debt, scope creep, initiative abandonment
- Governance models (RACI — Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed; DCI — Decision Rights, Consultation, Information; DACI — Driver, Approver, Contributor, Informed) as analytical lenses for accountability structure

**Out of scope:**
- Individual leadership capability failures unrelated to structural accountability design
- Failure modes in organisations with clear, non-overlapping accountability structures
- Detailed prescriptive RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) redesign blueprints without empirical grounding

**Constraints:**
- Prefer primary empirical sources (case studies, post-mortems, governance audits, surveys) over consulting opinion
- Every source must include a verifiable URL
- Acronyms expanded on first use throughout

## Context

Many organisations operate with accountability structures that have accreted over time — through mergers, reorgs, and incremental mandate changes — rather than being deliberately designed. The result is often both gaps (where no one is accountable for an outcome) and overlaps (where multiple parties believe they own the same outcome). Both conditions produce well-known pathologies — but the combination of strategic-layer and IT-layer misalignment is particularly damaging because it means neither the framing of a problem nor its execution has a clear owner. Understanding the empirical failure modes informs how to audit and repair accountability structures during organisational transformation.

## Approach

1. Define "overlapping accountability" and "accountability gap" precisely, distinguishing strategic-level and IT-delivery-level manifestations.
2. Search for empirical evidence of failure modes — governance reviews, audit reports, industry research, case studies — that are directly attributable to these structural conditions.
3. Identify the most frequently recurring failure modes for each condition and layer combination.
4. Examine whether failure mode severity differs when gaps/overlaps exist at only one layer versus both simultaneously.
5. Review evidence on governance interventions (e.g., RACI redesign, ownership registries, decision rights frameworks) and their effectiveness in resolving these conditions.

## Sources

- [ ] [Weill and Ross (2004) IT Governance: How Top Performers Manage IT Decision Rights for Superior Results](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=20375) — foundational empirical research on IT decision-rights structures and their relationship to performance
- [ ] [ISACA (2018) COBIT 2019 Framework: Governance and Management Objectives](https://www.isaca.org/resources/cobit) — practitioner framework for structuring IT governance accountability
- [ ] [Skelton and Pais (2019) Team Topologies: Organising Business and Technology Teams for Fast Flow](https://teamtopologies.com/book) — team ownership patterns and the role of clear team accountability boundaries
