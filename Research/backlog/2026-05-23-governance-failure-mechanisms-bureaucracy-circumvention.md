# Failure mechanisms of internal governance controls: bureaucratic inefficiency and informal circumvention in regulated enterprises

## Research Question

Through what mechanisms do internal governance controls in regulated enterprises transition from coordination cost minimisers to sources of bureaucratic inefficiency and informal circumvention, and what observable signals indicate each failure mode is active?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Mechanisms by which well-designed controls degrade: ossification, scope creep, incentive misalignment, and measurability substitution (Goodhart's Law)
- Informal circumvention patterns: workarounds, shadow processes, compliance theatre, and the conditions under which they emerge
- Oliver Williamson's opportunism concept applied to internal agents gaming governance controls
- Douglass North's concept of informal norms competing with formal rules when formal rules are perceived as illegitimate or excessively costly
- Empirical evidence — regulatory post-mortems, case studies, surveys — of governance failure modes in regulated enterprises

**Out of scope:**
- External regulatory failure (focus is on internally-designed controls, not regulator-mandated requirements)
- Cybersecurity controls and information technology-specific governance failures (unless illustrative of general mechanisms)
- Individual-level psychology of rule-breaking (treated as a boundary condition, not the primary object of study)

**Constraints:** Evidence from regulated sectors preferred. Theoretical frameworks from institutional economics, public choice theory, and organisational sociology included as explanatory lenses. Time horizon: 1990–present.

## Context

Governance controls in regulated enterprises are designed to minimise coordination costs and enforce accountability, yet practitioners widely observe that they frequently become the friction they were created to prevent. Compliance functions grow, approval chains lengthen, and front-line staff develop workarounds that render controls notional. Understanding the specific mechanisms through which this transition happens — and the early warning signals — is essential for governance designers and regulators who need to distinguish genuine control effectiveness from compliance theatre. This item is motivated by Ronald Coase's observation that administrative hierarchies are only efficient up to the point where internal coordination cost equals external market cost, and North's account of how formal rules lose legitimacy and are supplanted by informal norms.

## Approach

1. Identify and classify failure mechanism archetypes: (a) bureaucratic ossification, (b) Goodhart's Law and measurability substitution, (c) scope and complexity creep, (d) incentive misalignment between governance designers and governed parties, (e) information asymmetry exploitation by internal agents.
2. For each archetype, trace the causal mechanism using Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) and institutional economics: which transaction attributes, institutional conditions, or incentive structures trigger the failure?
3. Identify the informal circumvention patterns that correspond to each failure archetype: what workarounds emerge, how are they rationalised, and how do they propagate through organisations?
4. Review empirical evidence — regulatory post-mortems (bank failures, financial misconduct cases), organisational case studies — for documented instances of each mechanism.
5. Synthesise observable early warning signals for each failure mode that governance designers and regulators can use to detect deterioration before it becomes systemic.

## Sources

- [ ] [Goodhart (1984) Problems of Monetary Management: The UK Experience](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-349-17295-5_1) — foundational statement of Goodhart's Law; measurability substitution as a governance failure mechanism
- [ ] [Power (1997) The Audit Society: Rituals of Verification](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-audit-society-9780198289432) — compliance theatre and the social construction of audit legitimacy in regulated organisations
- [ ] [Crozier (1964) The Bureaucratic Phenomenon](https://archive.org/details/bureaucraticphen0000croz) — sociological account of bureaucratic dysfunction; vicious circles of control and informal circumvention
- [ ] [UK Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (2013) Changing Banking for Good](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/jt201314/jtselect/jtpcbs/27/2702.htm) — post-mortem evidence on governance failure mechanisms in UK regulated banking
