# Barriers to governance reform, leadership failure modes, and reform mechanisms in regulated enterprises

## Research Question

What institutional and organisational barriers prevent effective governance reform in regulated enterprises, through what leadership failure modes are dysfunctional controls perpetuated, and by what mechanisms have successful reforms been achieved?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Institutional barriers to governance change: path dependence (North), sunk costs in existing governance infrastructure, and regulatory lock-in
- Organisational barriers: principal-agent (PA) problems between boards and executives, executives and governance functions, and governance functions and front-line staff
- Leadership failure modes: diffusion of accountability, governance theatre as risk transfer rather than risk reduction, short-tenure incentive misalignment, and regulatory capture at board level
- Mechanisms of successful reform: structured simplification, outcome-based governance, regulatory dialogue, and governance assurance frameworks
- Empirical evidence from regulated enterprises that have reformed governance and the conditions that made reform possible

**Out of scope:**
- Regulatory reform (focus is on internal governance, not changes to external regulatory frameworks)
- Change management methodology as a general discipline (only where directly applied to governance reform)
- Technology-specific governance reforms (unless illustrative of broader institutional mechanisms)

**Constraints:** Prefer evidence from regulated enterprises in financial services, healthcare, and energy. Include public-sector cases where they exhibit regulated-enterprise characteristics (accountability structures, compliance obligations, audit regimes).

## Context

Governance dysfunction in regulated enterprises is widely documented but rarely reformed. The gap between recognising that governance is broken and successfully fixing it is large and poorly understood. Douglass North's framework predicts that institutional path dependence makes formal rule change difficult even when actors recognise that rules are inefficient; Oliver Williamson's framework predicts that governance reforms will be resisted where they threaten existing bilateral monopolies of information and control. Understanding the failure modes of leadership that allow known governance problems to persist — and the mechanisms through which successful reforms have been achieved — is essential for regulators, boards, and executives tasked with improving governance effectiveness without triggering the very compliance burden they seek to reduce. This item is downstream of the two companion backlog items on governance effectiveness conditions and failure mechanisms.

## Approach

1. Identify and classify institutional barriers to governance reform: (a) path dependence and sunk costs in existing governance structures, (b) regulatory lock-in and regulator risk-aversion, (c) information asymmetry between governance designers and governed parties, (d) vested interests in existing control and assurance structures.
2. Map leadership failure modes that perpetuate governance dysfunction: (a) accountability diffusion across committee structures, (b) short-tenure incentive misalignment, (c) governance theatre as regulatory risk transfer, (d) regulatory capture at board or governance committee level, (e) failure to distinguish compliance cost from control effectiveness.
3. Identify the principal-agent (PA) problems that sustain each failure mode: who holds the information, who bears the cost, and who benefits from the status quo?
4. Review case studies of successful governance reform: what institutional conditions, leadership actions, and reform sequencing enabled change? What counterfactual pressures preserved the status quo until change became unavoidable?
5. Synthesise into a set of actionable reform enablers — conditions and mechanisms that practitioners and regulators can use to catalyse governance improvement without adding governance overhead.

## Sources

- [ ] [North (1990) Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511808678) — path dependence; why inefficient institutions persist even when actors recognise the inefficiency
- [ ] [Williamson (1996) The Mechanisms of Governance](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-mechanisms-of-governance-9780195097856) — bilateral governance structures and the conditions under which reform becomes feasible
- [ ] [Jensen and Meckling (1976) Theory of the Firm: Managerial Behavior, Agency Costs and Ownership Structure](https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-405X(76)90026-X) — principal-agent problems in corporate governance; foundational model for leadership incentive misalignment
- [ ] [Haldane (2012) The Dog and the Frisbee](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/paper/2012/the-dog-and-the-frisbee) — empirical argument for simpler governance rules in regulated banking; complexity as a barrier to effective control and reform
- [ ] [Financial Stability Board (2017) Thematic Review on Corporate Governance](https://www.fsb.org/2017/04/thematic-review-on-corporate-governance-peer-review-report/) — cross-jurisdictional evidence on governance reform outcomes in regulated financial institutions
