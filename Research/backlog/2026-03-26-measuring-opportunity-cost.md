# The measurement asymmetry: why we cut costs but can't see lost opportunities

## Research Question

Why is measuring opportunity cost systematically harder than measuring direct costs, and what cognitive and structural mechanisms cause organisations to destroy value while claiming efficiency gains?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The structural asymmetry between cost visibility (fast, attributable) and opportunity visibility (slow, diffuse)
- Cognitive biases that amplify this asymmetry (loss aversion, present bias, attribution errors)
- Organisational incentive structures that punish opportunity investment and reward cost cuts
- How procurement, finance, and KPI (key performance indicator) design entrench this asymmetry
- Practical techniques for making opportunity cost more visible

**Out of scope:**
- Macroeconomic treatment of opportunity cost theory
- Capital allocation models (NPV (net present value), IRR (internal rate of return)) in depth — only as they relate to the visibility problem

**Constraints:** Focus on applied business context; academic theory only where it directly illuminates a practical mechanism.

## Context

Rory Sutherland frames this sharply in his Business Leader interview: "costs are quick to cut whereas opportunities take time to exploit." The asymmetry is structural, not just psychological — a balance sheet rewards margin expansion today, but the sales that don't happen because of a cut channel, a degraded customer experience, or an automated interaction that felt wrong are invisible. They never appear as a line item.

The procurement example from the interview illustrates this: procurement can claim credit for a cost reduction by centralising delivery through one provider. The resulting customer decisions to not purchase because their preferred carrier isn't available never flow back to procurement's scorecard. The cost reduction is real; the opportunity cost is real but unmeasured.

This problem applies directly to AI adoption decisions: companies will be able to show CFOs a cost-per-call or headcount reduction. They will not be able to show them the customers who called a competitor instead, the loyalty that eroded, or the cross-sell opportunity that a skilled human agent would have captured. The risk is that AI gets deployed to optimise the measurable at the expense of the valuable.

## Approach

1. Survey behavioral economics research on asymmetric loss/gain visibility and attribution bias
2. Examine structural incentive mechanisms in organisations (bonus structures, KPIs, balance sheet conventions) that amplify cost visibility
3. Identify documented cases where cost-reduction decisions destroyed unmeasured opportunity value
4. Research existing frameworks for making opportunity cost visible (strategic options, counterfactual modelling, marketing mix modelling)
5. Synthesise practical interventions that reduce the asymmetry
