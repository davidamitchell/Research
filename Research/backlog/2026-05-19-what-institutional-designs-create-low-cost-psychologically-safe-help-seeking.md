# What Institutional Designs Create Low-Cost, Psychologically Safe Help-Seeking?

## Research Question

Which institutional design choices create persistently low-cost, psychologically safe spaces for help-seeking, and what structural metrics indicate that vulnerability around knowledge gaps has become normalised?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Institutional design of recurring low-cost interaction spaces (mentoring windows, structured peer forums, office hours).
- Psychological safety as a mechanism for reducing help-seeking friction.
- Structural metrics that can track normalisation of vulnerability over time.

**Out of scope:**
- Individual therapeutic or clinical interventions.
- Technology-only interventions without institutional process change.

**Constraints:** Use field and workplace evidence where possible; include measurable design and evaluation indicators.

## Context

Without safe and low-friction interaction spaces, knowledge gaps stay hidden and compound. This question targets concrete institutional designs that can convert one-off support behaviour into stable organisational practice.

## Approach

1. Catalogue institutional patterns that reduce the social and cognitive cost of asking for help.
2. Evaluate evidence for psychological safety as a causal mediator of learning behaviour.
3. Define a practical metric set for monitoring institutional safety architecture.

## Sources

- [ ] [Edmondson, A. C. (1999) Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams](https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999) — foundational empirical link between safety and learning behaviour.
- [ ] [Google re:Work (2015) Guide: Understand Team Effectiveness](https://rework.withgoogle.com/guides/understanding-team-effectiveness/steps/introduction/) — large-scale workplace evidence operationalising psychological safety factors.
