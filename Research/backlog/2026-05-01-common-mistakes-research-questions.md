# What are the most common mistakes when formulating research questions and how are they avoided?

## Research Question

What are the most common and consequential mistakes researchers make when formulating research questions — such as questions that are too vague, unanswerable within resource constraints, or misaligned with available methodology — and what specific diagnostic and corrective techniques prevent each mistake?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Commonly documented failure modes: overly broad or vague questions, questions that assume their answer, questions that cannot be answered by empirical evidence, questions that exceed the available time/resource envelope, and questions that conflate multiple independent sub-questions into one
- Diagnostic tests to detect each failure mode before committing to a study design
- Corrective techniques: scoping, decomposition, reframing from topic to question, moving from normative to empirical framing
- Examples of flawed questions corrected to well-formed equivalents
- How these mistakes propagate into downstream methodology errors if left uncorrected

**Out of scope:**
- The positive definition of quality criteria — covered in `2026-05-01-qualities-effective-research-questions`
- The typology of question types — covered in `2026-05-01-types-research-questions`
- Systematic techniques for generating questions from scratch — covered in `2026-05-01-developing-sharpening-research-questions`

**Constraints:**
- Ground failure modes in published methodology literature and/or documented practitioner experience — not abstract speculation
- Each mistake must have a paired corrective technique, not just a diagnosis

## Context

Research methodology training typically teaches what a good research question looks like, but rarely provides an explicit catalogue of failure modes and targeted corrections. Without this, researchers must infer the diagnosis from the prescription — an error-prone process. This item approaches the problem from the failure side: given a bad question, what specific pattern of badness is it, and what is the minimum targeted intervention to correct it? The failure-mode framing is complementary to the quality-criteria framing in `2026-05-01-qualities-effective-research-questions` and together they form a complete diagnostic toolkit.

## Approach

1. **Catalogue failure modes** — Survey research methodology texts and supervisor/examiner guidance documents for documented question-formulation errors. Group into a mutually-exclusive typology (e.g., scope failures, answerability failures, framing failures, resource-mismatch failures).

2. **Find published examples** — For each failure mode, locate a published example of a flawed question and its corrected form. Journal articles with explicit methodology sections and thesis examination reports are good sources.

3. **Map each failure to a quality criterion** — Link each failure mode to the quality criterion it violates (see `2026-05-01-qualities-effective-research-questions`). This creates a two-way lookup: quality → what to aim for; failure mode → what to avoid.

4. **Identify corrective techniques** — For each failure mode, identify the specific, minimal intervention that corrects it without over-engineering the question. Document as a step-by-step corrective procedure.

5. **Produce a diagnostic checklist** — A structured checklist that a researcher can run against any candidate question before committing to a study design.
