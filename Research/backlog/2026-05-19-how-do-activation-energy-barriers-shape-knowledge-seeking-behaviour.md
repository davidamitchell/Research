# How Do Activation Energy Barriers Shape Knowledge Seeking Behaviour?

## Research Question

How do initiation costs such as evaluation anxiety, search effort, and access uncertainty act as activation-energy barriers that suppress knowledge seeking, and which organisational routines consistently reduce that barrier?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Behavioural and social initiation costs that discourage employees from asking for help.
- Organisational routines (office hours, peer mentoring, expert directories) as barrier-lowering mechanisms.
- Observable indicators for reduced initiation cost (faster first contact, higher help-seeking frequency).

**Out of scope:**
- Tool-specific user-interface optimisation of knowledge management platforms.
- Financial return on investment modelling for knowledge-sharing interventions.

**Constraints:** Focus on evidence from organisational behaviour and knowledge-management research with practical interventions.

## Context

Knowledge-sharing systems often fail at the very first step: people do not initiate requests. Clarifying the specific barriers and effective barrier-lowering routines is necessary before designing any broader knowledge-flow architecture.

## Approach

1. Identify and classify initiation barriers in internal knowledge seeking.
2. Evaluate intervention designs that reduce initiation friction without creating heavy process overhead.
3. Define practical metrics to test whether activation-energy reduction is durable over time.

## Sources

- [ ] [Szulanski, G. (1996) Exploring Internal Stickiness: Impediments to the Transfer of Best Practice Within the Firm](https://doi.org/10.2307/2392443) — foundational evidence on barriers to internal knowledge transfer.
- [ ] [Borgatti, S. P. & Cross, R. (2003) A Relational View of Information Seeking and Learning in Social Networks](https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.14.4.432.17487) — empirical model for why people do or do not seek expertise.
