# How do organisational incentives, culture, and behaviour influence adherence to governance in AI and low-code environments?

## Research Question

How do organisational incentives, culture, and behaviour influence adherence to governance in AI and low-code environments — specifically, what conditions drive teams to bypass governance controls (creating shadow Information Technology (IT) and ungoverned automations), and what governance design choices and cultural conditions produce durable compliance rather than formal compliance with behavioural circumvention?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The mechanism by which incentive structures (delivery velocity targets, individual productivity metrics, reward systems) drive circumvention of governance controls
- Shadow IT dynamics in the context of AI and low-code — what conditions produce ungoverned tool adoption and automation creation, and what evidence exists on the scale and risk profile of shadow AI/low-code
- The relationship between governance complexity and circumvention rate — whether more complex governance produces higher rates of workaround behaviour
- Cultural and leadership conditions associated with durable governance compliance vs formal-but-hollow compliance
- The role of change management, training, and governance communication in adoption
- Empirical evidence from technology governance research on the behavioural response to governance controls

**Out of scope:**
- Technical enforcement mechanisms (covered by Q3) — this item focuses on behavioural response to governance, not the controls themselves
- Maturity model stages (covered by Q13) — this item provides the behavioural input to the maturity model
- Regulatory compliance framing (covered by Q15) — focus is on internal organisational behaviour

**Constraints:**
- Must distinguish between evidence-based claims and management consulting assertions about culture
- Empirical evidence (surveys, case studies, incident analyses) preferred over prescriptive frameworks
- Sources should address the specific context of AI and low-code governance where possible; general IT governance behaviour research is acceptable as secondary evidence

## Context

Governance frameworks are typically designed as structural instruments — policies, controls, approval processes — without adequate attention to the behavioural conditions that determine whether those structures are actually followed. In AI and low-code environments, the gap between formal governance and observed behaviour is particularly acute because: (1) the tools are easily accessible to non-IT staff, (2) the productivity incentive to adopt tools quickly is high, and (3) the consequences of circumvention are often slow to materialise and difficult to attribute. Understanding the behavioural dimension of governance is a prerequisite for a realistic maturity model (Q13) — a maturity model that does not account for the gap between policy and behaviour will overestimate an organisation's actual governance capability.

## Approach

1. **Shadow IT and shadow AI evidence review:** Review empirical research on shadow IT prevalence, risk profile, and causal conditions; extend to AI and low-code tools where evidence exists. Assess whether AI/low-code shadow adoption has a different risk profile from traditional shadow IT.
2. **Incentive structure analysis:** Analyse how common enterprise incentive structures (sprint velocity targets, individual productivity measurement, cost centre pressure) create the conditions for governance circumvention, using principal-agent theory and behavioural economics frameworks.
3. **Governance complexity and circumvention:** Review evidence on the relationship between governance process complexity and workaround behaviour — does friction in governance processes directly predict circumvention rate?
4. **Cultural conditions for durable compliance:** Review evidence on what organisational cultural characteristics (psychological safety, leadership behaviour, peer norms, perceived legitimacy of governance) are associated with genuine rather than performative compliance.
5. **Design principles for behaviour-aware governance:** Based on the evidence, identify governance design principles that reduce the incentive to circumvent — e.g. fast-lane approval for low-risk use cases, self-service with guardrails, developer experience as a governance objective.
6. **Synthesis:** Produce an analysis of the behavioural risks to governance adherence and a set of evidence-based design principles for governance programmes that account for organisational behaviour.
