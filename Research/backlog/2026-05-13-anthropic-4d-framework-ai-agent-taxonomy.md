# What is Anthropic's '4D' framework for AI fluency, what are its four components and their definitions, and how does it compare to other published frameworks for taxonomising and compartmentalising AI agent terminology and concepts?

## Research Question

What is Anthropic's "4D" framework for Artificial Intelligence (AI) fluency, what do each of the four Ds (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence) mean in practice, and how does this framework compare to other published frameworks for taxonomising or compartmentalising AI terminology and concepts — both in scope and in practical design guidance for teams building or governing AI systems?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Anthropic's 4D framework as presented in the AI Fluency course (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence) — validate component names and definitions from primary sources
- Published frameworks from other major AI research organisations (e.g., NIST AI Use Taxonomy, OECD AI System Classification, OpenAI capability tiers, Google/DeepMind agent taxonomies) for categorising or labelling AI concepts
- How the frameworks compare: scope, target audience, practical utility, and convergence on key terms
- Practical design and governance guidance each framework provides for practitioners

**Out of scope:**
- Implementation details of specific AI products or models
- Non-agent AI frameworks (e.g., computer vision taxonomy, NLP task classification)
- Legal or regulatory frameworks that address AI liability rather than terminology
- Exhaustive survey of every AI taxonomy in academic literature

**Constraints:**
- Primary source preference: favour official publications, course materials, or papers from the named organisations over secondary summaries
- Sources must have verifiable URLs
- Time horizon: frameworks published or updated 2022–2025

## Context

Structured vocabulary and mental models for AI concepts reduce miscommunication between engineers, product managers, and executives. Anthropic's 4D framework (from their AI Fluency course) is one such model, framing human–AI collaboration around Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. Understanding what each D means — and where this framework sits relative to other published taxonomies (NIST, OECD, and industry papers) — helps practitioners choose the right vocabulary for their context, avoid concept drift, and align teams around shared definitions. The issue that spawned this item asked to "deep dive" into the 4D framework and identify comparable compartmentalisation approaches.

## Approach

1. **Validate the 4D framework components.** Locate the primary Anthropic source (AI Fluency course, Lesson 2B) and confirm the four Ds and their official definitions. Note any version history or evolution of the framework.
2. **Define each D with examples.** For each of Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence: state the definition, the capability it addresses, and a concrete agent-design example from the source material.
3. **Survey comparable frameworks.** Identify at least three other published frameworks for categorising AI concepts or agent behaviours — candidates include: NIST AI Use Taxonomy (human-centred outcome approach), OECD AI System Classification, Anthropic's own agent/workflow distinction from "Building Effective Agents", and any OpenAI or Google framework.
4. **Compare frameworks on key dimensions.** Build a comparison across: scope (what AI behaviour does the framework cover?), target audience (developers vs. policymakers vs. general users), granularity (high-level labels vs. detailed sub-categories), and practical utility (design guidance vs. governance vs. literacy).
5. **Identify convergence and gaps.** Which terms or dimensions appear consistently across frameworks? Where do frameworks diverge or leave gaps? Are there concepts the 4D framework addresses that others miss, and vice versa?
6. **Synthesise practical guidance.** What framework or combination of frameworks is most useful for a team building or governing AI agents? Under what conditions should practitioners prefer one over another?
