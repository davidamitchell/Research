# Meta-analysis standards and AI skill evaluation: what do professional systematic-review frameworks require, and how do existing AI research skills measure up?

## Research Question

What are the established standards and best practices for meta-analysis (MA) and systematic review, and how do existing AI research skills in this repository measure up against them?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Internationally recognised systematic review and meta-analysis standards (e.g., Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA), Cochrane Handbook, Campbell Collaboration guidelines)
- Study quality assessment tools and criteria (e.g., Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development and Evaluations (GRADE), Risk Of Bias In Non-randomised Studies of Interventions (ROBINS-I), Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS))
- Study selection protocols: search strategies, inclusion/exclusion criteria, Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome (PICO) framework
- Best practices for synthesis and summarisation of multiple studies (narrative synthesis, thematic analysis, evidence mapping)
- Evaluation frameworks or quality benchmarks proposed for Artificial Intelligence (AI)-assisted literature review or meta-analysis
- Inventory of research skills currently in `.github/skills/` and their alignment with the above standards
- Identified gaps and a recommended design for a dedicated meta-analysis skill

**Out of scope:**
- Conducting an actual meta-analysis on any domain topic
- Domain-specific meta-analysis standards for clinical medicine beyond their transferable methodological principles
- Quantitative statistical meta-analysis methods (pooled effect sizes, forest plots, heterogeneity statistics) — a specialist sub-topic that belongs in a follow-on item if warranted
- Skills in repositories other than `davidamitchell/Research` and `davidamitchell/Skills`

**Constraints:**
- Focus on methodological and process standards, not domain-specific ones
- Prioritise frameworks applicable to non-clinical research domains, especially software/AI research synthesis
- Sources must be publicly accessible (no paywall-only academic papers unless an accessible version exists)

## Context

This repository uses structured research skills to conduct investigations and produce findings. The issue from which this item was created asks whether the existing skills hold up against professional meta-analysis standards — a question that is directly actionable: if gaps are found, the output is a new or improved skill. The question is worth answering now because the repo is actively adding new research items and the research-loop workflow is running regularly; improving the synthesis quality standard early multiplies value across all future items.

## Approach

1. **Standards inventory** — identify and document the main internationally recognised systematic review and meta-analysis standards (PRISMA 2020, Cochrane Handbook 6th edition, Campbell Collaboration, AMSTAR 2). Extract the core requirements from each that are applicable outside clinical medicine.
2. **Study quality criteria** — examine how established frameworks (GRADE, ROBINS-I, NOS, JBI critical appraisal tools) assess the quality and risk of bias of individual studies. Identify the transferable principles for assessing source credibility and reliability in non-empirical research synthesis.
3. **Study selection protocols** — document standard inclusion/exclusion criteria design, PICO framing, and search strategy requirements. Assess what a rigorous protocol looks like for a knowledge-synthesis agent.
4. **Synthesis best practices** — survey established narrative synthesis, thematic analysis, and evidence mapping methods. Identify which are most applicable to the kind of synthesis this repo performs (across diverse, non-empirical sources).
5. **AI-assisted meta-analysis frameworks** — search for any proposed evaluation frameworks or quality benchmarks specific to AI-assisted literature review or meta-analysis (e.g., from NLP (Natural Language Processing) research, systematic review automation tools like Rayyan, Covidence, or academic proposals).
6. **Existing skills audit** — inspect the current skills in `.github/skills/` (if the submodule is initialised) and relevant prompts in `research-prompt.md` and `research-review-prompt.md`. Map each skill's steps against the standards identified in steps 1–5.
7. **Gap analysis and skill design** — identify the gaps between current practice and professional standards. Produce a recommended design for a `meta-analysis` skill (or improvements to existing skills) that closes those gaps.

## Sources

- [ ] [PRISMA 2020 Statement](https://www.prisma-statement.org/prisma-2020) — the canonical reporting standard for systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- [ ] [Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (6th edition)](https://training.cochrane.org/handbook) — comprehensive methodology for systematic reviews; transferable principles beyond clinical medicine
- [ ] [Campbell Collaboration Systematic Review Methods](https://www.campbellcollaboration.org/research-resources/writing-a-campbell-systematic-review.html) — extends systematic review methods to social, behavioural, and education research
- [ ] [GRADE Working Group — GRADE Handbook](https://gdt.gradepro.org/app/handbook/handbook.html) — framework for grading the quality of evidence and strength of recommendations
- [ ] [AMSTAR 2 — A MeaSurement Tool to Assess systematic Reviews](https://www.bmj.com/content/358/bmj.j4008) — tool for assessing the methodological quality of systematic reviews
- [ ] [Popay et al. (2006) Guidance on the conduct of narrative synthesis in systematic reviews](https://www.lancaster.ac.uk/media/lancaster-university/content-assets/documents/fhm/dhr/chir/NSsynthesisguidancefinal.pdf) — practical guidance for narrative synthesis methods
- [ ] [Rayyan — AI-powered systematic review tool](https://rayyan.ai/) — example of an AI-assisted screening and synthesis tool; useful for understanding what AI tools do well and where gaps remain
