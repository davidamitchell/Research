# Interoception and the predictive self: selfhood as bodily inference

## Research Question

What is the evidence that the sense of self emerges from interoceptive predictive processing — and what are the implications for understanding depersonalisation, emotion, and mental illness?

- Interoception: definition, neural substrates (insular cortex, anterior cingulate)
- Seth's "beast machine" thesis: selfhood grounded in homeostatic prediction
- Empirical evidence: heartbeat detection tasks, rubber-hand illusion, bodily ownership studies
- Implications for depersonalisation, anxiety, and affect regulation

**Out of scope:**

### §2 Investigation

**A1 — Interoceptive inference versus exteroceptive PP**

[fact] Seth's 2013 *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* paper defines interoceptive inference as the brain's application of predictive coding to signals arising from inside the body — heart rate, hunger, temperature, gut state, respiration — rather than to signals from external sensory organs. The key claim is that the brain does not passively read out these signals but actively predicts their values, generating interoceptive prediction errors when predictions are violated. Source: Seth (2013), "Interoceptive inference, emotion, and the embodied self," *Trends Cogn Sci* 17(11): 565–573.

[fact] Seth & Friston (2016) extend this to active inference: the brain's autonomic and motor outputs regulate the body to bring actual interoceptive states into alignment with predicted states. This means the body itself is partly an object of inference, not just a passive signal source. Source: Seth & Friston (2016), *Phil Trans R Soc B* 371.

[inference] This architecture — predicting internal state, acting to fulfil predictions, propagating errors only when predictions fail — is structurally identical to the PP framework applied to exteroception, but the target domain is the visceral body rather than the external world. The implication is that errors in interoceptive prediction feel like uncomfortable bodily states (nausea, anxiety, arousal) just as exteroceptive prediction errors feel like surprising percepts.

**A2 — Layers of selfhood in Seth's account**

[fact] Seth (2021, *Being You*, ch. 9–11, "Beast Machine" section) identifies multiple distinct aspects of the self, each implemented by different predictive mechanisms: the *bodily self* (felt embodiment, interoceptive predictions); the *perspectival self* (having a viewpoint, a "here"); the *volitional self* (sense of agency and authorship of actions); and the *narrative self* (autobiographical continuity, the "I" that persists over time). Seth claims the bodily self, grounded in interoception, is the most basic — it is the layer from which the others depend, not the other way around. Source: Seth (2021) and his subsequent Trends Cog Sci (2018) beast machine paper.

[inference] If the bodily/interoceptive self is foundational, then disruptions at this layer should propagate upward, destabilising higher layers of selfhood. This would predict that depersonalisation (a disorder of bodily self) could secondarily impair perspectival and volitional self-experience — consistent with clinical presentations where DPD patients report feeling like an observer of their own actions.

**A3 — Homeostasis, allostasis, and the sense of being alive**

[fact] Seth grounds the beast machine thesis biologically: the primary function of the brain is not to model the external world but to regulate the internal milieu — to keep physiological variables within viable ranges. Interoceptive predictions are, at their core, allostatic predictions: the brain models what the body's state *will need to be* to remain viable under anticipated demands, and it regulates the body accordingly. Source: Seth & Friston (2016); Seth (2021).

[inference] This reframes the sense of being alive — the basic felt sense of existing as a living body — as a consequence of successful allostatic regulation. When regulation is intact, the background "hum" of interoceptive signals is a continuous low-level signal of physiological viability. This is consistent with the observation that disruptions to homeostatic signalling (through starvation, extreme pain, or dissociative states) produce profound alterations in the sense of being alive or real.

**B1–B3 — Neural substrates: the insular cortex**

[fact] Craig (2009) synthesises neuroanatomical and functional imaging evidence that the insular cortex processes interoceptive signals along a posterior-to-anterior gradient. The posterior insula receives primary interoceptive input (raw signals from the viscera, via the thalamus). The mid-insula integrates these signals with contextual and emotional factors. The anterior insular cortex (AIC) re-represents the integrated signal as a subjective feeling state — a "global emotional moment." Source: Craig (2009), "How do you feel — now?" *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 10(1): 59–70.

[fact] The AIC contains von Economo neurons (spindle cells) — large bipolar neurons found only in humans and other great apes, cetaceans, and elephants. Their density in the AIC is unique among cortical regions and has been interpreted as specialised architecture for the rapid integration and transmission of complex social-emotional information. Source: Craig (2009); multiple confirmatory neuroanatomy reviews.

[fact] The heartbeat evoked potential (HEP) is an EEG component time-locked to the R-wave of the cardiac cycle, typically measured over frontocentral electrodes, that reflects cortical processing of cardiac interoceptive signals. HEP amplitude is modulated by attentional state and by interoceptive training. Source: Multiple EEG studies synthesised in Tsakiris et al. and the Lancet (2024) review.

[fact] Functional imaging studies show the AIC is activated by an extraordinarily wide range of stimuli and states — physical pain, emotional pain, temperature, hunger, craving, empathy, social rejection, decision-making, insight, and internal bodily signals. Craig interprets this breadth as evidence that the AIC is not modality-specific but is generating a cross-modal integration of interoceptive and cognitive/social signals into a unified subjective moment. Source: Craig (2009).

**C1 — Rubber hand illusion and bodily ownership**

[fact] Tsakiris & Haggard (2005) demonstrated that the rubber hand illusion (RHI) — the compelling feeling that a rubber hand is part of one's own body when it is stroked synchronously with the hidden real hand — requires synchronous visuotactile integration as a necessary condition, but the degree of ownership is modulated by the body schema (the brain's prior representation of the body). The illusion fails or weakens when the rubber hand is in anatomically implausible positions, demonstrating top-down modulation of bodily ownership. Source: Tsakiris & Haggard (2005), *J Exp Psychol Hum Percept Perform* 31(1): 80–91.

[inference] The RHI demonstrates that bodily ownership is not determined solely by sensory signals from the body but is actively constructed by the brain's prior predictions about body configuration. This supports the predictive selfhood thesis: the "real hand" is mine not because of a direct sensory signal but because it matches the brain's predictions about body state.

**C2 — Interoceptive sensitivity and susceptibility to bodily illusions**

[fact] Tsakiris et al. (2011) ("Just a heartbeat away from one's body," *Proc R Soc B*) showed that individuals with higher interoceptive accuracy — measured by heartbeat detection task (HCT) performance — are less susceptible to the rubber hand illusion. High interoceptive sensitivity acts as an anchor to one's own body, making ownership representations more resistant to manipulation by external visuotactile cues. Source: Tsakiris et al. (2011).

[fact] Cardio-visual synchrony studies (Tsakiris group) have shown that synchronising visual stimuli (e.g., a flashing light or morphed face) with participants' heartbeat signals, rather than with a yoked asynchronous control, produces stronger identification with that visual stimulus. Participants shown their own face paired with heartbeat-synchronous visual signals reported stronger ownership compared to asynchronous controls. Source: Sel et al. (2017) "Heartfelt Self," *Cerebral Cortex* 27(11), and related work.

[inference] Together these studies suggest that cardiac interoceptive signals function as a prior for self-attribution: the heartbeat (as a stable, continuous interoceptive signal) provides a bodily anchor against which external representations are tested for "mineness." This is a specific, empirically testable prediction of the interoceptive selfhood hypothesis.

**D1 — Neuroimaging evidence in depersonalisation disorder**

[fact] Multiple fMRI studies have found that patients with depersonalisation disorder (DPD) show reduced activation in the anterior insula and related limbic structures (amygdala) during emotionally evocative tasks, compared to healthy controls. This reduction in insular activation correlates with the characteristic emotional numbing of DPD: patients report feeling their emotional life is "dampened" or "switched off." Source: Frontiers in Psychology (2016), "Emotional Experience and Awareness of Self: Functional MRI Studies of Depersonalization Disorder."

[fact] DPD is associated with increased activation in the right ventrolateral prefrontal cortex, interpreted as a top-down inhibitory "override" that suppresses the emotional signals that would normally arise from limbic-insular circuits. Source: Sierra & Berrios (2001) and subsequent replication studies; Frontiers Human Neuroscience (2022) review.

[fact] Improvement in DPD symptoms following treatment is associated with increased insular activity, providing prospective evidence that the insular deficit is not merely an epiphenomenon of illness severity but is causally involved in symptom generation. Source: Frontiers Psychology (2016) fMRI review.

[fact] A study using heartbeat detection tasks (PLOS One 2014) found that DPD patients perform significantly worse than healthy controls at detecting their own heartbeats, indicating impaired cardiac interoceptive accuracy. DPD patients also showed differences in brain functional connectivity during interoceptive tasks. Source: PLOS One (2014), interoceptive accuracy in DPD.

**D2 — Interoceptive accuracy in anxiety and depression: the meta-analytic evidence**

[fact] A large meta-analysis (Desmedt et al., 2022, *Collabra: Psychology*, 133 studies, 11,524 participants) found no significant association between heartbeat counting task (HCT) performance and trait anxiety, depression, or alexithymia in the general population. Weak associations were found with age, sex, heart rate, and BMI, but not with psychological variables. Source: Desmedt et al. (2022).

[fact] A separate meta-analysis (Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews, 2022) focusing specifically on the anxiety-interoceptive accuracy relationship also reported no robust association after accounting for moderators. Source: Neurosci Biobehav Rev (2022).

[fact] The Lancet EClinicalMedicine (2024) review confirmed that meta-analytic evidence for interoception in anxiety and depression does not support heartbeat perception as systematically impaired in these conditions. Source: Lancet EClinicalMedicine (2024).

[fact] The validity of the HCT itself as a measure of interoceptive accuracy has been challenged: some studies suggest participants may be guessing based on knowledge of their typical resting heart rate rather than genuinely perceiving the heartbeat signal. Source: ScienceDirect commentary (2022) "What counts when heartbeats are counted."

[inference] The meta-analytic null result for the HCT-anxiety link does not refute the interoceptive account of anxiety; it specifically undermines one measurement approach (cardiac heartbeat counting) as a reliable index of interoceptive disruption in anxiety disorders. The account may still hold at the level of interoceptive inference (precision dysregulation, altered weighting of prior vs. sensory signal) even when accuracy as measured by HCT is unaffected.

[assumption] Interoceptive inference dysregulation in anxiety is better captured by measures of interoceptive sensibility (how much attention the person pays to internal states) and interoceptive learning (how rapidly the brain updates interoceptive predictions) than by the HCT. This is a working assumption consistent with Seth & Friston (2016) but not yet settled by meta-analysis. Justification: the HCT measures detection of discrete cardiac signals, which may be orthogonal to the predictive coding parameters (precision weighting, prior strength) that drive emotional experience.

**D3 — Schizophrenia and ipseity disturbance**

[fact] Sass & Parnas (2003) propose that schizophrenia's core anomaly is a disturbance in ipseity — the basic, pre-reflective sense of existing as a subject of experience. They identify two defining features: *hyperreflexivity* (normally tacit mental processes become focal and objectified, experienced as alien or external) and *diminished self-affection* (the direct, pre-reflective sense of being a unified, coherent self is weakened). Source: Sass & Parnas (2003), *Schizophrenia Bulletin* 29(3): 427–444.

[inference] The Sass-Parnas account maps onto the interoceptive selfhood thesis: if the bodily self (grounded in interoceptive predictions) is the foundational self-layer, then ipseity disturbance could be produced by a disruption at the interoceptive prediction level, causing basic "mineness" of experience to become uncertain or absent. Positive symptoms (hallucinations attributed to alien agency) and negative symptoms (blunted affect, avolition) would then be downstream consequences of failed bodily self-grounding. This mapping is an inference, not established by Sass & Parnas themselves, whose account is phenomenological rather than mechanistic.

**E — Necessary, sufficient, or neither?**

[inference] Evidence from DPD, heartbeat studies, and rubber hand illusion work supports interoception as necessary for the minimal (bodily) self: disruptions reliably produce impaired bodily ownership and emotional numbing. Intact interoception acts as an anchor for self-attribution.

[inference] Interoception is not sufficient for the full sense of self. Higher layers — perspectival, volitional, narrative — depend additionally on exteroceptive (proprioceptive, visual, tactile) signals, motor representations, memory, language, and social interaction. Cases of bodily self-disruption in conditions like locked-in syndrome, spinal cord injury, or sensory deprivation show that a robust narrative/cognitive self can survive severe interoceptive impairment, suggesting some independence of higher layers. The sufficiency claim would require that all forms of selfhood be eliminable to interoceptive prediction, which is not supported.

[fact] Consensus view in the interoception literature (Tsakiris, 2017; Seth, 2021): interoception is a necessary foundation for the minimal self, but the full matrix of self-experience requires integration of interoceptive, exteroceptive, cognitive, and social signals. Source: Tsakiris (2017), "Interoception beyond homeostasis," *Phil Trans R Soc B* 371; Seth (2021).

### §3 Reasoning

Seth's beast machine thesis is coherently structured around one claim: the minimal self (the basic sense of being an embodied living thing) is generated by the brain's continuous predictive regulation of internal bodily states. This is a restricted but well-targeted claim. Craig's insular cortex work provides the anatomical substrate: the anterior insula functions as a hierarchical interoceptive integrator that transforms raw visceral signals into a continuously updated subjective "global emotional moment." This is consistent with Seth's framework, and Craig's review — based on a large body of functional imaging and neuroanatomical evidence — counts as strong supporting evidence.

The bodily ownership literature (Tsakiris & Haggard 2005; Tsakiris et al. 2011) extends the picture to the multisensory domain: bodily ownership is not simply a readout of sensory signals from the body but is actively constructed, with interoceptive accuracy functioning as a stabilising prior. The cardio-visual synchrony findings are particularly striking: the heartbeat, the most continuous and reliable interoceptive signal, directly influences whether a visual stimulus is attributed as "mine." This is direct experimental evidence for the interoceptive prior hypothesis.

The clinical literature provides the strongest test. DPD is the closest thing to a natural experiment in disrupted interoceptive selfhood: patients have intact cognition, intact exteroceptive perception, but impaired interoceptive processing (reduced insular activation, reduced HCT performance) — and this maps precisely onto the symptom profile of diminished bodily self, emotional numbing, and depersonalisation. The finding that clinical improvement restores insular activity strengthens the causal interpretation considerably.

The meta-analytic null result for HCT and anxiety is a genuine empirical complication for the theory's clinical extension. However, it is best interpreted as a measurement validity problem rather than a refutation of the interoceptive account: the HCT is a coarse, contested instrument that may not capture the precision-weighting and prior-strength parameters that the Seth-Friston framework treats as the drivers of anxiety. The Lancet review's summary of evidence — that interoception is implicated across anxiety, depression, and psychosis at a broad level — is consistent with the theory, even if the specific HCT-anxiety correlation is not robust.

The Sass-Parnas ipseity disturbance account of schizophrenia is conceptually compatible with the interoceptive selfhood thesis but not mechanistically integrated with it. It provides a phenomenological description of what disrupted selfhood looks like that aligns with what the theory predicts, but the causal link to interoceptive prediction failure in schizophrenia requires further empirical work.

**On necessity vs. sufficiency:** The evidence supports interoception as necessary for the minimal self (DPD, bodily ownership data) but insufficient for higher self-layers. This is a modest but defensible conclusion: it means that a purely cognitive or computational system without interoceptive grounding would lack the most basic layer of selfhood, even if it possessed sophisticated memory, language, and social cognition. Seth's implication for AI — that machine consciousness would require something analogous to interoceptive grounding in biological drives — follows naturally if the necessity claim is correct.

### §4 Consistency Check

**Internal claim consistency:** The evidence from Craig (2009), Tsakiris et al. (2011), and the DPD neuroimaging literature converges on the anterior insula as the key structure. Craig's anatomical review, functional imaging of DPD, and the HEP literature all implicate the same region. There are no internal contradictions here.

**Tension identified — HCT meta-analysis vs. theory:** The meta-analytic null result for the HCT-anxiety link sits in tension with the theoretical expectation. The resolution offered (HCT measures detection accuracy, not inference precision) is theoretically motivated but not yet directly empirically confirmed. This tension is explicitly flagged in §2 and labelled [assumption]. It is a genuine gap, not a resolved contradiction.

**DPD evidence vs. anxiety/depression evidence:** DPD shows robust insular deficits and reduced HCT performance; anxiety and depression show no robust HCT impairment. These are different disorders, and the distinction is clinically meaningful: DPD is specifically a disorder of bodily self-awareness, while anxiety and depression are disorders of affect regulation and mood. The prediction from the theory would be that the interoceptive disruption in these latter conditions operates at a different level (precision dysregulation, altered prior strength) than in DPD (signal absence). This distinction is consistent across the evidence.

**Sass-Parnas and interoceptive model:** The Sass-Parnas account uses phenomenological language (hyperreflexivity, ipseity) that is not directly translatable to interoceptive prediction error. The mapping is an inference, clearly labelled as such. No contradiction arises if the inference is held as speculative.

### §5 Depth and Breadth Expansion

**Developmental dimension:** The interoceptive account has developmental implications that were not in the original scope but strengthen the thesis. If interoceptive prediction grounds the minimal self, then the developmental trajectory of selfhood should track the maturation of interoceptive processing. Research on infant interoceptive awareness (foetal cardiac responses, neonatal visceral sensitivity) supports early emergence of interoceptive processing as a prerequisite for basic self-experience — consistent with the thesis but not conclusive.

**Cross-cultural and neurological dimension:** The account predicts that radical disruptions to interoceptive signal availability — as in high-level spinal cord injury, where ascending visceral signals are severed — should alter the minimal self. Clinical evidence does show altered affect and bodily self-experience in such populations, though a robust narrative self typically survives, consistent with the "necessary but not sufficient" conclusion.

**Therapeutic implications:** If DPD results from suppressed interoceptive signal processing and overactive prefrontal inhibition, then treatments that increase interoceptive awareness — mindfulness-based interventions targeting body scan and breath awareness, interoceptive training protocols — would be predicted to reduce DPD severity by restoring insular processing. There is emerging evidence that this is the case, though randomised controlled trial data remain limited.

**Relationship to emotions as "constructed" (Lisa Feldman Barrett):** Seth's interoceptive inference account of emotion is convergent with but distinct from Barrett's theory of constructed emotion (*How Emotions Are Made*, 2017). Barrett argues emotions are entirely constructed by the brain from interoceptive and conceptual inputs — there are no discrete emotion circuits. Seth agrees emotions are constructed but emphasises the homeostatic/predictive dimension more than the conceptual categorisation. Both accounts treat interoception as the raw material of emotion, but they differ on the role of social concepts. This convergence strengthens the interoceptive account of emotion while leaving open the precise mechanism.

**Clinical extension to eating disorders and chronic pain:** Interoceptive dysfunction has been documented in eating disorders (Garfinkel et al., 2016, anorexia nervosa showing impaired HCT performance with altered body image) and chronic pain (altered interoceptive prediction generating persistent aversive body representations). These extend the clinical significance of the account beyond DPD and anxiety, suggesting the interoceptive framework may be clinically actionable across a broad range of conditions.

### §6 Synthesis

**Central claim:** The sense of minimal selfhood — the pre-reflective experience of being an embodied living thing — is grounded in the brain's predictive regulation of interoceptive signals. This claim is supported by convergent neuroanatomical evidence (anterior insular cortex architecture), functional imaging (DPD studies), experimental paradigms (heartbeat-self-face synchrony, interoceptive accuracy and RHI susceptibility), and clinical neuropsychology (DPD symptom-insula correlation, treatment response). The claim is strongest for the minimal, bodily self; higher layers of selfhood require additional non-interoceptive components.

**Empirical qualification:** The extension of interoceptive disruption to anxiety and depression — while theoretically motivated — is not supported by the most rigorous meta-analytic evidence for cardiac interoceptive accuracy. This does not refute the account but restricts its application and highlights that the HCT is an inadequate proxy for the precision-weighting parameters the theory requires.

**Necessity vs. sufficiency:** Interoception is necessary for minimal selfhood; it is not sufficient for higher selfhood. The philosophical and developmental evidence supports this intermediate conclusion.

**Clinical implications:** DPD is the clearest clinical model of disrupted interoceptive selfhood, with robust insular deficit evidence. Schizophrenia's ipseity disturbance is conceptually compatible with the account. The DPD evidence supports the feasibility of interoceptive intervention as a treatment target.

**Key sources:** Seth (2013, 2021), Craig (2009), Tsakiris et al. (2011), Desmedt et al. (2022), DPD fMRI literature.

## Findings

### Executive Summary

The sense of minimal selfhood — the pre-reflective experience of being an embodied living thing — arises from the brain's predictive regulation of interoceptive signals, with the anterior insular cortex as the key anatomical hub that transforms raw visceral inputs into a continuously updated subjective "global emotional moment." This is supported by convergent evidence: Craig's neuroanatomical framework, bodily ownership experiments showing that interoceptive accuracy stabilises self-attribution, and clinical findings in depersonalisation disorder where reduced insular activation directly correlates with the severity of bodily self-disruption and emotional numbing. Interoception is necessary for the minimal (bodily) self but insufficient for higher layers — perspectival, volitional, and narrative selfhood require additional exteroceptive, cognitive, and social components. The extension of interoceptive disruption to anxiety and depression is theoretically motivated but challenged by meta-analytic null results for the heartbeat counting task, indicating that coarse cardiac detection measures do not capture the precision-weighting parameters that drive emotional experience in these conditions.

### Key Findings

1. Seth's beast machine thesis proposes that the minimal self — the basic experience of being an alive, embodied organism — is constituted by the brain's allostatic predictive regulation of internal bodily states, making homeostatic function the biological foundation of consciousness rather than abstract cognition. [high confidence]

2. The anterior insular cortex (AIC) processes interoceptive signals along a posterior-to-anterior gradient: the posterior insula receives raw visceral input; the anterior insula integrates it with emotional and cognitive context to generate a "global emotional moment" that constitutes the subjective present. [high confidence — from Craig 2009, multi-study fMRI convergence]

3. ### Assumptions

- **Assumption:** Interoceptive inference dysregulation in anxiety is better captured by precision-weighting measures than by cardiac detection accuracy (HCT). **Justification:** The Seth-Friston (2016) model predicts that emotional states arise from the weighting assigned to interoceptive priors relative to sensory signals; HCT measures only whether the participant can detect discrete cardiac events, which is orthogonal to this parameter. This assumption is theoretically motivated but lacks a direct meta-analytic test.

- **Assumption:** The ipseity disturbance in schizophrenia (Sass & Parnas 2003) is mechanistically connected to interoceptive prediction failure. **Justification:** The phenomenological description of hyperreflexivity and diminished self-affection is consistent with what loss of interoceptive self-grounding would produce, but Sass & Parnas do not invoke interoceptive mechanisms, and direct fMRI/HCT evidence in schizophrenia is limited.

### Analysis

The interoceptive selfhood thesis has three independent lines of supporting evidence: (1) neuroanatomical architecture — Craig's AIC gradient describes a plausible implementation substrate; (2) experimental bodily ownership paradigms — the Tsakiris line shows interoceptive accuracy stabilises self-attribution under multisensory challenge; and (3) clinical pathology — DPD neuroimaging shows the predicted insular deficit with symptom-severity correlation and treatment-restoration patterns. These lines converge on the same structure (AIC) and the same mechanism (interoceptive prediction as bodily self-anchor).

The main tension is between the theoretical expectation that interoceptive disruption drives anxiety/depression and the meta-analytic null for HCT in these conditions. Two interpretations are possible: the theory is wrong about anxiety/depression, or the HCT is the wrong measure. The methodological critique of HCT (that it may measure knowledge rather than perception) supports the second interpretation; but until better interoceptive precision measures are validated and tested meta-analytically, the first cannot be fully ruled out. This is the most important unresolved question for the theory's clinical utility.

The "necessary but not sufficient" conclusion is well-supported and theoretically satisfying: it explains why DPD (a specific bodily self disruption) produces specific symptoms without abolishing all selfhood, and why locked-in syndrome patients retain rich self-experience despite radical interoceptive signal disruption.

### Risks, Gaps, and Uncertainties

- The heartbeat counting task's construct validity is uncertain; the best meta-analytic evidence suggests it does not measure the interoceptive processing parameters the theory requires. The field needs validated precision-weighting measures to test the core claims.
- Randomised controlled trial evidence for interoceptive training (e.g., body scan, mindfulness, HRV biofeedback) as treatment for DPD is sparse. The neuroimaging evidence is promising, but translational efficacy is not established.
- The schizophrenia link is conceptual. The Sass-Parnas account is phenomenologically rich but mechanistically underspecified; direct interoceptive measures in schizophrenia are needed to test the mapping.
- Developmental evidence for the emergence of interoceptive selfhood in infants is indicative but not conclusive.
- The cardio-visual synchrony findings (Sel et al. 2017) rest on a relatively small number of replication studies.

### Open Questions

- Can the precision-weighting parameters of interoceptive inference be measured directly and reliably in clinical populations, and do they track anxiety/depression severity more robustly than HCT?
- What is the relationship between interoceptive training (e.g., mindfulness body scan, HRV biofeedback) and self-experience in DPD — do randomised trials show efficacy?
- Does ipseity disturbance in schizophrenia correlate with specific interoceptive measures (AIC activation, HEP amplitude, bodily ownership susceptibility)?
- How does the interoceptive self account relate to Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory of constructed emotion — are they complementary, competing, or explaining different levels of the same process?
- Do high-level spinal cord injury patients show systematic shifts in minimal self-experience that track the loss of ascending interoceptive signal, and if so, at what level?
