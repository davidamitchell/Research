### §2 Investigation

**A1 — Chalmers' easy/hard distinction**

[fact] Chalmers introduced the term "hard problem" in a 1994 talk at the Tucson Science of Consciousness conference, published in 1995 in *Journal of Consciousness Studies* as "Facing up to the problem of consciousness," and expanded in *The Conscious Mind* (OUP, 1996). Source: Wikipedia, *Hard problem of consciousness* (accessed 2026-03-05); Wikipedia, *The Conscious Mind* (accessed 2026-03-05).

[fact] Chalmers' easy problems are those involving functional/mechanistic explanation: how the brain discriminates stimuli, integrates information, controls behaviour, and generates verbal reports. They are "easy" not because they are simple but because their solutions are in principle clear — find the relevant structural and dynamic facts. Source: Wikipedia, *Hard problem of consciousness*.

[fact] The hard problem is: *why* and *how* do those physical processes give rise to subjective experience? Even after all functional questions are answered, there remains the further question: "Why is the performance of these functions accompanied by experience?" Source: Chalmers (1995), as quoted in Wikipedia, *Hard problem of consciousness*.

[fact] Chalmers' formulation: consciousness is used in Nagel's sense — "what it is like to be something" — i.e., phenomenal consciousness, not access consciousness or behavioural responsiveness. Source: Wikipedia, *Hard problem of consciousness*.

[fact] A 2020 PhilPapers survey found 62.4% of professional philosophers surveyed consider the hard problem a genuine problem; 29.7% deny it. Source: Wikipedia, *Hard problem of consciousness*.

**A2 — The zombie argument**

[fact] The philosophical zombie (p-zombie) argument holds that a being physically identical to a conscious human but lacking any phenomenal experience is conceivable. Because p-zombies are conceivable, Chalmers argues they are logically possible. Because they are logically possible, facts about consciousness are not logically entailed by physical facts — consciousness is not reducible to physics. Source: Wikipedia, *Philosophical zombie* (accessed 2026-03-05).

[fact] Chalmers' three main arguments for irreducibility in *The Conscious Mind*: (1) appeals to conceivability (p-zombies; inverted qualia); (2) appeals to epistemology (knowledge argument: Mary's Room — gaining new knowledge by seeing red that was not entailed by physical facts); (3) appeals to analysis (no reductive account satisfactorily explains why brain states are accompanied by experience). Source: Wikipedia, *The Conscious Mind*.

[fact] A 2013 PhilPapers survey found 36% of philosophers said p-zombies are conceivable but metaphysically impossible; 23% said metaphysically possible; 16% said inconceivable; 25% other. A 2020 survey yielded nearly identical results. Source: Wikipedia, *Philosophical zombie*.

[inference] The zombie argument's force depends entirely on whether conceivability is a reliable guide to metaphysical possibility. Critics (Frankish, Dennett, Hill) argue it is not. Chalmers' position therefore hinges on a contested methodological premise about modal reasoning.

**A3 — The explanatory gap**

[fact] The term "explanatory gap" was introduced by Joseph Levine (1983), who pointed out that "Pain is the firing of C fibres" may be physiologically true while leaving entirely open why C-fibre firing *feels* like pain. Source: Wikipedia, *Explanatory gap*.

[fact] Levine distinguishes epistemic gap (our inability to explain the connection) from metaphysical gap (the connection being ontologically absent). He argues the former is clear; the latter is not established by it alone. Source: Wikipedia, *Explanatory gap*.

[inference] The explanatory gap and the hard problem are related but distinct: the gap is an epistemic observation about current science; the hard problem is a metaphysical claim about irreducibility. Seth's real problem addresses the epistemic gap; it does not address the metaphysical question.

**A4 — What would a solution look like for Chalmers?**

[fact] Chalmers argues in *The Conscious Mind* that a theory of consciousness must specify *fundamental laws* connecting physical structures to phenomenal experience, analogous to the laws of electromagnetism. Mere correlation maps (neural correlates of consciousness) do not close the explanatory gap because they leave open why the correlate produces experience. Source: Wikipedia, *The Conscious Mind*.

[inference] On Chalmers' framing, Seth's real problem — even if it fully succeeded in mapping brain states to conscious qualities — would remain an "easy problem" solution. It tells us which physical patterns correlate with which experiences, not *why* those patterns produce experience at all.

**B1 — Seth's real problem formulation**

[fact] Seth's "real problem" asks: why does *this particular* pattern of brain activity map to *this particular* kind of conscious experience? It is concerned with specifying the neural and computational properties that give rise to specific conscious qualities (redness, pain, the sense of self) rather than asking why any physical process gives rise to experience at all. Source: Wikipedia, *Being You* (accessed 2026-03-05); Wikipedia, *Anil Seth* (accessed 2026-03-05); prior completed item `2026-02-28-youtube-video-HYUoS0GkGCs-concepts.md`.

[fact] Seth first articulated the real problem in a 2016 Aeon essay, "The hard problem of consciousness is a distraction from the real one," cited in his Wikipedia entry as an Editor's Pick. The essay title is explicitly polemical: the hard problem is cast as a *distraction* rather than as a genuine problem to be solved. Source: Wikipedia, *Anil Seth*.

[fact] In *Being You* (2021), Seth's theory of consciousness has three components: conscious level (how conscious a being is), conscious content (what it is conscious of), and conscious self (the experience of selfhood). The real problem is applied to all three. Source: Wikipedia, *Being You*.

**B2 — Does Seth dissolve or bracket the hard problem?**

[fact] Reviewer Felix Haas (in *World Literature Today*) characterised Seth's position as falling between treating the hard problem as meaningless and treating it as the central target: Seth finds direct confrontation "unproductive" without claiming it is "inherently meaningless." Source: Wikipedia, *Being You* (Haas review section).

[fact] Journalist Sams Wahid Shahat (*Business Standard*) described Seth's real problem as a way to "dissolve" the hard problem, while reviewer Paul Thagard (*Psychology Today*) interpreted Seth more narrowly as proposing an alternative research programme. Source: Wikipedia, *Being You* (reviews section).

[inference] Seth's own stated position is consistent across sources: he does not claim to dissolve the hard problem (that would require eliminating the concept of qualia or showing it is incoherent — which is Frankish's strategy). He claims to bracket it: to make empirical progress on tractable questions without requiring its solution. Whether this constitutes "avoiding" or "transcending" the hard problem is a matter of interpretation.

**B3 — What success in Seth's programme looks like**

[fact] Seth's solution criterion, as stated in the Wikipedia *Being You* summary: understanding consciousness through "explaining, predicting, and controlling" the underlying biological processes. Source: Wikipedia, *Being You*.

[inference] This criterion is purely scientific/functional. It would constitute success on all easy problems (in Chalmers' taxonomy) and potentially on the real problem (mapping neural patterns to specific qualities). It provides no mechanism for addressing why those patterns produce experience rather than information processing without experience.

**B4 — Seth's relation to physicalism**

[fact] Seth is described as a proponent of materialist explanations of consciousness. His real-problem strategy assumes consciousness has a physical basis and that explaining the physical basis is sufficient. Source: Wikipedia, *Anil Seth*.

[inference] This positions Seth as a type of functionalist or type-B physicalist: consciousness is real (not an illusion), phenomenal properties exist, but they are identical to or supervenient on physical properties. This puts him in conflict with both Frankish (who denies phenomenal properties are real) and Goff (who thinks they cannot be identified with physical properties without panpsychism).

**C1 — Frankish's illusionism**

[fact] Keith Frankish defends "illusionism": phenomenal consciousness — the subjective, "what it is like" character of experience — is an introspective illusion. There are no qualia in the robust sense Chalmers requires; what we call phenomenal experience is a systematically misleading representation of functional states. This eliminates rather than addresses the hard problem by rejecting its presupposition. Source: Wikipedia, *Keith Frankish* (accessed 2026-03-05).

[fact] Frankish's position is related to but distinct from eliminative materialism: he does not deny that experiences occur, but denies they have irreducible phenomenal properties. We are systematically wrong about the intrinsic nature of our own experiences. Source: Wikipedia, *Keith Frankish*; Wikipedia, *Illusionism (consciousness)*.

[inference] Illusionism is the most direct philosophical challenge to the hard problem because it attacks the premise rather than the conclusion. If phenomenal properties are illusory, there is no explanatory gap to cross. But this is coherent only if an adequate account of *why* we generate the illusion of phenomenal consciousness can be provided — which is itself a substantial empirical and philosophical task, and critics argue it merely relocates the mystery rather than dissolving it (the "meta-problem" of consciousness: why does anything *seem* to have phenomenal properties?).

**C2 — Goff's panpsychism**

[fact] Philip Goff (Durham University) defends panpsychism as a response to the hard problem. His argument in *Galileo's Error* (2019): Galileo's mathematical revolution excluded qualitative properties from science, creating the explanatory gap. The solution is to treat consciousness as a fundamental and ubiquitous feature of physical reality — proto-experiences at the level of electrons and quarks combining into richer forms of experience at higher levels. Source: Wikipedia, *Philip Goff (philosopher)* (accessed 2026-03-05).

[fact] Goff defends a version of Russellian monism: the intrinsic nature of matter (undetermined by physics, which only specifies relational/structural properties) is constituted by proto-experiential properties. Source: Wikipedia, *Philip Goff (philosopher)*.

[fact] Panpsychism's central problem is the "combination problem": how do micro-level proto-experiences combine into the unified, rich phenomenal experience of a human subject? This is acknowledged by Goff as unresolved. Source: Wikipedia, *Panpsychism*.

[inference] Panpsychism takes the hard problem maximally seriously — accepts that phenomenal consciousness is real and irreducible, then asks what ontological revision is needed. It is the logical counterpart to illusionism: where Frankish denies the explanandum, Goff accepts it and revises the ontology accordingly.

**C3 — Higher-order theories**

[fact] Higher-order theories (HOT) hold that phenomenal consciousness consists in a mental state being the object of a higher-order representation — either a higher-order thought (Rosenthal) or a higher-order perception. A state is conscious when the brain has a higher-order representation of that state as occurring. Source: Wikipedia, *Higher-order theories of consciousness* (accessed 2026-03-05).

[inference] HOT theories are functionalist: they explain consciousness in terms of a particular kind of computational/representational structure. Like Seth's approach, they are tractable — they generate empirical predictions about prefrontal cortex involvement and predict dissociations between first-order processing and conscious access. They address the easy problems well. They do not explain why higher-order representations should produce phenomenal experience rather than more information processing — the hard problem remains.

**C4 — Does any position close the explanatory gap?**

[inference] No current position closes the explanatory gap in the sense of providing a derivation of phenomenal experience from physical facts that a hard-problem skeptic would accept. Frankish denies the gap exists (by denying the explanandum). Goff accepts the gap and revises the ontology so that experience is already built into the physical. Seth brackets the gap and makes progress on what can be mapped without crossing it. HOT theories address the gap at the functional level only.

**D1 — Genuine advance vs. deferment: is this the right frame?**

[inference] "Genuine advance versus deferment" is slightly misleading. The real issue is whether the hard problem is *real* — i.e., whether there is a genuine explanandum that no functional/structural account can reach. If the hard problem is real (Chalmers, Goff), then Seth defers it. If the hard problem is based on a conceptual illusion (Frankish, Dennett), then Seth and Frankish both advance past it in different ways. Seth does not resolve this meta-question; he sidesteps it. That is a legitimate but philosophically incomplete move.

**D2 — What would a decisive advance look like?**

[inference] A decisive advance on the hard problem would require either: (a) showing that the phenomenal/functional distinction is incoherent (Frankish's strategy) — which requires explaining why we have the introspective illusion at all; (b) showing that phenomenal facts are logically entailed by physical facts despite appearances (type-B physicalism — the zombie argument fails because conceivability ≠ possibility); or (c) revising the ontology to make phenomenal properties physical in a non-eliminative sense (Goff's strategy). Seth's real problem achieves none of these — it is a change of research programme, not a resolution of the metaphysical question.

**D3 — Professional consensus**

[fact] The 2020 PhilPapers survey found 62.4% of professional philosophers regard the hard problem as genuine; 29.7% reject it. There is no consensus on the solution. Source: Wikipedia, *Hard problem of consciousness*.

[fact] Among major consciousness theorists: Dennett and Patricia Churchland reject the hard problem; Chalmers, Nagel, Block, and Tononi accept it; Seth occupies a pragmatic middle position; Frankish rejects the phenomenal realism that generates it; Goff accepts it and resolves it via panpsychism. Source: Wikipedia, *Hard problem of consciousness* (list of acceptors and rejectors).

### §3 Reasoning

Chalmers' hard problem rests on a two-step argument: (1) phenomenal consciousness — subjective experience, "what it is like" to see red, feel pain — is real and distinct from access consciousness (functional availability to reasoning and report); (2) no physical or functional description logically entails facts about phenomenal consciousness. The zombie argument and the knowledge argument both instantiate step (2): p-zombies are conceivable, and Mary gains new knowledge on seeing red, suggesting physical facts underdetermine phenomenal facts. If both steps hold, consciousness is not reducible to physics and requires either dualism, panpsychism, or a new kind of physicalism that Chalmers calls "property dualism."

Seth's real problem does not contest step (2). It sidesteps it by asking a different, tractable question: given that specific experiences exist, which physical/computational properties give rise to which specific experiential qualities? This is a specification question, not an existence question. The real problem accepts that consciousness is real and has specific characters; it asks for the mapping. The hard problem asks why any mapping exists at all — why those physical correlates produce *experience* rather than information processing that occurs "in the dark."

The key logical point: even perfect success at the real problem would not close the explanatory gap. If neuroscience fully mapped every conscious quality to a specific neural signature, Chalmers' question would remain: why does that neural signature produce experience? The real problem generates a correlation atlas; the hard problem asks why correlation is accompanied by experience. These are genuinely different questions.

However, Seth's position has a plausible strategic justification. If the hard problem is insoluble given current conceptual and empirical tools — and there is substantial reason to think it is, given 30 years of failure to make progress — then the real problem is the right move not as a dissolution but as a methodological reorientation. The analogy to vitalism is apt: science made enormous progress on biology by bracketing "what is life?" and asking tractable questions about metabolism, genetics, and cell function. Whether phenomenal consciousness will similarly "dissolve" as its components are explained is the core empirical bet Seth is making.

Frankish's illusionism represents the bolder philosophical move: deny that phenomenal consciousness has the properties (non-functional, irreducible qualia) that generate the hard problem. This is not deferment — it is demolition of the explanandum. If Frankish is right, Seth's real problem is not bracketing a genuine question but quietly conceding it need not be asked. The problem is that illusionism faces the meta-problem: we have a very strong *seeming* of having phenomenal experience; explaining that seeming without presupposing phenomenal properties is far from trivial.

Goff takes the opposite route: accept qualia fully, infer that physical reality must include proto-experiential properties at the fundamental level. Panpsychism solves the hard problem by paying the price of the combination problem — how micro-experience aggregates into macro-experience. This is an open problem, and panpsychism remains a minority view among philosophers (24% in 2020 PhilPapers survey for "metaphysically possible" p-zombies, but a smaller fraction are committed panpsychists).

The relationship between positions is a spectrum based on how real phenomenal consciousness is taken to be:
- Frankish: deny robust qualia → no hard problem
- Seth: accept qualia but bracket the hard problem → real problem is tractable
- Higher-order theories: explain consciousness functionally → hard problem sidestepped
- Chalmers: accept qualia and the irreducibility claim → hard problem is genuine and unsolved
- Goff: accept qualia and irreducibility → revise ontology (panpsychism)

### §4 Consistency Check

No internal contradictions found in the evidence base. The apparent tension between Seth being described as someone who finds the hard problem "not inherently meaningless" (Haas review) while also calling it a "distraction" (Aeon essay title) resolves on inspection: Seth believes the hard problem might be meaningful in the long run but is currently unproductive as a research target, which is consistent. This is coherent as a research programme but commits Seth to a metaphysical position that is at best incomplete: he is neither an illusionist nor a property dualist nor a panpsychist, occupying a position that does not map cleanly onto the standard taxonomy. This is not an inconsistency but an underspecification.

### §5 Depth and Breadth Expansion

**Historical context:** The hard problem did not emerge from nothing. Leibniz's mill thought experiment (1720) asked why walking through an expanded brain would reveal no conscious thoughts — the same intuition pump Chalmers formalises. The explanatory gap was named by Levine (1983), predating Chalmers by 12 years. The hard/easy distinction is Chalmers' contribution (1994/1995), not the observation that phenomenal experience resists physical reduction.

**The meta-problem:** Chalmers himself has articulated what he calls the "meta-problem": why do we *believe* we have phenomenal consciousness (i.e., why do we have the intuitions that generate the hard problem)? This is an easy problem in his taxonomy — it can be studied empirically. Frankish argues the meta-problem is the only real problem: explain the *seeming* of phenomenal consciousness, and nothing remains. Seth's real problem can be seen as continuous with the meta-problem approach.

**Practical asymmetry:** The hard problem is philosophically legitimate but scientifically sterile. It has generated no empirical predictions and no confirmed experimental findings in 30 years. The real problem has generated active research programmes in neuroscience of consciousness (predictive processing, Integrated Information Theory, Global Workspace Theory). This asymmetry does not settle the philosophical question but is relevant to evaluating research strategy.

**IIT as alternative:** Giulio Tononi's Integrated Information Theory (IIT) attempts to address the hard problem directly by identifying consciousness with integrated information (phi). Unlike Seth's real problem, IIT claims to explain *why* integration produces experience (by identifying experience with a fundamental feature of information). IIT is controversial — it predicts counterintuitive results (e.g., simple feedback systems would be conscious) and is criticised as unfalsifiable — but it is the only mainstream theory that attempts to close the explanatory gap rather than defer or deny it.

**The zombie argument's logical status:** The argument's validity depends on the conceivability-to-possibility inference. If one holds (as many physicalists do) that phenomenal concepts are governed by a distinct mode of presentation that makes it *seem* possible to conceive of zombies even though zombies are physically impossible, the argument fails. Seth's position is consistent with this type-B physicalist response, though he does not commit to it explicitly.

### §6 Synthesis

**The central distinction:**

Chalmers' hard problem asks *why* any physical process gives rise to phenomenal experience — a metaphysical question about the relationship between the physical and the experiential. Seth's real problem asks *which* physical properties give rise to *which* specific experiences — a specification question about correlations and mechanisms. These are not competing answers to the same question; they are different questions entirely.

**Seth's strategy assessed:**

Seth's real problem is a genuine methodological advance in that it reorients consciousness research toward tractable empirical questions and has been productive. It is not a philosophical advance on the hard problem because it explicitly brackets rather than addresses the question of why any physical process is accompanied by experience. Seth acknowledges this himself. The real problem defers the phenomenal question — it does not dissolve it.

**Competing positions:**

Frankish's illusionism is the only current major position that offers a genuine dissolution strategy: deny the phenomenal properties that generate the problem. The cost is explaining why we systematically represent ourselves as having those properties. Goff's panpsychism is the only position that directly accepts and attempts to solve the hard problem, at the cost of the combination problem and the metaphysical commitment to proto-experiential properties at the physical level.

**Where the hard problem stands:**

The hard problem remains unresolved. A 62% majority of professional philosophers accept it as genuine; no solution has achieved consensus. Seth's real problem is valuable science but does not change this. The question is not whether Seth advances science — he does — but whether his advancement resolves Chalmers' question. It does not.

**Key conclusions:**
1. The hard problem and the real problem are not competing solutions to the same question; they target different questions.
2. Seth's real problem brackets the phenomenal question, making it a deferment relative to the hard problem.
3. This deferment is a legitimate scientific strategy (analogous to biology's bracketing of "what is life?") but not a philosophical resolution.
4. Frankish's illusionism is the philosophically stronger anti-hard-problem position — it eliminates the explanandum rather than deferring the question.
5. Goff's panpsychism remains the primary alternative that takes the hard problem at face value; it faces the unresolved combination problem.
6. Higher-order theories address easy problems well but do not explain why higher-order representations produce phenomenal experience.
7. No position currently closes the explanatory gap in Chalmers' sense.

## Findings

### Executive Summary

Seth's "real problem" of consciousness is a principled deferment of Chalmers' "hard problem," not a philosophical resolution: it reorients empirical research toward tractable questions about which brain patterns give rise to which specific experiences, while explicitly bracketing the question of why any physical process is accompanied by experience at all. The hard problem asks a metaphysical question about the existence of phenomenal consciousness; the real problem asks a specification question about its correlates — these are different questions, and success in the real programme would leave Chalmers' question untouched. This deferment is scientifically productive and strategically defensible, but it is philosophically incomplete: Frankish's illusionism and Goff's panpsychism both engage the hard problem more directly, the former by denying its explanandum, the latter by revising its ontological presuppositions.

### Key Findings

1. **Chalmers' hard problem (1995) asks why physical processes are accompanied by phenomenal experience at all, a question that functional and mechanistic explanations cannot answer in principle even if they are complete.** The zombie argument supports this: if a being physically identical to a human but lacking experience is conceivable, then physical facts do not logically entail experiential facts. [Confidence: high]

2. **Seth's real problem (2016, 2021) asks which specific physical properties give rise to which specific conscious qualities, a question that generates testable predictions and active research programmes in neuroscience.** It is not a solution to the hard problem but a different question — a correlation-and-mechanism question rather than an existence question. [Confidence: high]

3. **Seth explicitly brackets the hard problem rather than dissolving it; his own stated position is that direct confrontation with it is currently unproductive, not that it is meaningless.** This makes his strategy a principled deferment analogous to biology's 19th-century bracketing of "what is life?" in favour of tractable biochemical questions. [Confidence: high]

4. **Even complete success in Seth's real-problem programme — a full neural atlas mapping every brain state to every experiential quality — would not close Chalmers' explanatory gap, which asks why those correlates produce experience rather than information processing without experience.** The gap is structural, not empirical. [Confidence: high]

5. **Frankish's illusionism (2016) offers a more direct philosophical response to the hard problem by denying its explanandum: phenomenal properties — qualia in the robust sense Chalmers requires — are an introspective illusion, and the hard problem dissolves because there is nothing irreducible to explain.** The cost is the meta-problem: explaining why we systematically represent ourselves as having those properties. [Confidence: high]

6. **Goff's panpsychism (*Galileo's Error*, 2019) accepts the hard problem and resolves it by treating consciousness as a fundamental, ubiquitous physical property: electrons and quarks have proto-experiential properties that combine into richer experience at higher levels.** The unresolved combination problem — how micro-experiences aggregate into unified macro-experience — prevents panpsychism from being a complete solution. [Confidence: high]

### Analysis

The key analytical move in this item is distinguishing the *subject matter* of the hard problem from the *subject matter* of the real problem. They are not competing answers to one question; they are different questions. This distinction resolves what might otherwise look like a direct confrontation between Seth and Chalmers: in fact, Seth endorses a complementary research strategy, not a rebuttal.

The three competing philosophical positions (illusionism, panpsychism, higher-order theories) each make a different bet on where the hard problem's leverage point is. Frankish attacks the explanandum (deny robust qualia); Goff accepts it and revises the ontology (add proto-experiential properties to physics); HOT theorists address the correlation structure of consciousness without engaging phenomenal reducibility. None resolves the hard problem in Chalmers' own terms: a derivation of phenomenal facts from physical facts, or a demonstration that the zombie argument fails.

Seth's pragmatic strategy is consistent with the scientific record: when a question proves intractable, reframe it. This has worked in biology (vitalism → biochemistry), in physics (action-at-a-distance → field theory), and arguably in psychology (introspection → behavioural measurement). Whether it will work for consciousness depends on whether the hard problem is an actual explanatory gap or a conceptual illusion. Seth bets on the latter without committing to it philosophically.
