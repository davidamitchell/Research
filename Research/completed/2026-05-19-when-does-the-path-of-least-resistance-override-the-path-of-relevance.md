# When Does the Path of Least Resistance Override the Path of Relevance?

## Research Question

When formal expertise pathways are harder to use than nearby informal pathways, under what conditions does the lowest-effort route become dominant and persist even when it lowers knowledge quality?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Trade-offs between effort minimisation and authoritative, context-fit knowledge in day-to-day knowledge seeking.
- Conditions that make informal pathways sticky, including proximity, speed, trust, and social comfort.
- Organisational consequences of persistent low-relevance routing.

**Out of scope:**
- Redesign of specific enterprise software products.
- Individual performance-management policy design.

**Constraints:** Prioritise studies that compare formal and informal pathways in real organisational settings.

## Context

Low-effort knowledge routes become organisationally important when workers can reach them faster, more safely, and with less interpretation burden than the formally preferred route, because pathway choice is a live coordination problem rather than a neutral search process. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html]

## Approach

1. Map the main drivers that make low-effort pathways more attractive than high-relevance pathways.
2. Assess how repeated shortcut usage changes network norms and knowledge quality over time.
3. Identify design levers that raise relevance without sharply increasing effort.

#### A. What makes a nearby informal path win at the moment of choice?

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428] Borgatti and Cross propose and test a formal model in which the probability of seeking information from another person depends on knowing what that person knows, valuing what that person knows, gaining timely access to that person's thinking, and perceiving that seeking information from that person would not be too costly.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428] The same study reports strong support for the model and for the mediation of physical proximity through knowing, access, and cost variables, with weaker support only for one generic undifferentiated cost variable.
- [fact; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] Menon and Pfeffer report that managers often value external knowledge more highly than internal knowledge because learning from internal competitors carries different status implications and internal knowledge is more exposed to scrutiny.
- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] The adjacent activation-energy item shows that help-seeking stalls when expertise is hard to identify, hard to reach, or socially risky to ask for.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html] A nearby informal route overrides a formally preferred route when it makes expertise easier to identify, answer latency easier to predict, and asking safer from status or competence costs before the worker has to test answer quality directly.

#### B. Why do informal paths persist after initial success?

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075] Cross and Sproull find that personal information relationships provide five components of actionable knowledge: solutions, referrals, problem reformulation, validation, and legitimation.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536] Tsai reports that formal hierarchical structure, in the form of centralization, has a significant negative effect on knowledge sharing, while informal lateral relations, in the form of social interaction, have a significant positive effect on knowledge sharing among units that compete with one another for market share.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183] Edmondson defines psychological safety, a shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk taking, and both Edmondson and Frazier show that it is associated with learning behavior, voice, information sharing, and performance-relevant outcomes.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1177/1059601103258439] Bryant reports that a peer mentor training course increased perceived peer mentor knowledge and skills, and that higher perceived peer mentoring was associated with higher perceived knowledge creation and sharing in a software firm.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075; https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183; https://doi.org/10.1177/1059601103258439] Informal pathways persist because repeated personal exchanges provide not only fast answers but also validation, referral, legitimation, and social reassurance, which makes the same ties more attractive on the next request and shifts local norms toward reuse of those ties.

#### C. When does persistence lower knowledge quality?

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032] Hansen finds that weak interunit ties help a project team search for useful knowledge in other subunits but impede the transfer of complex knowledge, which tends to require a strong tie between the two parties to a transfer.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032] Hansen also finds that weak interunit ties speed projects when transferred knowledge is not complex but slow projects when the knowledge to be transferred is highly complex.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] Szulanski finds that major barriers to internal knowledge transfer are the recipient's lack of absorptive capacity, the recipient's ability to recognise, assimilate, and apply incoming knowledge, causal ambiguity, unclear understanding of why a practice works, and an arduous relationship between source and recipient rather than motivation alone.
- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-formal-governance-structures-distort-cross-department-knowledge-flows.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-asset-specificity-and-information-asymmetry-block-knowledge-transfer.html] Adjacent completed items in this repository show that formal boundaries and asset-specific knowledge amplify the same transfer problem, because local shortcuts can solve search cheaply while still failing to transfer context-rich practice accurately.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-formal-governance-structures-distort-cross-department-knowledge-flows.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-asset-specificity-and-information-asymmetry-block-knowledge-transfer.html] The lowest-effort route lowers knowledge quality when the task needs cross-boundary, tacit, or causally ambiguous knowledge, because the cheap route is good enough to resolve a routine local question but not rich enough to transfer authoritative practice safely.

#### D. Which interventions raise relevance without sharply increasing effort?

- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428] Borgatti and Cross imply that good intervention targets are expertise visibility, access predictability, and perceived cost of asking, because those variables directly shape whether information seeking starts at all.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183] Edmondson and Frazier show that psychologically safe team conditions and supportive leader behaviors increase learning behavior and information sharing rather than only subjective satisfaction.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1177/1059601103258439] Bryant provides direct field evidence that peer mentoring is associated with higher perceived knowledge creation and sharing.
- [fact; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-align-strategic-relevance-with-low-effort-knowledge-pathways.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-what-are-the-micro-transaction-costs-of-internal-knowledge-sourcing.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-formal-governance-structures-distort-cross-department-knowledge-flows.html] Adjacent completed items argue that visible expertise maps, predictable access routines, and governance by exception lower several request costs at once, while universal vertical mediation can raise them.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183; https://doi.org/10.1177/1059601103258439; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-formal-governance-structures-distort-cross-department-knowledge-flows.html] A well-supported low-effort relevance bundle includes visible expertise routing, predictable low-risk access, peer mentoring, psychologically safe escalation, and formal hierarchy focused on ownership and exception handling rather than on routine clarification.

### §3 Reasoning

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] Direct entry-stage evidence covers visibility, access, and social meaning, because Borgatti and Cross model the request decision directly and Menon and Pfeffer show that perceived status cost changes which source is even considered attractive.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075; https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536] Direct persistence evidence covers relational value rather than generic habit, because personal sources offer validation and legitimation while lateral social interaction predicts more knowledge sharing than centralization does.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] Direct quality-risk evidence is clearest when the knowledge required is complex, tacit, or ambiguous, because the search stage and the transfer stage follow different relational requirements.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] The synthesis therefore supports a conditional answer rather than a blanket anti-informal answer: low-effort informal routes can be locally useful, but they become quality-lowering when the apparent convenience of the route hides a mismatch between the relationship used and the complexity of the knowledge required.

### §4 Consistency Check

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075] Cross and Sproull show that personal information relationships can improve short-term problem solving through referrals, validation, and problem reformulation, so it would overstate the evidence to treat all informal pathways as low-quality by definition.
- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] Hansen and Szulanski show that quality risk rises when the task requires strong transfer conditions, especially for complex or ambiguous knowledge, so it would also overstate the evidence to claim that low-effort routes are harmless for all task types.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105] The apparent contradiction resolves when local usefulness is separated from organisational relevance: an informal route can feel more useful in the moment while still underperforming on accuracy, authority, or transfer depth for cross-boundary and high-complexity tasks.
- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183] No unresolved contradiction remains once persistence is treated as a joint effect of structural cost, lateral legitimacy, and interpersonal safety rather than as a single variable.

### §5 Depth and Breadth Expansion

**Behavioural lens**

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference] Help-seeking is a socially interpreted choice as well as an information choice, because visibility of expertise matters only when the worker also expects the interaction to be worth the interruption and safe from negative status meaning.

**Network lens**

- [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032] Network structure shapes different parts of the problem differently, because lateral interaction improves sharing overall while weak ties are better for search than for complex transfer.

**Organisational lens**

- [inference; source: https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-formal-governance-structures-distort-cross-department-knowledge-flows.html; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-what-are-the-micro-transaction-costs-of-internal-knowledge-sourcing.html] Organisations accidentally train low-relevance routing when they make the authoritative path expensive at the point of request, because workers learn from repeated cost outcomes more quickly than they learn from diffuse future quality losses.

**Design lens**

- [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1177/1059601103258439] Design should focus on lowering entry costs and preserving strong-transfer options at the same time, which means combining visible routing with low-risk first contact and stronger relational support for high-complexity cases.

### §6 Synthesis

#### Executive Summary

The path of least resistance overrides a more relevant knowledge pathway when workers can identify, access, and use a nearby informal source faster and with less social risk than the formal route, especially when the task appears routine enough that local validation seems sufficient. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html]
Those routes persist because personal relationships deliver referrals, problem reformulation, validation, legitimation, and social reassurance in addition to fast answers, while centralised formal pathways can add waiting time and extra handoffs. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075; https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999]
Knowledge quality falls when the same low-effort route is used for complex, tacit, or cross-boundary knowledge, because weak or low-intensity ties help search but do not transfer complex practice well and recipients still need absorptive capacity and relationship quality. [fact; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105]
The practical remedy is to make the authoritative path nearly as cheap as the shortcut through visible expertise routing, predictable low-risk access, peer mentoring, and psychologically safe escalation, while reserving hierarchy for ownership and exception handling rather than routine mediation. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183; https://doi.org/10.1177/1059601103258439; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-formal-governance-structures-distort-cross-department-knowledge-flows.html]

#### Key Findings

1. **Low-effort informal routes dominate when workers can identify a nearby source quickly, expect timely access, and avoid the scrutiny or status risk attached to the formal pathway before answer quality is even tested.** ([inference]; medium confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html)
2. **Informal pathways persist because personal information relationships provide referrals, problem reformulation, validation, and legitimation as well as answers, which makes repeated local reuse rational for the seeker even when a formal repository exists.** ([inference]; medium confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075; https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999)
3. **Centralization and mandatory vertical routing reduce knowledge sharing between units while lateral social interaction increases it, so official pathways lose behavioural share when formal control adds waiting time, handoffs, or exposure costs.** ([inference]; medium confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-formal-governance-structures-distort-cross-department-knowledge-flows.html)
4. **The cheapest route lowers knowledge quality when the task requires complex, tacit, or cross-boundary knowledge, because weak ties help search but do not transfer complex practice well and recipients still need absorptive capacity and low ambiguity.** ([fact]; high confidence; source: https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032; https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.4250171105)
5. **Informal asking is especially likely to outrank formal expertise pathways when internal requests are socially expensive, because internal knowledge can carry more scrutiny and status exposure than outsider or local alternatives.** ([inference]; medium confidence; source: https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference; https://davidamitchell.github.io/Research/research/2026-05-19-how-do-activation-energy-barriers-shape-knowledge-seeking-behaviour.html)

#### Analysis

The evidence supports a conditional explanation rather than a generic complaint about shortcuts. Informal routes win first because they lower request-stage costs, and they remain attractive because the same relationships supply validation and legitimation, not only a fast answer. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.1040.0075]

One rival explanation is that formal systems lose because their content is poor. That account is too narrow, because Borgatti and Cross show that access and perceived cost matter in addition to knowing who knows what, and Menon and Pfeffer show that social exposure can make a formally available source unattractive before quality is considered. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/working-papers/valuing-internal-vs-external-knowledge-explaining-preference]

Another rival remedy is to strengthen quality by adding more vertical review or more mandatory routing. Tsai and Hansen do not support that as a general solution, because centralization suppresses sharing overall and weak ties plus formal routing still fail when the transfer problem itself is complex. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.13.2.179.536; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032]

The evidence supports a two-capability design: a cheap entry path for routine clarification, and stronger relational or expert-transfer mechanisms for ambiguous, tacit, or cross-boundary work. Visible expertise, psychologically safe first contact, and peer mentoring fit that evidence, whereas content-only fixes or more hierarchy alone do not address the full mechanism. [inference; source: https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.49.4.432.14428; https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999; https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12183; https://doi.org/10.1177/1059601103258439; https://doi.org/10.2307/2667032]
