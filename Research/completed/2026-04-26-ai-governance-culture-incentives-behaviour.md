# How do organisational incentives, culture, and behaviour influence adherence to governance in AI and low-code environments?

## Research Question

How do organisational incentives, culture, and behaviour influence adherence to governance in Artificial Intelligence (AI) and low-code environments, specifically, what conditions drive teams to bypass governance controls, creating shadow Information Technology (IT) and ungoverned automations, and what governance design choices and cultural conditions produce durable compliance rather than formal compliance with behavioural circumvention?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- The mechanism by which incentive structures, such as delivery velocity targets, individual productivity metrics, and reward systems, drive circumvention of governance controls
- Shadow Information Technology (shadow IT) dynamics in the context of AI and low-code, what conditions produce ungoverned tool adoption and automation creation, and what evidence exists on the scale and risk profile of shadow Artificial Intelligence (shadow AI) and low-code
- The relationship between governance complexity and circumvention rate, including whether more complex governance produces higher rates of workaround behaviour
- Cultural and leadership conditions associated with durable governance compliance versus formal-but-hollow compliance
- The role of change management, training, and governance communication in adoption
- Empirical evidence from technology governance research on the behavioural response to governance controls

**Out of scope:**
- Technical enforcement mechanisms, covered by Q3, because this item focuses on behavioural response to governance rather than the controls themselves
- Maturity model stages, covered by Q13, because this item provides the behavioural input to the maturity model
- Regulatory compliance framing, covered by Q15, because the focus here is internal organisational behaviour

**Constraints:**
- Distinguish evidence-based claims from management-consulting assertions about culture
- Prefer empirical evidence, surveys, case studies, incident analyses, and peer-reviewed literature over prescriptive frameworks
- Use sources that address AI and low-code governance where possible, while allowing general IT-governance behaviour research as secondary evidence

## Approach

1. **Shadow IT and shadow AI evidence review:** Review empirical research on shadow IT prevalence, risk profile, and causal conditions; extend to AI and low-code tools where evidence exists. Assess whether AI and low-code shadow adoption have a different risk profile from traditional shadow IT.
2. **Incentive structure analysis:** Analyse how common enterprise incentive structures, such as sprint velocity targets, individual productivity measurement, and cost-centre pressure, create the conditions for governance circumvention, using principal-agent and behavioural-response evidence where available.
3. **Governance complexity and circumvention:** Review evidence on the relationship between governance process complexity and workaround behaviour.
4. **Cultural conditions for durable compliance:** Review evidence on what organisational cultural characteristics, such as open communication, leadership behaviour, perceived legitimacy of governance, and training, are associated with genuine rather than performative compliance.
5. **Design principles for behaviour-aware governance:** Based on the evidence, identify governance design principles that reduce the incentive to circumvent, such as fast-lane approval for low-risk use cases and self-service with guardrails.
6. **Synthesis:** Produce an analysis of the behavioural risks to governance adherence and a set of evidence-based design principles for governance programmes that account for organisational behaviour.

#### A. What recurring root drivers produce shadow behaviour

- [fact; source: https://jitm.ubalt.edu/XXX-4/article1.pdf] The 2019 practitioner-perceptions study compares 29 chief information officers and senior Information Technology managers with shadow-IT literature and identifies three cross-cutting themes across causes, outcomes, and governance responses: poor business-IT alignment, lack of agility, and lack of policies.
- [fact; source: https://jitm.ubalt.edu/XXX-4/article1.pdf] In that same study, business-IT non-alignment appears in 48% of interviews, while "more agility" appears in 76% of governance-response mentions and system modernisation appears in 90%, which indicates that practitioners see capability remediation as central to reducing shadow behaviour rather than as a secondary improvement.
- [fact; source: https://www.sciencesphere.org/ijispm/archive/ijispm-070102.pdf] The 2019 systematic literature review says slow responsiveness to requests, long development times, and lengthy procurement processes are symptoms of Information Technology organisation slowness, and that this lack of agility fosters the emergence of shadow IT and business-managed Information Technology.
- [fact; source: https://www.sciencesphere.org/ijispm/archive/ijispm-070102.pdf; https://jitm.ubalt.edu/XXX-4/article1.pdf] The same shadow-IT literature also identifies employee motivation and peer behaviour, lack of restrictions, and lack of awareness as recurring contributing factors, which means bypass behaviour is not purely a friction story even when agility gaps are the dominant pattern.
- [fact; source: https://www.sciencesphere.org/ijispm/archive/ijispm-070102.pdf] The same review says shadow-IT and business-managed-Information-Technology use can be reduced if existing system shortcomings are addressed to fulfil unmet needs, while also warning that technical monitoring can be bypassed by employees.
- [fact; source: https://d-nb.info/1270139835/34] The low-code adoption review says organisations face strong pressure to digitalise rapidly, face a significant shortage of skilled Information Technology developers, and adopt low-code development platforms to reduce hand-written code, reduce time to market, and increase productivity.
- [fact; source: https://d-nb.info/1270139835/34] That same review also says low-code platforms can induce risks, including inexperienced developers neglecting security standards, the emergence of shadow Information Technology, and scaling and maintenance difficulties.
