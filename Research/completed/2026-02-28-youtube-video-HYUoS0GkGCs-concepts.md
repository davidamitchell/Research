## Findings

### Executive Summary

Anil Seth argues that conscious experience — including both the perception of the external world and the sense of self — is a "controlled hallucination": an active, top-down construction by the brain, continuously constrained by sensory signals rather than a direct readout of reality. The mechanism is predictive processing: the brain perpetually generates predictions about the likely causes of its sensory input, propagating prediction errors upward when those predictions are wrong. The self is not a fixed observer but an ongoing inference grounded in interoceptive (bodily) signals. Crucially, Seth sidesteps the classical "hard problem" of consciousness in favour of what he calls "the real problem" — characterising *which* physical/computational properties give rise to *which* types of conscious experience — arguing this is both scientifically tractable and philosophically honest. Five concepts warrant deep-dive investigation: controlled hallucination, predictive processing, the hard vs. real problem, interoception and the predictive self, and consensus vs. subjective reality.

### Key Findings

1. **Perception is generative, not receptive.** The brain never directly accesses the world; it infers the most probable external cause of its sensory signals. Perception is the brain's best-guess prediction, corrected by error signals — not a veridical record.
2. **Hallucination and perception are mechanistically identical.** Both involve the same top-down generative process; the difference is only how tightly sensory error-correction constrains the guess. "Controlled hallucination" collapses the perceptual/hallucinatory distinction.
3. **The self is an interoceptive inference.** The felt sense of being a body — including emotions, affect, and selfhood — arises from predictive processing of internal (bodily) signals, not just external ones. Disrupting interoceptive prediction produces depersonalisation, anxiety, and related disorders.
4. **"Objective reality" is intersubjective agreement.** When multiple brains' hallucinations converge on the same model, that convergence is what we call shared reality. Objectivity is an emergent property of coordinated prediction, not access to a mind-independent world.
5. **Seth reframes, not dissolves, the hard problem.** He proposes "the real problem": explain why specific neural correlates produce specific conscious qualities. This does not answer why *any* physical process produces experience, but it makes the question experimentally tractable.
6. **Psychiatric conditions as predictive failures.** Conditions like schizophrenia, depression, and anxiety can be modelled as maladaptive prediction regimes — priors that are too rigid, too weak, or improperly weighted by error signals.

### Analysis

The talk sits at the intersection of philosophy of mind, cognitive neuroscience, and phenomenology. Seth's central move is to treat the "hard problem" (Chalmers 1995) as epistemically premature: before asking why physical processes produce *any* consciousness, we should characterise the specific physical correlates of specific conscious states. This pragmatic deflation is controversial — philosophers like Chalmers consider it question-begging — but it maps directly to experimental programmes.

The controlled hallucination framing is most powerful in unifying ordinary perception, dreaming, hallucination, and illusion under one mechanism. Its weakest point is explaining the apparent transparency of perception: most experiences do not *feel* like guesses, which Seth attributes to the suppression of prediction error in successful prediction.

Interoception is the conceptual bridge between the external-perception story and the self story. Seth's claim that selfhood is a survival-oriented inference about bodily homeostasis is more contentious than the external-world predictive-processing account and opens genuine research questions about the minimal conditions for selfhood.

### Risks, Gaps, and Uncertainties

- The real-problem strategy does not touch the phenomenal-consciousness question; Seth's critics argue it merely defers rather than resolves the hard problem.
- "Controlled hallucination" as a label may mislead: it implies more certainty about the mechanisms of actual hallucination than the evidence currently supports.
- The intersubjective account of objectivity raises questions Seth does not fully address: what anchors shared hallucinations to a stable external world?
- Friston's free energy principle (the mathematical backdrop) is mathematically contested; simplifications in the talk may not survive contact with the full formalism.

### Open Questions

- Does predictive processing fully account for phenomenal consciousness, or only for its functional/cognitive aspects? → deep-dive: hard vs. real problem
- How is interoceptive inference distinguished from (and integrated with) proprioception and exteroception? → deep-dive: interoception and the predictive self
- What are the implications of the "consensus reality" account for epistemology and scientific realism? → deep-dive: consensus vs. subjective reality
- How does the free energy principle cash out in empirically testable predictions about consciousness? → deep-dive: predictive processing and active inference
