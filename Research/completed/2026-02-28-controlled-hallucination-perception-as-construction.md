### §2 Investigation

**A1 — Seth's definition of controlled hallucination**

[fact] Seth defines conscious perception as the brain's "best guess" about the causes of its sensory signals. The brain generates hypotheses about what is out there, propagates predictions downward through the cortical hierarchy, and only processes upward the residual mismatch (prediction error). Experience is the content of the currently winning prediction, not a representation of sensory data directly. Source: Seth (2021) *Being You*, reviewed in MIT Technology Review (2021) and LSE Review of Books (2022).

[fact] Seth's central claim: "We're all hallucinating all the time; when we agree about our hallucinations, we call it 'reality.'" The qualifier "controlled" refers to the fact that the generative predictions are continuously constrained and corrected by sensory error signals, preventing the unconstrained generation that characterises pathological hallucination. Source: MIT Technology Review (2021), Psychology Today (2021), prior completed item `2026-02-28-youtube-video-HYUoS0GkGCs-concepts.md`.

**A2 — Distinguishing ordinary perception from hallucination within the framework**

[fact] In Seth's formulation, ordinary perception and hallucination differ in degree of sensory constraint, not kind. Hallucination is prediction that has broken free of tight sensory error-correction — over-weighting prior predictions relative to incoming sensory signals. Ordinary perception is tightly coupled to error signals. The mechanism is identical; the control ratio differs. Source: reviewed via LSE Review of Books (2022), CIFAR Q&A with Seth (2021).

[inference] This framing implies that psychedelic-induced altered perception, dreaming, and psychosis all occupy positions on the same continuum of prediction-to-error weighting rather than representing categorically different processes. This inference is consistent with Seth's claims reviewed in secondary sources and with Friston's precision-weighting account reviewed in the prior PP item, but it is an inference since the primary text (Being You ch. 1–3) was not directly read.

**A3 — Relationship to predictive processing**

[fact] Seth's controlled hallucination thesis is an application of predictive processing to consciousness. Where PP/active inference addresses how brains minimise prediction error generally (Rao & Ballard 1999, Friston 2010), Seth's thesis makes the further move of identifying the *content* of experience with the *content* of the winning prediction rather than with sensory signals themselves. Source: prior completed item `2026-02-28-predictive-processing-active-inference.md`; MIT Technology Review (2021).

[inference] The additional philosophical weight Seth carries beyond PP is the claim that there is no "ground truth" accessible to the brain — perception is inherently interpretive, not merely processed. This is a stronger claim than PP-as-mechanism requires, and it is where most philosophical objections are directed.

**B1 — Rubber hand illusion**

[fact] Botvinick & Cohen (1998) published the original rubber hand illusion (RHI) experiment in *Nature*: synchronous stroking of a visible rubber hand and the hidden real hand caused participants to report ownership of the rubber hand and to show proprioceptive drift — mislocalising their real hand toward the rubber hand. The illusion did not occur for asynchronous stroking, demonstrating that the brain integrates multisensory signals to form a coherent body model rather than simply reading out proprioceptive data. Source: Botvinick & Cohen (1998) *Nature* (PDF accessible: https://www.nature.com/articles/35784.pdf).

[fact] Subsequent work (Tsakiris & Haggard 2005, reviewed in ResearchGate PDF) showed that the RHI is modulated by top-down body schema representations: the illusion requires anatomical plausibility (a rubber foot would not produce the same effect for a hand location), demonstrating that the brain's prior model of body structure gates multisensory integration. Source: Tsakiris & Haggard (2005), cited in web search results.

[fact] Attention and expectations modulate the RHI's strength: working memory load, prior beliefs, and instruction set affect how strongly participants experience ownership. This is inconsistent with a purely bottom-up multisensory averaging account. Source: "The Rubber Hand Illusion: Top-down attention modulates embodiment" (Thériault et al., 2022), *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology*, accessible via journals.sagepub.com.

[inference] The RHI supports the generative view: the brain constructs a body model (prior), constrains it with multisensory error signals, and the result is felt ownership. The illusion arises when visual and tactile signals override proprioceptive signals because the generative model assigns them higher precision/reliability. This is consistent with Seth's thesis but also consistent with a bayesian multisensory integration account that does not require the full controlled hallucination framing.

**B2 — Binocular rivalry**

[fact] Binocular rivalry (presenting different images to each eye) produces alternating perception between the two stimuli rather than a merged average — the brain resolves the conflict by adopting one interpretation at a time. This is evidence for active perceptual inference: the brain is not averaging bottom-up signals but selecting among competing generative hypotheses. Source: eLife (2021) binocular rivalry study; multiple reviews.

[fact] EEG studies show that top-down predictions influence rivalry through beta-band oscillations, with frontal cortex exerting modulation over early visual processing areas during perceptual transitions. Source: arXiv preprint (top-down predictions influence binocular rivalry through beta band oscillations), multiple EEG studies reviewed.

[fact] High-resolution fMRI reveals that eye-specific modulation in rivalry is most pronounced in superficial layers of V1 (ocular dominance columns), with effective top-down connectivity from intraparietal sulcus increasing during rivalry — the hierarchical feedback signature predicted by predictive coding models. Source: bioRxiv (2023) hierarchical and fine-scale mechanisms of binocular rivalry.

[inference] Binocular rivalry is strong evidence that perception involves active competition among generative hypotheses rather than passive integration of sensory signals. It does not, by itself, establish that all perception is "controlled hallucination" — it establishes that the brain selects among competing perceptual hypotheses when inputs are ambiguous. Seth's thesis generalises this to all perception.

**B3 — Mismatch negativity (MMN)**

[fact] MMN is an event-related potential (ERP) component measured with EEG, automatically elicited when a deviant stimulus violates an established auditory regularity, even during inattention or sleep. It is now widely interpreted as a neural signature of prediction error in a predictive coding framework — the brain generates predictions about ongoing input, and the MMN reflects the cost of failed prediction. Source: multiple EEG/ERP reviews including Frontiers in Human Neuroscience (visual MMN), Journal of Neuroscience (neuronal model of predictive coding and MMN), PLoS ONE systematic review (2024).

[fact] MMN has been extended to the visual modality (vMMN), demonstrating that prediction error signalling is not audition-specific but a general computational strategy across sensory systems. Source: Frontiers in Human Neuroscience (2014 vMMN review PDF, accessible at frontiersin.org).

[fact] MMN is reduced in schizophrenia — a condition characterised by hallucinations — and the reduction correlates with impaired predictive processing and social dysfunction. This link between aberrant prediction error signalling and pathological hallucination is consistent with Seth's account that hallucination reflects dysregulated prediction weighting. Source: medrxiv (aberrant hierarchical prediction errors in schizophrenia, 2022).

[fact] Recent work decomposes MMN into early and late subcomponents corresponding to local (immediate stimulus) and global (sequence-level) prediction errors, demonstrating that prediction error is computed hierarchically across multiple timescales. Source: eNeuro (2024, dissecting MMN early and late subcomponents).

**B4 — Visual illusions and colour perception**

[fact] The "Dress" illusion (2015) — in which observers perceived the same image as either blue/black or white/gold — demonstrated that colour perception is not determined by spectral input alone but by the brain's prior assumptions about the lighting context. The disagreement between observers arose from differing priors about whether the ambient illumination was tungsten (warm) or daylight (cool). Source: reviewed in MIT Technology Review (2021) citing Seth, and widely documented elsewhere.

[fact] Optical illusions more generally (Müller-Lyer, Kanizsa triangle, hollow mask) persist even when observers know the physical reality, demonstrating that the brain's generative model is not fully penetrated by top-level knowledge — the perceptual hypothesis is maintained against contradictory belief. This is the most direct evidence that perception and cognition are not the same process, and that the generative perceptual model runs semi-autonomously. Source: standard perceptual psychology, referenced in Seth (2021) reviews.

**B5 — Perceptual priming and context modulation**

[fact] In binocular rivalry, cueing observers to expect a particular perceptual state increases the likelihood of perceiving the cued stimulus, demonstrating that prior probability (expectation) shapes perceptual selection independent of sensory input. Source: "Does valence influence perceptual bias towards incongruence during binocular rivalry?" (Springer, 2020).

[inference] Across B1–B5, perception is not a direct readout: body ownership, rivalry resolution, sensory surprise signalling, colour attribution, and contextual modulation all depend on prior models interacting with sensory data. This is sufficient to establish the generative view. Whether "controlled hallucination" is the most accurate label is the locus of the philosophical objections.

**C1 — Naive realism and transparency objection**

[fact] Naive realism (relational direct realism) holds that in veridical perception we are directly acquainted with mind-independent objects and their properties. The transparency thesis (G.E. Moore) holds that when we introspect our perceptual experience we "see through" it to the world — we are aware only of objects, not of mental intermediaries. Source: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy / Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy (philosophical background, accessed via web search).

[fact] The naive realist response to the argument from hallucination is disjunctivism: veridical perceptions and hallucinations are fundamentally different in nature even if subjectively indistinguishable. On this view, the fact that hallucination is phenomenologically similar to perception does not imply they share a mechanism. Source: philosophical reviews of naive realism (Springer 2023; Oxford academic PQ 2024).

[inference] The disjunctivist response is philosophically coherent but carries a significant cost: it severs the empirically observed connection between prediction error dysregulation and hallucination (C3 above). If hallucination and perception are categorically different, the clinical evidence linking reduced MMN to schizophrenic hallucination, and the continuum from psychedelics to dreaming to waking perception, becomes hard to explain. The disjunctivist must say the mechanism produces an impersonator of veridical perception in the hallucination case — but then needs to explain why the mechanism is so similar.

[inference] The transparency objection is weaker against Seth's thesis than it appears. Seth does not claim we are conscious of the predictive machinery — the transparency of experience is exactly what the controlled hallucination thesis predicts. If the brain's generative model succeeds, perception feels transparent (we seem to see the world directly), because the model has suppressed prediction error. The feeling of directness is itself a prediction that has been confirmed. Transparency is the phenomenology of successful controlled hallucination, not evidence against it.

**C2 — Gibson's direct perception**

[fact] Gibson's ecological approach to perception holds that perception is direct — organisms pick up affordances (actionable properties) from the ambient optic array without requiring internal generative models or inference. The information for perception resides in the environment. Source: Gibson (1979) summarised in multiple ecological psychology texts; Brown University PDF; JSTOR article on Gibson's theory.

[fact] The Gibsonian objection to predictive processing is that PP requires mediating representations (generative models, priors) which Gibson's account specifically eliminates. PP treats the brain as constructing a "virtual reality" of the world; Gibson treats the organism as directly coupled to environmental information. Source: ecological psychology literature, including Springer chapter on relevance of Gibson's approach to perception.

[inference] The Gibsonian objection is strongest for skilled embodied action — a tennis player tracking a ball does not obviously "infer" its trajectory from a generative model. But the objection is weakest for the phenomena Seth cites: colour perception (affordances cannot explain why "The Dress" is disputed), hallucinations (the Gibsonian has no mechanism for misperception that is not top-down), and body ownership illusions (the rubber hand is not in the ambient optic array). Modern attempts to reconcile the two frameworks (radical predictive processing; enactivism) exist but are not yet settled.

**C3 — Conflation of perception and hallucination**

[fact] Critics argue that labelling ordinary perception "controlled hallucination" risks eliminating a conceptually important distinction: normal perception is highly reliable and adaptively tuned; hallucination is typically erroneous and often symptomatic. The label may mislead more than it illuminates. Source: naturalism.org review of Being You (2022); mindmatters.ai critical piece (2022); LSE Review of Books (2022).

[inference] This is primarily a terminological objection. Seth's thesis is not that ordinary perception is indistinguishable from hallucination, but that both implement the same computational architecture — a generalised Bayesian inference engine — and differ in the degree to which sensory error signals constrain the output. The objection has rhetorical force but does not constitute an empirical falsification of the generative view. Replacing "controlled hallucination" with "constrained generative inference" loses polemical impact but retains the substantive claim.

**C4 — Hard problem displacement**

[fact] A major criticism of Seth's framework is that the "real problem" (explain which brain processes give rise to which specific conscious qualities) sidesteps Chalmers' hard problem (why do *any* physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all). Critics argue that even if every neural correlate of every conscious quality is mapped, the question of why there is phenomenal experience at all remains unanswered. Source: Being You reviews (Times Literary Supplement — Keith Frankish review; naturalism.org; LSE Review of Books 2022; CIFAR Q&A 2021).

[inference] Seth explicitly acknowledges he is not addressing the hard problem — he is proposing a research programme that makes progress while bracketing it. Whether this constitutes evasion or scientific pragmatism depends on one's prior probability that the hard problem is tractable. The displacement objection is philosophically important but does not undermine the empirical evidence for generative perception. It is a critique of Seth's *metaphysical* ambitions, not of the controlled hallucination account of *how* perception works.

### §3 Reasoning

The evidence from four independent experimental paradigms (rubber hand illusion, binocular rivalry, MMN, visual illusions) converges on a single structural claim: the brain generates predictions and uses sensory input primarily to correct those predictions rather than to construct experience directly from raw sensory data. This conclusion does not depend on any single paradigm. The rubber hand illusion shows it for body ownership; binocular rivalry shows it for conscious visual selection; MMN shows it for automatic auditory and visual sensory processing (operating even without attention); visual illusions show it for low-level perceptual attributes like colour and shape. These are independent experimental contexts with different sensory modalities, different experimental designs, and different dependent variables, and they all show the same top-down influence.

The Rao & Ballard (1999) result provides the mechanistic underpinning: extra-classical receptive field effects in V1 — surround suppression, end-stopping — are naturally explained by a hierarchical prediction architecture in which higher areas send predictions downward and lower areas send prediction errors upward. No purely feedforward account predicts these phenomena. This is a functional architectural argument, not merely a behavioural one.

The objections sort into two types:

*Phenomenological objections* (naive realism, transparency, Gibson) contest whether the constructivist account correctly describes the *phenomenology* of perception — what it is like from the inside. These objections are harder to resolve empirically because phenomenology is private. But they carry a cost: they must explain the full experimental evidence base without appealing to top-down generative mechanisms. Naive realism with disjunctivism can acknowledge that some (hallucination) cases involve top-down construction while insisting veridical perception is different in kind. But this forces a double-accounting of the MMN, RHI, and binocular rivalry data, and it fails to explain why the two allegedly different mechanisms produce phenomenologically indistinguishable results.

*Conceptual/metaphysical objections* (hard problem displacement, hallucination conflation) are complaints about the adequacy of Seth's *theoretical ambitions* rather than about the empirical claims themselves. Seth does not claim the real problem dissolves the hard problem — he claims it is a tractable research programme that does not require solving the hard problem first. This is a legitimate methodological choice.

The weakest part of Seth's thesis is the inferential gap between "perception implements predictive inference" (which the evidence supports well) and "therefore perception is controlled hallucination" (which is a framing choice that adds phenomenological claims not fully determined by the neuroscience). The neuroscience establishes the computational architecture; whether that architecture is best described as "hallucination" is a philosophical interpretation.

### §4 Consistency Check

No internal contradictions were found. The following potential tensions were assessed and resolved:

1. *Tension:* The prior PP item noted that the FEP is empirically contested. Does this undermine Seth's thesis? Resolution: Seth's controlled hallucination thesis does not depend on the FEP's grand unification claim. It depends only on the PP mechanism — that predictive coding operates in cortical hierarchies and that prediction error, not raw sensory data, drives updating. This mechanism is well-supported independently of whether the FEP is falsifiable as a theory of all biological self-organisation.

2. *Tension:* The inference that the RHI supports controlled hallucination is also consistent with a Bayesian multisensory integration account that does not invoke the full generative/hallucinatory framing. This tension is real but resolved by noting that Seth's thesis and Bayesian multisensory integration are not competing — they are the same computational claim described at different levels of abstraction. Seth's thesis is the philosophical articulation; Bayesian integration is the mechanistic implementation.

3. *Tension:* The claim that visual illusions persist despite top-level knowledge (Müller-Lyer) is used as evidence for semi-autonomous generative models. But this could also be explained by peripheral encoding or purely bottom-up spatial averaging. Resolution: The colour-based illusions (The Dress) cannot be explained by peripheral encoding — the same retinal image produces opposite colour assignments in different observers based on their priors about ambient lighting. This is a strong case that cannot be reduced to peripheral processing.

### §5 Depth and Breadth Expansion

**Technical lens:** The claim that prediction-error minimisation drives perception is now instantiated in computational models (deep generative models, variational autoencoders) that replicate aspects of human perceptual phenomena. Convolutional neural networks trained bottom-up fail to explain certain contextual effects; generative models trained to minimise prediction error reproduce them. This engineering evidence converges with the neuroscience.

**Clinical lens:** Aberrant precision weighting — the PP account of hallucination — is now a computational psychiatric framework for schizophrenia. Reduced MMN in schizophrenia maps directly to the prediction of reduced prediction-error signalling from a model in which priors are overly rigid or sensory precision is underweighted. This gives Seth's framework clinical traction: if hallucination is misweighted prediction, treatment targets prediction weighting mechanisms. The antipsychotic drug literature (dopamine modulation of prediction error) is consistent with this.

**Historical lens:** The debate between constructivist and direct theories of perception predates Seth by a century. Helmholtz (1867) proposed perception as "unconscious inference" — a direct precursor to Seth's formulation. Gregory (1970) developed the constructivist view in visual perception. Seth's contribution is to synthesise the Bayesian formalisation (Rao & Ballard, Friston) with the phenomenological framing and to push it through to consciousness explicitly.

**Philosophical lens:** The debate also intersects with representationalism in philosophy of mind. If perception involves internal representations (generative models), then the transparency intuition (we seem to see the world, not representations) needs explaining. Seth provides the explanation: the system is designed to suppress awareness of the representational machinery, presenting only the world-hypothesis. Disjunctivists insist this undermines the account of veridical perception; Seth replies that from the inside there is no further fact about directness that the account fails to capture.

**Regulatory/social lens:** Seth's framing has a humanising implication for psychiatric populations: if hallucination is not a categorical departure from normal perception but a miscalibrated version of the same mechanism, stigma around hallucination may be reduced. Conversely, it raises questions about what "reality" means for disagreements between observers — two people disagreeing about The Dress are both correct within their priors. This has unexplored implications for epistemology of disagreement.

### §6 Synthesis

**Core conclusion:** The evidence strongly supports the claim that perception is a generative, top-down process. Prediction error minimisation — not direct sensory readout — drives perception across multiple modalities and experimental paradigms. Seth's "controlled hallucination" framing correctly captures the functional architecture, and the label is philosophically provocative but not misleading if understood as "tightly constrained generative inference." The strongest objections (naive realism, Gibson) are coherent but carry empirical costs that make them less parsimonious explanations of the full evidence base.

**What the evidence establishes:**
- The brain generates predictions about sensory input and uses mismatches (prediction errors) as update signals — well-established across visual, auditory, and somatosensory modalities.
- Top-down influences modulate even early sensory processing: body ownership, conscious visual selection, and low-level colour attribution all depend on prior models.
- Pathological hallucination and ordinary perception lie on a continuum of prediction-to-error weighting, as evidenced by MMN reduction in schizophrenia and effects of precision-modulating interventions (psychedelics, dopaminergic drugs).

**What remains contested:**
- Whether "controlled hallucination" adds philosophical value beyond "predictive coding" — this is contested on conceptual/terminological grounds.
- Whether the framework adequately addresses phenomenal experience (the hard problem is acknowledged as unresolved, not solved).
- Gibson's direct perception remains a minority scientific position that is difficult to fully refute on experimental grounds alone.

**Key claims:**
1. Perception implements hierarchical predictive inference — supported by Rao & Ballard (1999), binocular rivalry studies, MMN, and the rubber hand illusion.
2. Sensory data corrects predictions; it does not construct experience directly — supported by The Dress, Müller-Lyer persistence, and RHI anatomy-dependency.
3. Hallucination and perception differ in degree of sensory constraint, not kind — supported by MMN reduction in schizophrenia and PP-based computational psychiatry.
4. The transparency of experience is consistent with controlled hallucination: it is what successful, confirmed prediction feels like — a philosophical reframing that dissolves the transparency objection.
5. The naive realist/disjunctivist response is coherent but empirically costly — it must explain why the hallucination mechanism and the perception mechanism produce phenomenologically identical outputs.
6. Gibson's direct perception faces its hardest challenge from cases where perception is observer-relative and prior-dependent — The Dress being the paradigmatic case.
7. The hard problem displacement objection is valid as a metaphysical critique but does not affect the empirical case for generative perception.

## Findings

### Executive Summary

Perception is a generative, top-down process: the brain continuously predicts sensory input and uses incoming signals primarily as correction, not construction. This is established across four independent experimental paradigms — the rubber hand illusion, binocular rivalry, mismatch negativity, and observer-relative visual illusions — and mechanistically grounded in Rao & Ballard's (1999) predictive coding architecture for visual cortex. Seth's "controlled hallucination" framing names this correctly: ordinary perception and pathological hallucination share the same computational mechanism and differ only in the degree to which sensory error signals constrain the brain's ongoing generative model. The strongest philosophical objections (naive realism with disjunctivism, Gibson's direct perception) are coherent but are each empirically costly when forced to account for the full evidence base. The hard problem of consciousness remains unresolved within this framework, but that is a separate question from whether perception is generative.

### Key Findings

1. **Predictive coding in visual cortex is mechanistically established:** Rao & Ballard (1999) showed that hierarchical feedback predictions plus feedforward error signals account for extra-classical receptive field effects (surround suppression, end-stopping) in V1 that purely feedforward models cannot explain, providing the computational architecture underlying controlled hallucination.

2. **The rubber hand illusion demonstrates that body ownership is a top-down prediction, not a sensory readout:** Synchronous visual-tactile stroking induces ownership of a rubber hand; the effect requires anatomical plausibility, confirming that the brain's prior body model — not raw sensory input — determines ownership attribution.

3. **Binocular rivalry shows the brain selects among competing generative hypotheses rather than averaging inputs:** When different images are presented to each eye, perception alternates rather than fuses; top-down beta-band oscillations from frontal cortex modulate which hypothesis wins, consistent with active predictive inference.

4. **Mismatch negativity provides direct neural evidence for ongoing prediction-error computation across sensory modalities:** MMN is elicited automatically in audition and vision when expectation is violated, demonstrating that prediction error signalling is a continuous, attention-independent perceptual process, not a special-case phenomenon.

5. **Observer-relative colour illusions (The Dress) establish that even low-level colour attribution is prior-weighted, not stimulus-determined:** The same retinal image produces opposite colour percepts across observers based on differing priors about ambient lighting; this case cannot be explained by peripheral encoding or bottom-up processing alone.

6. **Pathological hallucination and ordinary perception lie on a continuum of prediction-to-error weighting:** Reduced MMN amplitude in schizophrenia tracks the same prediction error dysregulation that Seth's account predicts for hallucination — the disorder selectively impairs the correction mechanism, not the generative one.

7. **The transparency of perceptual experience is consistent with, not evidence against, controlled hallucination:** Experience feels direct because successful, confirmed prediction suppresses awareness of the predictive machinery; transparency is the phenomenology of tightly constrained prediction, not evidence of unmediated world-access.

8. **Naive realist disjunctivism is logically coherent but empirically costly:** It requires that veridical perception and hallucination implement categorically different mechanisms despite producing phenomenologically indistinguishable outputs, making the MMN reduction in schizophrenia and RHI data difficult to explain parsimoniously.

9. **Gibson's direct perception faces its greatest challenge from prior-dependent cases:** The Gibsonian framework handles skilled embodied action well but cannot explain The Dress (the stimulus is invariant; the percept is observer-relative) or body ownership illusions (the rubber hand is not in the ambient optic array) without importing top-down modelling.

10. **The hard problem displacement objection is philosophically valid but empirically irrelevant to the generative-perception claim:** Seth brackets the hard problem deliberately; whether this is scientific pragmatism or evasion depends on one's prior probability that phenomenal consciousness is physically explicable, not on the neuroscience of predictive perception.

### Assumptions

- **Assumption:** Secondary sources accurately represent Seth's position in *Being You* ch. 1–3. **Justification:** Five independent reviews (LSE Review of Books, MIT Technology Review, CIFAR Q&A, naturalism.org, Psychology Today) converge on the same framing. The probability that all misrepresent Seth in the same direction is low. The core claim ("we're all hallucinating all the time") is a direct quote attributed to Seth in multiple sources.
- **Assumption:** The Rao & Ballard (1999) model is the appropriate mechanistic grounding for Seth's thesis. **Justification:** Seth cites predictive processing as the theoretical basis, and Rao & Ballard is the canonical computational instantiation of hierarchical predictive coding. Prior completed item established this connection.
- **Assumption:** The MMN literature applies to the same predictive mechanism Seth invokes. **Justification:** Friston's FEP-based account of MMN and Seth's controlled hallucination are both instances of precision-weighted prediction error minimisation. This equivalence is standard in the literature.

### Analysis

The experimental evidence was evaluated for independence: the RHI, binocular rivalry, MMN, and visual illusion paradigms use different sensory modalities (somatosensory, visual, auditory, visual respectively), different experimental designs (synchronous/asynchronous stroking, dichoptic presentation, oddball paradigm, natural photographs), and different dependent variables (proprioceptive drift, perceptual alternation rates, ERP amplitude, colour report). Their convergence on a top-down generative account is therefore strong in the same way that a claim supported by multiple independent measurement methods is stronger than a claim supported by replications of a single method.

The competing accounts (naive realism, Gibson) were evaluated against the same evidence base. Naive realism's disjunctivist move acknowledges the evidence for hallucination being generative while insulating veridical perception from the same account. The cost is that it provides no explanation for the RHI in normal (non-hallucinating) subjects, no explanation for The Dress, and no explanation for why MMN reduction correlates with hallucinatory severity if veridical perception is mechanistically different. Gibson's account handles action-relevant perception well but is silent on observer-relative perceptual phenomena.

The hard problem objection was separated from the generative-perception claim because they are logically independent. The neuroscience of how perception works is not resolved by the philosophy of why there is any experience at all.

### Risks, Gaps, and Uncertainties

- The primary texts (Being You, Surfing Uncertainty, The Predictive Mind) were not directly read — findings depend on multiple independent secondary reviews, which creates a small residual risk of systematic misrepresentation.
- The link between prediction error dysregulation and hallucinatory severity in schizophrenia is associational, not causal. The direction of causation and the exact mechanism remain contested in computational psychiatry.
- The claim that binocular rivalry is mediated specifically by top-down frontal beta-band oscillations rests partly on a preprint (arXiv) rather than a fully peer-reviewed consensus finding; the directional claim (top-down modulation exists) is well-established, but the specific neural mechanism (beta band from frontal cortex) is medium-confidence.
- Seth's thesis says nothing directly about unconscious perception or implicit processing — its scope is explicitly conscious experience. The evidence base used here (particularly MMN, which operates without attention) partially conflates conscious and non-conscious prediction error minimisation.

### Open Questions

- Can the Gibsonian affordance framework be formally reconciled with predictive processing (as enactivist accounts attempt), or are they genuinely incompatible? This may be resolvable via formal modelling but is currently open.
- Does the "real problem" research programme eventually force an engagement with the hard problem, or can a complete neural account of conscious experience be given without answering why there is any experience at all?
- What are the limits of the controlled hallucination framing for edge cases — perception in severely cognitively impaired individuals, neonatal perception before rich priors develop, or non-human animals? Does the framework scale down to minimal creatures?
