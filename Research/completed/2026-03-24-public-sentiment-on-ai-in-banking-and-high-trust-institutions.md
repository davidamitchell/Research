# Public sentiment on AI in banking and high-trust institutions

## Research Question

What does current (2024–2025) survey data reveal about customer sentiment toward Artificial Intelligence (AI) in banking and high-trust Financial Services (FS) institutions — in Australia, across Asia-Pacific (APAC), and globally — and would a two-plane architecture (a "production plane" that augments human employees, and an "operational plane" that minimises direct AI involvement in binding customer-financial-data decisions) represent a viable, trust-differentiated approach that can be clearly communicated to customers?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Customer sentiment surveys from 2024–2025 covering AI in banking and FS
- Australian-specific data (Publicis Sapient, KPMG/University of Melbourne)
- APAC regional data (F5/Twimbit, FICO, EPAM Continuum, Edelman)
- Global benchmarks (Accenture, Qualtrics Experience Management (XM) Institute, Edelman Trust Barometer)
- Regulatory context: Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) Cross-industry Prudential Standard (CPS) 230, Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) AI guidelines, Association of Banks in Singapore (ABS) Generative AI (Gen AI) Guardrails Handbook
- Architecture viability: production plane (human augmentation) vs. operational plane (AI guardrails on binding financial decisions)
- Communication strategies for trust differentiation

**Out of scope:**
- Detailed technical implementation of specific AI/Machine Learning (ML) models
- Non-financial-services industries (healthcare, education) except where trust comparison is relevant
- Vendor-specific product recommendations
- Retail banking product design

**Constraints:**
- Publicly available survey data and reports only
- English-language sources
- Published 2024–2026

## Context

The 2024 Publicis Sapient Customer Banking Report found that 96% of Australian banking customers have reservations about their banks using AI; within that group, 58% prefer speaking to a human bank employee, 54% worry about job losses, and 49% are concerned about data security and privacy ([Publicis Sapient 2024](https://knowledge.publicissapient.com/01/news-publicis-sapient-customer-banking-report-press-release.html)).

At the same time, AI investment in banking is accelerating. McKinsey projects that banks scaling AI could achieve 60% or greater productivity gains in some processes ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/financial-services/our-insights/extracting-value-from-ai-in-banking-rewiring-the-enterprise)). The tension between customer anxiety and institutional investment creates the central research question: can an organisation articulate an architecture that addresses both the efficiency imperative and the trust deficit?

The proposed two-plane model, a production plane where AI makes human employees more effective and an operational plane that minimises direct AI involvement in systems where customer financial data drives binding decisions, is the specific architectural hypothesis tested in this item.

## Approach

1. **Q1 — Australian baseline:** Gather the Publicis Sapient 2024 data and the KPMG/University of Melbourne 2025 Trust in AI study for Australia-specific sentiment.
2. **Q2 — APAC regional sentiment:** Collect data from EPAM Continuum, F5/Twimbit, FICO APAC, and Edelman Trust Barometer on APAC-wide customer attitudes.
3. **Q3 — Global benchmarks:** Use the Accenture 2025 Global Banking Consumer Study (49,300 respondents, 39 countries) and Qualtrics XM Institute 2025 data for global comparison.
4. **Q4 — Regulatory alignment:** Assess whether the two-plane model aligns with APRA CPS 230, MAS AI guidelines, and ABS Gen AI Guardrails.
5. **Q5 — Architecture viability:** Evaluate whether existing evidence supports "human augmentation + AI guardrails on binding decisions" as a trust-building strategy, and what communication requirements exist.

### §3 Reasoning

**Facts established:**

1. **[fact]** Australian AI trust is among the lowest globally: 36% are willing to trust AI in the KPMG/University of Melbourne 2025 study, while 96% report reservations about AI in banking in Publicis Sapient 2024.
2. **[fact]** APAC sentiment is split: China and India are materially more optimistic about AI adoption, while Australia and Japan are materially more sceptical, so regional averages hide country-level divergence.
3. **[fact]** Globally, only 26% of consumers trust organisations to use AI responsibly (Qualtrics XM Institute 2025), while financial services remains the most trusted financial-services sector at 64% overall trust (Edelman 2025).
4. **[fact]** Customer concerns cluster around three themes: loss of human connection, job displacement, and data privacy or security, and those concerns recur across Australian, APAC, and global sources.
5. **[fact]** Acceptance is higher for routine and lower-stakes AI tasks such as support triage, payments assistance, and balance monitoring than for binding financial decisions such as advice, underwriting, and approvals.
6. **[fact]** Regulatory frameworks from Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA), Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS), and the Association of Banks in Singapore (ABS) all require stronger human oversight as AI risk rises, aligning with the operational-plane concept.
7. **[fact]** Direct experience is a major trust driver: Edelman 2025 reports that trust rises by roughly 40-50 points when Generative AI (Gen AI) helps users understand complex financial topics.

**Inferences drawn:**

1. **[inference]** The 96% Australian reservations figure, combined with the 36% trust figure, indicates an unusually wide trust-use gap: Australians use AI at meaningful levels but remain structurally wary of its banking applications.
2. **[inference]** A two-plane architecture is viable because it matches both the observed task-level acceptance pattern and the regulatory requirement for stronger controls on high-risk uses.
3. **[inference]** Differentiation value depends less on inventing a unique architecture than on making the control boundary legible to customers through visible human review, clear language, and better service outcomes.

**Assumptions made:**

1. **[assumption]** Customer sentiment surveys from 2024–2025 remain directionally valid through 2026.
2. **[assumption]** "Binding financial decisions" can be operationally defined and separated from "augmentation tasks" in a real banking architecture.
3. **[assumption]** Customers will attribute trust value to an explicit articulation of the two-plane separation, rather than treating it as unremarkable boilerplate.

### §4 Consistency Check

- **[fact]** Potential contradiction 1: EPAM reports 96% satisfaction among banking customers who have used AI tools, while Publicis Sapient reports that 96% of Australian banking customers have reservations about banks using AI.
- **[inference]** Resolution 1: these figures describe different populations rather than conflicting outcomes; high satisfaction among the minority with direct experience can coexist with broad reservations among the majority without experience.

- **[fact]** Potential contradiction 2: APAC is described as both more positive about AI overall and as having persistent trust deficits around AI in banking.
- **[inference]** Resolution 2: regional averages are being pulled upward by high-trust markets such as China and India, while lower-trust markets such as Australia and Japan produce a very different local reality.
